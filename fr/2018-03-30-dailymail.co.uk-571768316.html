‘I snuck onto Concorde after its 1976 maiden voyage'

Rare pictures have emerged of Concorde in a hangar at London Heathrow after it returned from its maiden flight to Bahrain in the Middle East in 1976.

What makes them extra remarkable is that they were taken by someone who snuck into the hangar dressed as an engineer.

In her disguise, Rosalind Griffiths, who was 20 at the time, excitedly posed next to the history-making supersonic airliner and snapped the cockpit and cabin.

Ms Griffiths, who's now 62 and retired, explained to MailOnline Travel that she arrived on the back of a motorcycle in British Overseas Airways Corporation apprentice overalls and that security waved her through to see what the public has never seen until now.

Ms Griffiths, from Oxfordshire, said: 'My friend Bob who worked on DC-10s and Concorde offered to sneak me into British Airways so I could see the famous plane and I leapt at the chance.

'He donated to me his old green BOAC apprentice overalls and assured me that the security would wave us on through, as in those days it was near impossible to carry a bomb on a motorcycle, then sure enough I was there!

'I don't know how long I was fixed to the spot just staring in awe but spotting a set of steps I got out of my overalls and climbed up for a photo.

'I ran my hand along the wing thinking most aeroplanes have joins with pop rivets and dull paintwork, but this was a perfect seamless finish with a high gloss.'

THE HISTORY OF CONCORDE 

Concorde first flew in 1969 for 20 minutes, was commercialised in 1976 and was grounded on April 11, 2003.

It halved airtime by getting passengers to New York from London in less than three and a half hours, a jaw-dropping reduction from the usual seven or eight hours.

In 2000, Air France flight 4590 caught fire on take-off after debris on the runway punctured a fuel tank. It crashed, killing 109 on-board and four on the ground.

The day before the disaster, BA revealed there were hairline cracks in the wings of all seven of its Concordes.

Air France and British Airways subsequently grounded Concorde due to low passenger numbers and rising maintenance costs. 

Earlier this year Club Concorde announced it had raised the funds to purchase and restore a Concorde aircraft with the aim of returning it to service by 2019, to coincide with the 50th anniversary of the aircraft's inaugural test flight.
