Fund management industry ordered to wake up to its lack of competition

Fund managers have been told to prove they offer clients value for money under new rules from the financial services regulator aimed at safeguarding investors' interests.

The Financial Conduct Authority said firms should demonstrate they offer value through their funds on an annual basis after finding evidence of 'weak' price competition across the fund management industry.

The watchdog has also introduced a requirement for firms to appoint two independent directors to boards to increase scrutiny, ensuring they account for 25 per cent of the board, and said firms must do more to move clients into cheaper versions of funds.

Thursday's announcement marked the culmination of a two and a half year investigation into the asset management industry which found evidence that some investors could have been paying over the odds in fees and charges.

The rules are important for more than three quarters of the UK population who either invest directly or have an invested pension pot.

Higher fees charged by fund managers to look after client's money can eat away at people's savings with the FCA claiming there was a 'significant amount of potential harm to be addressed'.

Christopher Woolard, executive director of strategy and competition at the FCA said its asset management market study had found weak price competition in a 'number of areas'.

He added the new rules aimed to create a fairer and more transparent industry.

'The investment choices open to people, and the decisions they make on how to invest, can have a profound impact on their financial health. They can also have consequences for their families, as well as society as a whole. That's why it is important the asset management industry, which looks after the savings of millions of investors, is working as well as possible,' he said.

Central to the new rules is the strengthening of fund manager's duty to act in the best interests of their investors.

Fund managers have been given 18 months to implement the new rules, which includes assessing the value for money for investor and explaining the assessment in an annual report published publicly.

The FCA's Christopher Woolard (pictured) said: 'The investment choices open to people, and the decisions they make on how to invest, can have a profound impact on their financial health.'

Firms must also appoint independent directors to the boards of funds to ensure extra scrutiny of decisions that affect clients. Even smaller start-up firms must field the cost of paying new directors as the FCA said 'the challenge independent directors bring is particularly important in a firm's formative years when its strategy and culture are set'.

The FCA has published a second paper with proposals to make it clear to investors what a fund aims to do, how it does it and how performance is shown to help investors choose the right products.

Christopher Cummings, director of the fund industry body the Investment Association, welcomed the update.

He said: 'Our industry is committed to demonstrating, and delivering, good value to the millions of people who entrust their savings to us. We welcome the FCA recognising that people judge their asset manager by investment performance and service, as well as cost.

'We strongly support a greater emphasis on communication as well as governance to help customers better understand what they are investing in, what they are paying for and what they are getting in return.'

PriceWaterhouseCooper boss Andrew Strange said the FCA had found a 'pragmatic balance' in its rules, but warned some consumers could be overwhelmed with the amount of information they will now be given on fees, performance and benchmarks.

He added: 'As always, however, the challenge for the industry will be the breadth and diversity of funds, strategies, firms and approaches covered by this set of rules.

'These changes will also pose some risk for firms in the future, as they will need to be even more vigilant in how they use benchmarks in marketing materials and represent fund performance when no benchmarks are used.'
