You slam insurer Ageas for refusing family whose home burnt down

Snubbed: Paul and Sophie Weldin with children Daisy, 10, Woody, 8, Albie aged, 5, and Dexter, 5

Insurer Ageas faces a backlash after refusing to pay out to a family whose home was gutted by fire.

Industry experts and Money Mail readers criticised Ageas after we told how the Weldins' £460,000 claim was denied in a dispute over the number of bedrooms at the house.

They described the decision, based on a technicality, as 'irrational' and called on the firm to reconsider.

One expert has written to Ageas chief executive Andy Watson asking him to ensure the firm lives up to its pledge to 'treat customers fairly'.

And Money Mail readers have offered cash to help the Weldins meet their £460,000 bill and move back home — believing the family has suffered an injustice at the hands of Ageas, which last year made a £25 million profit in the UK.

One of the critics is Roger Flaxman, chairman of London-based insurance claims advocacy service Flaxman Partners, who has worked in the industry for more than 40 years and acts as an expert witness in court cases.

'My initial reaction to the known facts of this case is that it is an astonishing decision which bears much closer scrutiny,' he says.

'Assuming the Weldins bought the insurance in good faith, then this outcome seems at the very least a disproportionate penalty.

'It is a serious matter for any insurance company to dispossess a family of its home, and the apparent reasoning in this case appears irrational by a modern insurer.'

The Weldins took their case to the Financial Ombudsman, whose job it is to decide what is fair and reasonable, but it sided with the insurer.

Alex Rosenfield, associate at Fenchurch Law, says: 'The Weldins' case appears to be an example of an insurer relying upon a purely technical defence to avoid paying a claim, resulting in an unduly harsh outcome.

'The starting point in any such dispute is, did the consumer take reasonable care to represent the true position to their insurer? In the Weldins' case, we are not at all convinced that the Ombudsman's view in that respect is the correct one.'

Consumer expert James Daley, founder of insurance ratings agency Fairer Finance, has also written to Ageas boss Andy Watson to ask him to intervene personally.

Mr Daley says: 'I wrote to him to say I didn't think the outcome was fair or proportionate and that it epitomises the lack of humanity in insurance.

'I also said that I know Ageas strives to hold itself to a higher standard, so would he reconsider on this basis.'

Ageas claims Paul Weldin, 42, and wife Sophie, 44, wrongly insured their home as a five-bedroom house when, in fact, it had seven bedrooms. It says their policy is void as a result.

But the couple, who live in East Devon with their four children, argue that two of the rooms could not be termed as bedrooms as they were in the attic space, were used only for storage and did not meet the building regulations required to call a room a bedroom.

But as the Financial Ombudsman has sided with the insurer, it means the family has to find the £460,000 needed to repair the house and replace their belongings.

But experts believe the Ombudsman was wrong to say the couple had been 'careless' when buying the Ageas policy, which they did online in a tick-box process. The Weldins answered all of the questions — which did not include any about the use of loft or roof space.

The experts also say it was wrong of the Ombudsman to say that the rooms' compliance or otherwise with building regulations was not relevant. And they claim the effect of the decision on the family, who bought the cover in good faith, is 'disproportionate' and the penalty too high.

Money Mail has been inundated with letters from readers backing the Weldins. Many can't understand why the number of bedrooms the house contained should make a difference if they played no role in the fire. 

In the Weldins' case, the fire is believed to have started as a result of a faulty security light outside the front door.

Readers have also pointed out that the amount of cover the couple bought would more than cover the cost of rebuilding their home and replacing their belongings.

Many homeowners say they fear their insurers could reject claims based on similar technicalities. Some readers are so dismayed by the decision, they have offered donations to help the family take the insurer to court to try to get a fair outcome.

Val Lee, from Hampshire, says: 'The Ombudsman's ruling in this instance surely means that anyone can shove a bed up into their loft or attic and thereby ignore all safety aspects (including fire risks) and then, on selling the property, can enhance the sale price by claiming additional bedrooms.

'I feel strongly that an injustice has been done and would urge Mr and Mrs Weldin to run a crowd-funding appeal so that they are in a financial position to question the Ombudsman and insurance company's ruling and get a satisfactory outcome. I, for one, would donate.'

Roy Page, of Salisbury, Wiltshire, says: 'I was incensed and saddened by your article. Attic rooms can only be classified as bedrooms if they comply with building and fire regulations.'

Others have threatened to boycott the firm.

Ageas, based in Belgium, is the sixth largest general insurer in the UK and has more than 3.5 million car, home and travel insurance customers.

Chief executive Mr Watson, took part in a panel discussion about putting consumers 'at the heart of the process' at an Association of British Insurers conference in London in February. In March he said: 'If we struggle to treat customers fairly, then we have a problem.'

He also appears in numerous online videos tackling insurance issues. In one, he is asked to describe Ageas in three words. 'Customer-focused,' he answers, followed by 'energised' and 'partnerships'.

Mr Watson told Money Mail this week: 'We take our responsibilities to all our customers seriously — so seriously in fact that in 2017 Ageas paid out £150 million to 74,000 customers who fairly described the home they needed to insure.'
