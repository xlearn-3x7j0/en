ALEX BRUMMER: Advisory fees in the City continue to soar

When it comes to boardroom excess there is no shortage of examples. Galloping into view in recent times has been the £2m mortgage subsidy for well-heeled Prudential chief executive Mike Wells.

The egregious £112m pay-out to Jeff Fairburn at Persimmon (reduced by £25m under pressure) and the potential £285m rewards for victorious Melrose bosses after the takeover of engineer GKN.

But the real scandal surrounds payments to advisers, lawyers and auditors whose high pay for often light and unimpressive work largely passes under the radar. In the heat of the takeover battles or the administration or insolvency of large firms free market capitalism fails miserably.

Time-constrained companies have no time to conduct beauty parades or initiate a tendering. The result is that they become price takers rather than price givers.

The Melrose assault on GKN did not come cheaply. Thanks to the transparency of the offer document we know its bankers – Rothschild, RBC and Investec – will share £50m in fees and up to £69m more is possible for debt raising to finance the deal.

As for GKN, its bankers Gleacher Shacklock, JP Morgan and UBS will receive £60m for the defence. Communications adviser Montford is set for up to £5m and FTI Consulting and Brunswick will get about £6m.

One of the features of modern takeovers and stock market floats is how a cartel of advisers swoop in like carrion birds.

When one considers that Melrose made no profits at all last year – and indeed has been running up losses at its Bush offshoot – the amount spent on trying to secure riches for the company’s bosses looks obscene.

The sense of entitlement among City firms is illustrated by the work of PwC in winding up Carillion. Insolvency always has been rewarded highly and PwC has so far helped itself to £20m in fees. And it has managed to anger all those involved with surcharges of up to 20 per cent on reassigned contracts.

AS Carillion headed for the buffers, it paid £6.4m for advice provided by disgraced auditors KPMG as well as EY, PwC, Lazard and lawyers Slaughter & May – all of whom were called in during the final days.

Indeed, a feature of many flawed deals is the forces behind them had no hesitation in bringing out the big guns.

During Parliamentary hearings into the failure of BHS, we heard how ‘magic circle’ firm Linklaters relied upon work done by Olswang and Grant Thornton in signing off the sale of the department store by Sir Philip Green to Dominic Chappell. Among severe critics of the City law firms is the director of the Serious Fraud Office David Green QC. He accuses the magic circle of ‘ploughing the field’ and establishing qualified privilege by conducting ‘independent’ investigations before the authorities arrive on the scene.

At the big four accounting firms, partner fees at Deloitte reached £837,000 last year, although they fell away at KPMG after a series of blunders. This is minor league compared to the big City law firms such as Linklaters, where pay-outs to partners have smashed through the £1.5m mark.

None of this might matter if the public felt that professional firms were doing a brilliant job. Carillion, BHS, Toys R Us et al suggest deep flaws.

Much has changed in the City since the financial crisis. But advisory fees for bankers, lawyers, accountants and PR advisers continue to soar. What is deeply troubling is that advisers feel they are as entitled to rich rewards as big employers.

But grasping fees and benefits largely escape proper scrutiny.
