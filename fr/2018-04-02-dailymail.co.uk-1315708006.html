Chelsea 1-3 Tottenham: Dele Alli double ends 28-year wait for visitors

Mauricio Pochettino stayed until every last one of his players had left the field. 

He let Dele Alli and Mousa Dembele perform one of those endless, daft hand gesture celebrations, waited for Alli to run the length of the field to present his shirt, scrunched up, to a fan, paused for Son Heung-min to seek out and acknowledge the supporters with the South Korea flag.

Pochettino knew the importance of this win for Tottenham. Huge, the biggest of the season. Bigger than Real Madrid, now we know how far it got them, bigger than the back to back league wins over Manchester United and Arsenal at Wembley. This wasn't just a game, it was a game-changer, for Tottenham, and Chelsea, too.

Barring a quite spectacular collapse in the final two months – and whatever jokes have been made at Tottenham 's expense, they are too good for that – this result places a metaphoric ocean between these teams in terms of where they appear to be heading next season. 

Tottenham, back to the Champions League for the third consecutive campaign, and their first at the new White Hart Lane. For Chelsea, to the Europa League at best, with all the attendant uncertainty such a failing introduces. 

Will they be able to keep Eden Hazard while out of the Champions League for a second season in three? They probably won't be working with Antonio Conte beyond the summer, that can be presumed. As for the squad, how will it be shaped from here? What will a new man make of Victor Moses' lapses at full-back, will he persevere with Andreas Christensen or demand another finished article to slot in his place? 

Moses was at fault for the first Tottenham goal, Christensen should have cleared when Alli added the third, killing the game and Chelsea's league season unless a battle for Thursday night football is floating any boats. This is the win that establishes Tottenham as London's leading club right now – and at Chelsea the bloodletting begins anew.

So Pochettino counted them all out, and counted them all in again. He knew the importance of Tottenham holding their nerve in what was, for long periods, a very even game against a team that had an enviable record in this fixture.

It was 1990 when a Gary Lineker goal last gave Tottenham victory over Chelsea at Stamford Bridge, and this stadium was the scene of the most horrid humiliation of the Pochettino era, the chaotic, ill-disciplined denouement to the 2015-16 season.

Leicester may still be waiting for Tottenham, but Tottenham do not have to wait for their moment of redemption against Chelsea any longer. This felt like a watershed, certainly for football in the capital. 

The odd result might go against them, but Tottenham have been travelling in a different direction to Arsenal for several seasons now. Chelsea, though, are obstinate. Chelsea are winners. They have the one thing Tottenham do not possess, still. Trophies. 

Yet if Pochettino and his players can stay together, they could reverse that trend, too. It is not unthinkable that this fixture is this season's FA Cup final. A trophy for Tottenham at Chelsea's expense would be far from unimaginable if that happened.

Yet for Conte and his players it started so positively. They were good in the first-half. Organised, excellent on the counter-attack, and should have gone in at half-time a goal ahead. Hugo Lloris is many things, but one thing he is not is Anthony Joshua.

With another four inches or so he might have done enough to divert Moses' 29th minute cross. Might. That's the key word. Even with the height of a heavyweight champion, however, that cannot be guaranteed. He was very short of dealing with Chelsea's opener.

And that was a big mistake, because Chelsea are the headed goal specialists in the Premier League this season. When Alvaro Morata scored it was their 14th of 2017-18, two more than any other club, with Morata the specialists' specialist. He has 11 this season, including seven headers, the best return in the air of any player in the league.

Antonio Rudiger hit his favourite crossfield ball, deep left to forward right, like a bishop's uninterrupted diagonal in chess, it picked out Moses in enough space to select his target and execute a perfectly tempting cross. Lloris, who looks far from fallible of late, took the bait and came. 

He didn't get near. He needed a pogo stick, or stilts. The ball sailed over one despairing hand – there was not a prayer he could take it with two – and fell to the player who lives for those opportunities. Morata can be a frustrating presence, spending so much time on the floor he could be nicknamed 'Underlay' – say it with a Spanish accent, like Speedy Gonzalez – but he knows what he is about when a cross comes in. There was no chance Lloris would not be made to pay for his error of judgement.

Yet the moment Tottenham equalised in first-half injury time, the momentum in this game changed. Opinion is divided on the influence of Willy Caballero, standing in for the injured Thibaut Courtois, in Tottenham's revival, but Chelsea did not appear as comfortable with their back-up back stop. Could he have done better for the first goal?

Some will argue Christian Eriksen's strike was up there with any this season, that it moved and spun in the air and no goalkeeper would have had a chance. Alternately, Caballero was beaten by one that wasn't far from him, and should have dealt with it. 

It wasn't the first time he seemed startled by Eriksen's shooting power, either. Just seven minutes earlier, he had palmed one out which Eriksen hit from 25 yards, recovering to avert danger. Second time, he did not get that opportunity.

As against Barcelona, sloppiness changed the complexion of the game for Chelsea. Tottenham had enjoyed a lengthy period of possession in Chelsea's half, but had been repelled by a tight defensive line. Then Moses decided to try something clever, dangerous, and unnecessary. A little flick from deep on the right side, over two Tottenham players, to Willian. 

The pass didn't make it. Worse, it was recycled swiftly to Erikssen – Tottenham's most likely scorer. His shot was a pearler, shifting, dipping, somehow dropping over the head of Cabellero who was barely off his line. Maybe he thought it was going high; maybe he was simply bamboozled.

Tottenham emerged a different team in the second-half and were well worth their win by the end. Like a man with a point to prove – which, let's face it, he is – it was Alli whose coolness in front of goal encapsulated what set them apart. In the space of four minutes, he steered Tottenham out of sight.

Chelsea's errors played their part. When Eric Dier played a sweet through ball from deep after 62 minutes, Christensen and Cesar Azpilicueta somehow allowed Alli to split them with a lovely curving run. It left him with only Caballero to beat, which he did with a calm that belied his years. The best 21-year-old in the world? That's some claim from Pochettino, but there are plenty a decade older who cannot keep their heads in front of goal as Alli does.

He had some help with the next. Son made a furious run down the left, but Chelsea had several chances to clear. Caballero was scrambling, Christensen and Alonso air-kicking. Alli resolved it all for them; that and several other issues, too.
