Giancarlo Stanton Quiets the Boos by Leading the Yankees’ Homer Barrage

The bat flip pretty much said it all.

On Tuesday, Giancarlo Stanton experienced a Bronxian rite of passage, when a player expected to be great has an awful day and is booed before he is cheered.

Even though Stanton led the major leagues with 59 home runs for the Miami Marlins last year and opened his Yankees career with a pair of home runs in a 6-1 win over the Blue Jays in Toronto last Thursday, his five strikeouts in his first game at Yankee Stadium earned him nothing but a resounding Bronx cheer, despite his team’s win over the Tampa Bay Rays.

Some of that discontent lingered into Wednesday, as more than a couple of boos mixed in with the cheers as Stanton strode to the plate in the first inning against Rays starter Blake Snell. But any negative crowd feedback melted away when he crushed the fourth pitch he saw into the second deck beyond left field to give the Yankees a 2-1 first-inning lead on their way to a 7-2 victory.

After Stanton’s home run, his third of the season, he struck out three more times, giving him eight in his last nine at-bats, and he once again drew the wrath of the Yankee Stadium crowd.

But there was plenty more for the crowd to be pleased about, as Stanton’s blast, with an exit velocity of 117.9 miles per hour and a distance of 458 feet, was the first of three home runs by the Yankees.

Gary Sanchez, who hit 33 home runs in 122 games last season, followed Stanton’s third-inning strikeout with a two-run homer that ended an 0-for-17 slump and gave the Yankees a 4-1 lead. Aaron Judge, who finished second to Stanton in the American League with 52 home runs in 2017, hit his first homer of the season, a two-run shot in the fourth that extended the lead to 7-1.

In addition to the power surge, the Yankees got dominant starting pitching from Luis Severino, who settled down after allowing a first-inning run and limited the Rays to two singles over seven and one-third innings. Severino is now 2-0 with a 1.38 E.R.A.

It was still Stanton’s shot that left the deepest impression, especially since he punctuated it with a vigorous bat flip.

It seemed to be something of a statement to the Rays, to Snell, and perhaps even to the segment of Yankees nation that had given him the raspberries the previous day.

“Giancarlo is a smart guy,” Yankees Manager Aaron Boone said before the game. “I think he absolutely knows what he signed up for.”

Didi Gregorius, the star of the Yankees’ home opener with two home runs and a career-high eight runs batted in, had suffered through his own rite of Bronx passage in his first month as a Yankee in 2015, a reaction intensified by the fact he was replacing Derek Jeter at shortstop.

“It’s a bad day today,” Gregorius said of Stanton after Monday’s game. “But tomorrow if he gets five home runs, everybody’s going to forget about it.”

For a while, anyway. When Stanton came up for the second time on Wednesday, there was an expectant buzz in the crowd. But not even a home run as impressive as Stanton’s first-inning laser earns a player all that much equity at Yankee Stadium. After he struck out on a slider, Stanton was booed on his way back to the dugout.

He was booed again after being called out on strikes to end the fourth inning, but after he swung through a fastball from reliever Chaz Roe in the seventh, the crowd was largely mute. With Stanton, Judge and, to a lesser extent, Sanchez, even the demanding Yankee Stadium audience seems to accept that home runs and strikeouts will often go hand in hand.

On Tuesday, for example, Rays pitchers struck out Yankees hitters 14 times — but still allowed 11 runs, six of them on Gregorius’s homers.

On Wednesday, the Yankees struck out 11 times — and canceled those out with the three mammoth home runs.

Stanton struck out 163 times last year with the Marlins, a total that would have placed him a distant second on the Yankees, behind Judge’s 203. But their combined total of 111 home runs would have been the second-highest single-season total in franchise history, behind Roger Maris and Mickey Mantle’s combined 115 in 1961.
