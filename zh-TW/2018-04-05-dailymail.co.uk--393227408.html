Ohio residents report sightings of 'zombie raccoons'

Local Ohio police found themselves face-to-face with several feral raccoons at the weekend.

Over the last few weeks, police in Youngstown, Ohio, have had to respond to over a dozen calls detailing how the zombie-like rodents have been exhibiting strange behavior. 

Most of the phone calls have been made during the daytime, which is a strange occurrence given raccoons' propensity to be nocturnal.

'I looked over there and got distracted because I saw a raccoon coming our way,' said Robert Coggeshall to WKBN. 

Youngstown, Ohio, police have had to respond to numerous calls of raccoons exhibiting 'extremely strange behavior' (stock) 

Coggeshall's routine playtime with his dogs was cut short when a sickly raccoon made its way toward them.

He adds that even when he tried to bring the dogs inside, the raccoon followed them up until the door. 

The wildlife photographer was quick to grab his camera and snap photos of the 'extremely strange behavior.    

'He would stand up on his hind legs, which I've never seen a raccoon do before, and he would show his teeth and then he would fall over backward and go into almost a comatose condition,' Coggeshall added.

The spooked man attempted to scare the pest but found that it was unbothered. 

'He'd come out of it, walk around and then he'd do the same thing again. Get on his hind feet and show his teeth,' Coggeshall said.

In the past three weeks, authorities have had to respond to 14 calls depicting similar altercations. 

The 'particular behavior' is daunting because raccoons aren't responding to loud noises or motions to scare them away.

All have had to be put down.  

According to the Ohio Department of Natural Resources, the pests don't appear to have rabies but actually a disease called distemper. 

Distemper is a viral disease that normally infects unvaccinated dogs but can also infect animals like coyotes, foxes and skunks. 

It can cause brain damage along with respiratory disease, seizures, immobility and even death.  

For raccoons, the symptoms do favor rabies but aren't exactly the same. They also tend to become infected by coming in contact with body fluids or droppings. 

'Raccoons are really prone to getting diseases that even among themselves can be devastating to the population,' Geoff Westerfield, a wildlife biologist with the Ohio Department of Natural Resources’ Division of Wildlife, explained.
