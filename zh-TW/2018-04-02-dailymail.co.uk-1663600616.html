Brussels plans new laws to stop on fake news ahead of 2019 elections

  • Commissioner Julian King said companies should disclose 'sponsored content' 
  • In a letter he said the 'harvesting' of personal information needed to be limited
  • EU bosses are cracking down on 'fake news' ahead of the next European poll 

Published: 12:49 EDT, 2 April 2018 | Updated: 14:11 EDT, 2 April 2018

New powers to stop 'fake news' on social media from influencing elections are being considered by EU leaders. 

Brussels wants companies to reveal who is paying for 'sponsored content' on their websites in a bid to promote transparency during election campaigns. 

EU bosses are cracking down on 'fake news' ahead of the next European Parliament elections, scheduled for May 2019, it is reported. 

Julian King, European Commissioner for Security, wrote in a letter that there needed to be limits on the 'harvesting' of personal information for political purposes, the Financial Times reports.

His letter to fellow commissioner Mariya Gabriel, who holds the digital economy and society portfolio, said a 'more binding approach' than self-regulation may be required. 

The changes should include 'clearly and carefully defined performance indicators', he said. 

The letter said that cyber-attacks on elections were not just about damaging systems but also about deepening social divides and encouraging doubts about the democratic process. 

Commissioners are set to lay out proposals on how to tackle 'online disinformation' later this month. 

It comes after a Cambridge Analytica whistleblower suggested that Facebook gathered data from up to 50 million people to help its work during the 2016 U.S. presidential election.  

Christopher Wylie appeared in front of a Commons committee to answer questions on Cambridge Analytica's influence over the Brexit referendum result. 

Cambridge Analytica has denied the claims while Facebook denies spying on people to customise its adverts. 

Facebook founder Mark Zuckerberg said on Monday that an advertising-supported business model is the only way that the service can survive because not everyone would be able to pay for Facebook if it charged a fee.
