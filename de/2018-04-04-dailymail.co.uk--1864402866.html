Lifeguards were scared to ride the deadly Verruckt slide

Lifeguards at the Schlitterbahn water park were afraid to ride the Verruckt water slide that eventually caused a 10-year-old boy's decapitation.

Former Schlitterbahn lifeguard Nathan Campbell said he and other workers were required to ride Verruckt every day, but no one ever wanted to volunteer because they regularly saw test dummies fly off the world's largest slide. 

'We had to ride it three times before we actually opened the park every day,' Campbell told WDAF on March 25. 'They would ask lifeguards who would want to volunteer and no one would put their hands up.' 

He continued: 'It was like, "no I don’t want to do it." They are just making us, like, "oh yeah, go test this, it is fine," even though the test dummies fly off.'

On one of the days Campbell was chosen to test the slide, he says his raft flipped and crashed into a wall on the third trip down and he suffered a back injury. 

He was sent home for the injury, but the incident was never reported, he says.  That was the last summer he worked there.  

The slide has been shut down ever since the August 2016 death of 10-year-old Caleb Schwab, who was decapitated when his raft went airborne and his head hit an overhead loop.  

One of the three men arrested in connection to Schwab's death is the park's former operations manager, who is accused of intercepting incident reports from lifeguards and destroying witness statements.

The park's co-owner Jeff Henry, left, and the slide's designer John Schooley, right, have been arrested on second-degree murder charges 

Miles is said to have coached lifeguards into writing incident reports that hid crucial details about the ride's safety. He has been charged with a slew of counts - including involuntary manslaughter.

But the heftiest charges are focused on the park's co-owner, Jeff Henry, and the slide's designer, John Schooley.

According to the indictment, Henry was allegedly inspired to build the slide to get on a Travel Channel TV show, and he roped Schooley into designing it, even though Schooley had no engineering experience.

Instead, the two relied mostly on trial and error in creating the slide, as opposed to the rigorous modelling that professional engineers are trained to do.   

On Wednesday, Henry, right, bonded out of jail. Schooley, left, also appeared in court in Texas, where he was ordered extradited to Kansas to face his charges 

Both men had court appearances on Wednesday. 

Henry posted $500,000 bond within hours of being booked into the Wyandotte County jail in Kansas.  

Henry was extradited from Texas, where he lives, after he was indicted last week on charges including second-degree murder in Schwab's 2016 death. 

His attorney, Carl Cornwell, spoke outside of court on Wednesday, saying that they plan to fight the charges.

'What they are trying to do with my client is say, "He just didn’t give a damn, that he didn’t give a damn about anyone sliding down that water slide,"' Cornwell said.

Schooley was arrested Monday in Texas after returning on a flight from China and also faces charges including second-degree murder. During a hearing Wednesday, he agreed to be extradited to Kansas and will be transferred there next week, said Dallas County sheriff's spokesman Raul Reyna. An attorney for Schooley didn't respond to a request for comment.
