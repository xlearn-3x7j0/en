The strange stories behind Augusta's quirky traditions

It is a tournament unlike any other, due to its proud traditions and pristine conditions. The Masters is back and the excitement for the first major of the year is as big as ever.

14-time major champion Tiger Woods returns to Augusta for the first time since 2015 after recovering from recurring back problems, and is among the favourites for the green jacket along with the likes of Justin Thomas, Jordan Spieth, Rory McIlroy and Dustin Johnson.

However, it isn't only the fierce competition that captivates golf fans worldwide. Aside from the colourful nature and beauty of the course, the Masters preserves many proud traditions that ooze class.

Here, Sportsmail takes a look at the history behind the Masters, which began all the way back in 1934, when it was originally known as the 'Augusta National Invitation Tournament'.

THE COURSE

Amen Corner

Where better to start than the most famous part of the course. Amen Corner, comprising of the 11th, 12th and 13th hole, is one of the most hallowed places in golf. It never fails to deliver drama. The name was coined by Herbert Warren Wind in a 1958 issue of Sports Illustrated. 

He wrote: 'At the farthest reach of the Augusta National course—down in the Amen Corner where Rae's Creek intersects the 13th fairway near the tee, then parallels the front edge of the green on the short 12th and finally swirls alongside the 11th green.' 

Wind said that he'd been inspired by a jazz song called 'Shouting at Amen Corner,' by Mezz Mezzrow. Those lucky spectators at Augusta, can watch most of the famous three holes from the same spot on the 12th.

Amen Corner, comprising of the 11th, 12th and 13th holes, is one of golf's hallowed places

They paint the grass

If you watch the Masters, you straight away realise just how spotless the conditions are every day. But as you can expect, brown spots do appear on the tees, fairways and greens.

But according to Andy Stranger, one green-keeper who has worked at Augusta, he has revealed they work on making everything look green 100 per cent of the time for the cameras. 

He told National Club Golfer: 'You will get weak spots, bits that are in the shade we will be working on. Anything that can be picked up on camera will be painted green.'

All plastic bags are green

In keeping with the colourful theme of Augusta, all bags that are sold at the Masters are green! Wondering why now? They are that colour so that they blend in perfectly with the grass if picked up by television cameras.

Those picturesque fairways and greens are usually the result of artificial paint being applied

The holes have a theme to them

Every hole at the historic course is named after a flowering shrub or trees. This has been done to give a nod to the heritage of the property on which Augusta now sits. When the club's founders brought the land, it had been a plant nursery. Each hole showcases the plant after which it is named.

Augusta is next to a major road

When you watch the Masters on television, you may innocently think that Augusta is miles away from anything. But actually, the course is right next to a busy interstate, called Washington Road.

Be aware if you ever plan on making the trip to Augusta, then this road is not fun during Masters week!

Water contains food dye 

Have you ever looked at the water at Augusta and thought how do they make it look so perfect? Well, here it goes. An American journalist took a sample from the pond in front of the 15th and ran tests to reveal the water had food dye in it. It turns out, it was a dye similar to that used in blue-coloured icing. 

The bunkers at Augusta National aren't filled with sand but a mining waste product call 'quartz'

Bunkers are not filled with sand

Ever wondered how they get the bunkers to look so clean as well? Again, it's not what you think, they don't actually use sand. It is a waste product from mining and is a substance called 'quartz'.

Hogan Bridge covered in fake grass

The Hogan Bridge, next to the 12th green, is one of the most photographed sights of Augusta. It is made of stone and crosses Rae's Creek. Due to its arch, the bridge is covered in artificial turf to help the world's best players wearing studs get better grip.

THE CLUB 

Augusta relaxes its membership policy in 1990

Augusta National, which opened in 1932, did not allow black members to join until 1990. 

Meanwhile in 2012, Augusta finally let females join the club. Prior to this, women were allowed to play the golf course as guests, including on the Sunday before the Masters week began in April.  

However, Augusta still remains very private...

To join this great golf club, a current member has to nominate you to stand any chance of being accepted. Among those belonging to Augusta, include Bill Gates and Warren Buffett, a wealthy American businessman. 

Oh and in case you were wondering, it would only cost between £8,000 and £24,000 to become a member! 

Becoming a member of Augusta can set you back anywhere between £8,000 and £24,000

Magnolia Lane

The most famous drive at Augusta doesn't involve a golf club or ball. The entrance to the club is where those lucky enough to play in the Masters, really pick up their excitement levels.

The entrance, just off Washington Road, stretches 330 yards and is well known for the large amount of magnolia trees that align the side of the road. 

Three-time green jacket winner Gary Player said: 'The Masters is the only tournament I ever knew where you choke when you drive through the front gate.'

No women's Masters

As you already know, there is no Masters for women to compete in. Currently, the LPGA has five majors: the ANA Inspiration, the US Open, the Ricoh British Open, the Women's PGA Championship and the Evian Championship. 

The one closest major to the Masters is the ANA Inspiration, which preserves some traditions. The caddies wear white jumpsuits and the winner customarily jumps into Poppie's Pond just off the 18th green.

RITUALS 

Green jacket

The biggest tradition at the Masters is the awarding of the green jacket to the champion on the final day. 

Every year, the winner is ceremonially fitted with the emblem of Augusta National membership, the green jacket. In fact, it happens twice, once for television cameras in Butler Cabin and another for photographers and the crowds outside.

The green jacket was introduced in 1937, with the idea that Augusta members would wear them during the tournament to stand out to the public. Then in 1949 Sam Snead became the first winner to be honoured with the now famous item of clothing... and it has been a tradition ever since.

After Snead was awarded the green jacket, Augusta retroactively awarded them to each former winner.

Former Masters champions wear their green jackets as they attend the Champions Dinner

Champions Dinner

This annual dinner, one of the most exclusive clubs in the world of sport, sees past champions come together on the Tuesday before the tournament gets underway.  The food menu is chosen by the previous year's champion. This time, it was held by Sergio Garcia, who chose a menu inspired by his Spanish roots.

Par-3 contest

The nine-hole tournament, which takes place on Wednesday, is when the excitement really starts to kick in for fans. Despite some of the game's better players declining to participate, the field for the par-3 contest is still always star-studded and they bring their their children, partners or other celebrities to serve as their caddies.

Caddies must wear jumpsuits

For many years at the Masters, players weren't allowed to use their own caddies. In 1983, this rule was lifted and they were allowed to use who they wanted, as long as their No 2s dressed in the required caddie attire - a white jumpsuit. 

Caddies, such as that of Matt Kelly (left), were permitted in 1983 but made to wear jumpsuits

TELEVISION 

Butler Cabin

Those that watch the Masters annually will be very aware of the Butler Cabin, one of the most historic buildings at Augusta. Directly after winning the tournament, the champion will be directed to the building, where they get interviewed on their achievement.  

The victorious player will also be presented with his famous green jacket for the first time by the previous year's champion.  

Why there's no TV coverage on Thursday or Friday afternoon

Basically, because Augusta says so. 

Officials are keen to keep live TV coverage to an absolute minimum for no reason other than to maintain that unique sense of exclusivity. Even when the cameras are switched on, they only follow a select few groups. 

Sky Sports begin their live coverage at 8:00pm BST, by which time every player would have teed off with the last group (Justin Rose, Dustin Johnson, Rafael Cabrera-Bello) scheduled to start at 7:00pm.

Artificial bird noises used on TV

When you are watching the tournament at home, television companies put bird noises in to make the whole scene more picturesque, to go along with the green fairways, colourful bushes and white jumpsuits. 

American network CBS once admitted to using bird noises to make Augusta seem more of a 'natural paradise'.

AND FINALLY... FOOD!

We couldn't go this whole time without talking about the food on offer at Augusta. Some of the stand-out items on offer include the iconic pimento cheese sandwich, egg salad sandwich and sausage biscuit. Wondering if the prices are rather hefty, well fear not!

A pimento cheese costs just $1.50, while the most expensive sandwiches on the menu, the Bar-B-Que, Grilled Chicken Wrap and Classic Chicken sandwich, cost only $3.00. Bargain.
