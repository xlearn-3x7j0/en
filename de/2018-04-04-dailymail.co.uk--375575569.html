Ray Wilkins was a one-off and played football from his heart

Butch was a one-off. Show me a man who didn't warm to Ray 'Butch' Wilkins and I'll show you a liar.

I met him when he was just 15, when he already knew he had exceptional talent, but combined confidence in his burgeoning ability with a certain modest humility.

It was hard not to take to him on a first handshake. His pleasant demeanour, his open smile, were immediately infectious.

Ray Wilkins (pictured) at Chelsea's Mitcham training ground during his time with the club

Wilkins died, aged 61, of a heart attack in St George's Hospital in Tooting, London this week

But his teenage innocence in the dressing room gave way to a maturity way beyond his years once he crossed that line onto a football pitch. There he felt most at home and at his most adult.

Ray was not your modern mercenary. He played the game from his heart. He played because he loved it, the rewards and the fame it gave him were mere incidentals.

As captain of the England Youth side in the early seventies, he set a standard. Ferociously proud to captain his country in his teens he was a dedicated trainer, an inspiration to his team mates and a leader.

At Chelsea, where his heart truly lay, he developed those leadership qualities. He was made skipper in his teens and embraced the responsibility

These were the days when we critics were invited into the dressing room. Ray was always the last to leave, painstakingly using a hairdryer to shape his head of dark hair.

The midfielder had good spells outside England with AC Milan (above), PSG and Rangers

I would joke with him about it, suggesting if he took as much care over those passes he used to thread through for the likes of Peter Osgood as he did over his grooming, Chelsea's results might improve.

So it is ironical that he went bald at a young age. I always ribbed him that it was over concentration on that damned coiffuring!

In reality there was nothing wrong with his vision on the pitch. He excelled at seeing the wider picture. I used to tease him, suggesting he was the poor man's Johan Cruyff. 

In reality England could do with his control from midfield as they head for Russia this summer.

Indeed it is no coincidence that Don Revie, Ron Greenwood and Bobby Robson all appreciated his organisational skills as he compiled a very impressive 84 England caps.

After a glittering playing career, Wilkins was assistant and caretaker at various clubs

He had his critics, of course. Ron Atkinson, his manager at Manchester United used to call him The Crab because of his tendency to pass the ball sideways.

That was part myth. His passing ability was up with the best and he and Bryan Robson were my best remembered midfield pairing in 40 years of covering England.

Just a month ago he popped into my local in Weybridge, ironically called The British Volunteer after Kitchener's famous 'You Country Needs You' slogan... It fitted him perfectly. He was so proud to serve his country.

He was his usually buoyant self despite his recent heart surgery and we reminisced about his life and his career. Little did I realise it would be the last time I was to see him.

Like those other modest and likeable stars, Bobby Moore and Gary Speed, he left before his time and football and sport have lost another fine ambassador.
