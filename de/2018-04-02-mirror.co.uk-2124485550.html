Our reporters debate who Chelsea should apoit if they sack Antonio Conte

Antonio Conte is fighting for his job at Stamford Bridge - less than 12 months after leading Chelsea to the Premier League title.

The Italian was rewarded with a bumper new deal following their triumph, but finds himself on the brink after a disastrous campaign that leaves them struggling to even make the top four.

Chelsea are eight points adrift of Spurs, who occupy the final Champions League spot, after Sunday's 3-1 home defeat against their London rivals.

Pressure on Conte has cranked up another notch, with speculation rife that he will be on his bike in the summer.

But who could replace the former Juventus boss at the Bridge? Our reporters have their say:

James Nursey - Carlo Ancelotti

The Italian has already proved he can be a success in the Premier League with a landmark double win in 2010 for the Stamford Bridge club.

Now I think they should bring him back like Blues did with Jose Mourinho who then won Chelsea the title again.

I think Ancelotti has the personality and presence to handle Chelsea's big stars and get the best out of them.

From what I have heard about his management, he is superb as a man motivator and knows how to deal with big egos.

Currently unattached after leaving Bayern Munich , he has already stated an interest in returning to England and the Premier League.

Mike Walters - Max Allegri

Portugese, Israeli, Dutch, Italian, Brazilian... think of a country, and Roman Abramovich has probably been shopping there for a manager.

There is already plenty of talk about former Barcelona coach Luis Enrique checking the District Line timetable for Underground trains to Fulham Broadway, but if Abramovich is feeling adventurous, how about former Borussia Dortmund boss Thomas Tuchel or another Italian, Max Allegri, for whom there are few mountains left to climb at Juventus?

Allegri would be my choice, but it is unlikely that Chelsea will go for another Italian with similar footballing DNA straight after parting company with Antonio Conte, which now looks inevitable.

Whoever gets the job will find the purse strings tight at Stamford Bridge. Poor old Roman is down to his last £10 billion or so.

Darren Lewis - Thomas Tuchel

There is really little point in throwing top names around as if Chelsea are the attractive, top-line outfit they once were.

Conte's exit should be a red flag to potential candidates that the club don't have a commitment to investing well enough to support their manager.

He has his failings, of course. It is inconceivable that he has had no say whatsoever in the recruitment policy.

But he has said since last summer that the squad is not strong enough. Now he appears to be vindicated.

Bayern Munich are claiming Thomas Tuchel has already committed his future to a new club and that

they will not be signing him for next season. If Chelsea have already landed Tuchel then they would have someone able to make

the best of what he has in difficult circumstances, as he did at Dortmund. But they need somebody capable of attracting top

players and leading them back into battle against a clutch of teams in the top four with clear strategies in terms of what they are trying to do.

Neil Moxley - Rafael Benitez

Not going to be popular - but I’d like to see what Rafael Benitez could do if he was given the gig on a permanent basis.

He’s earned respect for staying on at St James’s Park and providing more stability than Newcastle owner Mike Ashley probably deserves.

Benitez has pretty-much unrivalled international experience, got on well with the Chelsea hierarchy first-time out and won over the doubters. Let’s face it, if Jose Mourinho can take over at Manchester United, the Spaniard can do so at the Bridge.

More importantly, he would be well-received in the dressing-room. I think Benitez would try to bring back John Terry in a coaching capacity too. A move that is sure to appeal.

James Whaling - Frank Lampard

I understand the cry for players coming out of the game to cut their teeth at a lower level - but Frank Lampard could be an exception.

The Blues legend is clearly a studious thinker about the game and, on the face of it, he has everything it takes to be a top manager.

There seems to be a theme of iconic players taking over in the dugout of their former clubs in recent years; Gennaro Gattuso at Milan and Zinedine Zidane at Real Madrid to name a couple.

While it would be an undoubted risk to appoint Lampard - could he really fail?

His legacy at Stamford Bridge is secure; the club's all-time top goal scorer - from midfield no less - and a clutch of major honours to his name.

He would unite dressing room and would certainly have the fans behind him.

Roman Abramovich could do worse.

Mark Jones - Diego Simeone

The Atletico Madrid boss has had contract issues in the Spanish capital for the past few seasons, and although he did sign a new deal last September to take him through to 2020 you do get the impression that the bond isn't as strong as it once was between him and the club.

A potential summer sale of Antoine Griezmann would test his relationship with them even further, and although he's been quite public about his desire to go and manage in Italy, surely he'd be tempted by the offer from Stamford Bridge if it came along.

The gritty Argentinean possesses plenty of the qualities we associated with Conte when he first came to the Blues, and he'd surely be a hit with the fans. It would be an ambitious move, but isn't it time that Chelsea were a bit more ambitious?

David Maddock

They should get a Mauricio Pochettino or Jurgen Klopp, and then give the new manager five years to get the dozens and dozens of promising young players they sign back from their pointless loan spells, and develop them into an exciting team, all working for each other, and all with a common cause.

Wait, Liverpool and Spurs have already done that, and will be the main rivals to Manchester City for the next five years.

That's what Chelsea SHOULD do, but they won't of course. They'll sign an experienced manager with a track record at a big European club, who plays boring, defensive football, give him a lot of money to win a trophy in his first season, and then expect him to operate under a completely different system from there on in. And then sack him of course, after two seasons or so.

David Anderson

So yet again, Chelsea’s chaos theory when it comes to coaching is leaving them searching for another manager.

If a coach comes in and succeeds like Antonio Conte and Jose Mourinho did initially, they are rewarded handsomely in financial terms.

But they soon discover they have no credit in the bank when results take a tumble.

Out goes that coach and in comes another one who must also work with a loaded gun pointed to his head.

Any coach would be doomed to fail working under such conditions and their plight is not helped by the fact they do not have full control over signings.

The landscape has also changed since Chelsea appointed Conte.

Then their only consistent rivals to the title where Manchester City. Since then, Manchester United have re-emerged under Jose Mourinho, Tottenham continue to progress under Mauricio Pochettino, while Jurgen Klopp has turned Liverpool into consistent top-four finishers.

The bar has been raised and it is hard to see Chelsea winning the Premier League or Champions League next season in this new ultra-competitive era.

Whoever comes in must be wary of this. The money isn’t even that good anymore and Conte has lagged behind Mourinho and Pep Guardiola in the pay stakes.

Chelsea no longer splash the cash like United, City or even Liverpool. Yes, they spent big on Alvaro Morata and Tiemoue Bakayoko, but they only replaced Diego Costa and Nemanja Matic.

A street fighter like Diego Simeone would be ideal for Chelsea, someone willing to take on anyone within or without the club.

He has made a career out of upsetting the established order with Atletico Madrid.

His style is hardly pleasing on the eye, but that has rarely been Chelsea’s priority.

Beyond Simeone, Maxi Allegri would not flourish in such a poisoned environment, nor would Maurizio Sarri.

Chelsea could find themselves working off a very short shortlist this summer because of the way they have acted in the past.

Gulliver Burrows - Jody Morris

Jody Morris has worked wonders with the Chelsea U18s so it's now time for him to the call up. He is a proper Chelsea man through and through.

He would have control of the dressing room and would in still a willingness to play for the shirt. He would also do what Abramovich has always wanted and bring young players through.

Get him in with Frank Lampard as the assistant and give them plenty of time.

Also lets not forget Xavi (at one point) called him 'his toughest opponent', name another potential manager with that kind of pedigree, I can wait.
