The day a Mirrorman took on Ray Wilkins - and lost

Viv Anderson is sat opposite me in a pair of white y-fronts.

He’s just had a pre-match shower and is holding court with Clive Allen and Tony Woodcock

They’re exchanging jokes and gossip about fellow players. “What about Kyle Walker?” Viv asks Clive.

I strain to hear his answer but it’s drowned out by a booming Luther Blissett poking fun at Ray Parlour, who’s having trouble squeezing into a pair of extra-large shorts.

Ian Walker, Mark Wright and Alvin Martin are deep in conversation on the other side of the changing room, with Rob Lee, Rob Jones and Nigel Winterburn sitting quietly a short distance away.

They’ve all come together for an outing as ‘England Legends’ and despite no previous experience of managing a football team, I’m in charge.

Like Steve McClaren, I wasn’t first choice. But thoughts of brollies and dodgy Dutch accents won’t get in the way of my big day.

Our opposition is your average Sunday league side, with a difference.

Ivory FC hail from The Only Way Is Essex headquarters, Brentwood, and until six months ago propped up their division.

But they won a competition to be coached by none other than Terry Venables, Ray Wilkins and Ray Clemence and, hey presto, started winning games.

Today’s clash at Dagenham and Redbridge’s ground is the biggest game of their lives – and they’re up for it.

Their large squad arrived at the stadium in excellent time, suited and booted after a morning team-talk at a hotel attended by Terry and the two Rays.

“This is one you’ll want to tell the grandkids about, and especially if we win,” was the essence of the skipper’s speech.

Our preparation couldn’t have been any more different.

For one, I had a bare XI of legends I’d never met before. I turned up late, wearing a hoodie and jogging bottoms. And my backroom staff consisted of my father, who was revelling in his role.

“I’ve got the England manager in the car,” he proudly informed the bloke guarding the car park’s VIP entrance.

With half-an-hour to go until kick-off the atmosphere in our changing room is nice and relaxed.

So relaxed in fact that no-one can be bothered in warm-up.

The main topics of conversation are – who needs a spot of physio and Ray’s ongoing shorts crisis.

Outside, Ivory are putting the finishing touches to their meticulous preparation and going through their drills in front of 400 or so family and friends in the stands.

I just start to mull over the formation when Luther tells me we’re playing 3-4-3.

It’s attacking but I go with it and announce the team, naming Alvin as captain and putting Nigel Winterburn and Rob Jones on the wrong flanks in the process.

I follow it up with a call to arms. “Come on boys, let’s get the ball down and play some football,” I bark. And it appears to go down well as Clive says: “I like that.”

I lead the team out into the tunnel and onto the pitch. There are photographers taking pictures but there’s no cacophony of noise and this isn’t Wembley.

Still, I am an England manager and it feels good, if not a little odd.

I head for my technical area as the boys stretch their legs and bang my head on the roof of the dugout as soon as I get there.

Venables, Wilkins and Clemence are standing shoulder-to-shoulder, wearing expensive clothes and relaxed smiles.

As the whistle goes I realise I have no idea what I’m going to say to players who have a mountain of international caps between them.

But for the first two minutes, nothing needs to be said, because Ivory don’t touch the ball.

And just when we’re playing like Barcelona Mark Wright goes and gives it away at the back and their centre-forward runs through to make it one nil.

Our fortunes then go from bad to worse.

Clive, who was having a terrible game, limps off with a calf strain. Tony Woodcock then hobbles out of the match with Achilles trouble.

Walker keeps us in the game with a string of good saves as Ivory’s younger legs and superior fitness tells.

Our passing is great but with no-one prepared to put in a lung-busting run or two we can’t get it past the halfway line.

Any decent manager would then have changed something or brought a sub on. But I was so caught up in the moment it didn’t cross my mind.

On the stroke of half-time though, a chance. Luther is hacked down in the box and a penalty in awarded.

Mr Reliable Rob Lee, who’s still reeling from a bruising tackle minutes earlier, steps up to take it and slots his pen past the right-hand upright.

The whistle goes and we head back into the changing room.

Two Ivory players are rowing and at one nil, we’re very much still in it, despite not creating much.

Not being the tea cup-throwing kind, I spend the break passing round orange juice and checking on injuries.

Back out on the pitch, Ivory start the second-half still on the front foot.

Walker continues his good work with a fine penalty save, but can do nothing about a fine header from six yards out a few minutes later that hits the back of our net.

At two nil down the wheels are starting to fall off as Ivory go close again. Viv then decides to go up front, without any consultation.

It’s time for a change – I’m putting myself on.

Radical it might be but with 30 minutes to go and defeat looming it’s worth a gamble – and I want my first England cap.

I slot in on the left and with a tiring Winterburn tucking in I’m covering the whole wing.

We get back into the game down the right-hand side a short while later when Viv taps in from close range after a mazy run from our winger.

And despite my efforts to influence the game, I’m blowing too hard to make any impact whatsoever.

We go close several times in the final minutes but can’t find the equaliser. And as the ref blows up Ivory celebrate wildly.

Ray Parlour is rightly named man-of-the-match for a superb display in the middle of the park.

Never a fan of his when he was playing full-time, I have a new found respect having seen him at close quarters. His touch, passing and vision are a class apart.

I have it on good authority that the Essex boys headed for TOWIE’s favourite Brentwood haunt, Sugar Hut, for a knees-up.

I said my goodbyes to the legends – and any (admittedly, slim) chance of inheriting Don Fabio’s tracksuit at the end of the summer.
