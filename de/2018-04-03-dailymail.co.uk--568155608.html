Trump admitted Jared and Ivanka made a mistake by coming to Washington

Ronald Kessler, a former Washington Post and Wall Street Journal investigative reporter, is the New York Times bestselling author of 'The First Family Detail: Secret Service Agents Reveal the Hidden Lives of the Presidents' and 'The Secrets of the FBI.'  

Donald Trump confided to his aides that his daughter and son-in-law had jumped the gun and made a mistake by joining the White House, Steve Bannon has claims in a new book about the inner workings of the White House.

The president said that Jared and Ivanka 'screwed up' and even recognized them as a 'problem,' according to Ronald Kessler's new book The Trump White House: Changing the Rules of the Game, published by Crown Forum.

In the book, Bannon describes to Kessler the behind-the-scenes clashes between White House aides and the first daughter and her husband - who he believes has views 'closer to Hillary Clinton's than to Trump's.'  

'They're nice people, but they don't know anything,' Bannon is quoted in the book.  'If their name wasn't Trump, they would be midlevel marketing managers somewhere.

'They are so opposed to his [Trump's] program that they're there to destroy him.

Donald Trump admitted that Ivanka Trump and son-in-law Jared Kushner had jumped the gun and made a mistake by joining the White House, a new book has claimed 

White House aides have described the couple as 'simply power hungry' and that the two enjoy throwing their weight around 

'This White House was very divided from the beginning, but those divisions were papered over during the campaign because everyone was focused on winning.' 

Jared and Ivanka, or 'Ja-vanka' - as Bannon derisively refers to the couple - took on the roles of senior advisors to the president, but behind closed doors it's been far from smooth sailing. 

Jared and Bannon often butted heads and the two were often 'leaking critical material about each other,' Kessler writes.

Bannon also criticized the president's son-in-law for having a moderate approach in politics, and claimed Jared had advised him 'you've got to make everyone your friend.' 

One aide even claimed the couple had no real grasp of policy and stated that Jared and Ivanka are all about 'puppies and rainbows.'  

As one of his first initiatives as a political aide, Jared established the Office of American Innovation - aimed at upgrading the federal government's information technology- but his efforts were criticized for having a 'ridiculously broad scope.'

Other aides believed that he was over his head with his 'platitudes' about achieving peace in the Middle East, but in reality 'had no real idea what he was doing.'

'Jared is the kind of guy who likes to go to meetings and say, "We need to solve this problem" or "We need peace in the Middle East,"' former press secretary Sean Spicer said.

Bannon considered Ivanka and Jared as liberals whose views were more closely aligned with Hillary Clinton's than Trump's 

Other White House staffers also felt Jared and Ivanka, who both took on unpaid roles, are 'simply power hungry' and enjoy throwing their weight around.

'In the view of many Trump aides, the notion of neophyte Jared Kushner negotiating with Israeli prime minister Benjamin Netanyahu and Palestinian leader Mahmoud Abbas was laughable,' Kessler writes.  

The two were accused of having 'White House-itus', a 'malady' said to take over overly-keen junior staffers in every White House administration.  

In The Trump White House: Changing the Rules of the Game, Ronald Kessler reveals the behind-the-scenes clashes between Jared and Ivanka' and other White House aides

Ivanka and Bannon once butted heads after she insisted she did not have to go through former White House Chief of Staff Reince Preibus to discuss issues with her father, after Bannon had told her she was just a staffer like everyone else.

'Ivanka reminded him that she is the first daughter, above staff.' 

And while Jared was the brains behind Trump's successful trip to Saudi Arabia, the couple has also been blamed for pushing the 'most disastrous and most foolish decisions of Trump's presidency.' 

Trump even told Jared and Ivanka several times that they made a mistake by coming to Washington, Bannon claimed.

'A couple of times, he told them right in front of me in the Oval, "I told you guys you should have stayed in New York, because it's going to be vicious," Bannon said. 

'He wasn't saying that they're useless but that you're hurting yourselves by being here. But I think he was also giving a hint. If they had taken the hint and skedaddled, he'd be fine with it.' 

Bannon also claimed that Trump had previously told the couple to stay in New York before he took office by warning: 'You don't understand how this town is going to kill you. Don't come.'

Once Jared and Ivanka decided to come to Washington despite her father's advice, Trump supported it, a White House aide claimed. 

However, after the first daughter and her husband came under intense scruitny by the media, Trump was quick to say, I told you so

'Trump had told them in front of aides, "Remember I told you not to do this, and you had to do it? Now look.'"'
