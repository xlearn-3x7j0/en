Librarian discovers fascinating secret code bookworms have been using

A woman was left baffled by a 'secret code' discovered in dozens of books at the library she works at.

Georgia Grainger, from Dundee, Scotland, started her role as a librarian at Charleston library recently and soon came across a recurring theme in many of the books.

A customer pointed out that lots of the tomes had page seven underlined, and the books were all wartime romances favoured by elderly female clientele.  

Georgia shared her story on Twitter under the title the Mystery of the Page 7 Vandal, and it quickly went viral.

Georgia tweeted: 'A wee old women came in and said "I've a question. Why does page 7 in all the books I take out have the 7 underlined in pen? It seems odd." 

'"What?" I say, thinking she might be a bit off her rocker. She showed me, and they did.'

Suspicious that the lady may be doing the underlining herself, the librarian began going through all the possible explanations in her head. 

In her next tweet she writes: 'I asked if she was doing it, she said she wasn't and showed me the new book she was getting out that she hadn't even had yet. 

'It also had the 7 underlined! "I don't know, maybe someone really likes page 7?" I said, assuming of course that there is a serial killer in the library.'

And Georgia soon discovered a pattern, noticing that many of the books underlined were wartime romances. 

She continued: 'I checked some other books. Most didn't have it, but a lot in this genre did - they're 'wee old women' books (romances set in wartime Britain etc). 

'Lots of underlined 7s. The woman who pointed it out shrugged and went on her way, "just thought you should know."' 

Jumping to conclusions, Georgia turned to her manager to help her solve the mystery. 

She tweeted: 'My manager came back from doing arts and crafts with some of the kids and I decide to tell her about the serial killer in the library. And that's how I found out that a lot of our elderly clientele have secret codes to mark which books they've read before.' 

Explaining a customer had flagged the odd discovery to her, the librarian described how an elderly lady noted that many books had the number 7 underlined

'So, good news: there's not a serial killer in the library whose MO include the number 7 and wartime romances. Bad news: people are defacing books rather than just asking us to scan them.'

Georgia explained that many of the older customers got used to the method in order to keep track of the books they had already read before computer systems kept a record. 

'Our computers do it automatically but many have been doing it since before that was possible, so Esther might underline page 7, while Anne might draw a little star on the last page, and Fred might put an 'f' on the title page. Then when they pick it up, they can check!' she added.

Revealing her mischievous side, Georgia added: 'It's quite clever really but now I'm dying to just underline page 7 of every new wee old women book we get in.'

Her story quickly went viral, garnering 21,000 favourites, 7,6000 retweets and hundred of replies with people sharing their own stories.

A former librarian admitted it was a common practice in her library, and admitted she liked the character it added to the books.

'Most of them used to do it in pencil (ex-librarian here). Also back in the days of properly stamping the books, some of them would underline/circle/highlight the date. I like it - but I think that books shouldn't be pristine,' she tweeted.

But another pointed out there was a possible fault with the system, adding: 'That also only works for that particular copy of the book, but if you have more than one copy of the book the wee old ladies will end up reading the same book twice'.
