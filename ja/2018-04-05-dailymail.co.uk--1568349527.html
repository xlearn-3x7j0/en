Woman's crippling anxiety has left her living like a 'hermit'

A woman's crippling anxiety has left her living like a 'hermit'.

Barbara Cooper, 88, from Horsham, West Sussex, also battlies severe loneliness as her mental-health disorder means she lacks the confidence to leave the house.

After undergoing multiple scans and tests, Ms Cooper, who lives alone and has no children, feels abandoned by the NHS who she says refuses to take her condition seriously as doctors can find no evidence she is suffering.

Ms Cooper, who said she did not have time to get married while she cared for her arthritis-suffering mother, told MailOnline: 'I’ve thought to myself, "if I wake up another day feeling like this, I might as well be dead". I am like a hermit with no life.

'I’m really desperate; I need someone to help me.'

Ms Cooper is convinced her symptoms, such as abdominal pain, demonstrate her anxiety is physically led and should therefore be treated with painkillers.

Research released in September last year suggested mental-health disorders are caused by widespread inflammation, which implies they are physical conditions, according to Cambridge University scientists.

A study released last month also suggested loneliness is a health risk after it emerged a campaign to combat isolation in Frome, Somerset, has led to a dramatic reduction in emergency hospital admissions.

Anxiety affects more than eight million people every year in the UK. Previous research suggests around 85 per cent of elderly people receive no help from the NHS for mental-health problems.

ARE MENTAL-HEALTH CONDITIONS PHYSICAL ILLNESSES?

Depression is a physical illness that could be treated with anti-inflammatory drugs, a Cambridge University professor stated in September 2017.

An overactive immune system may trigger the mental-health condition by causing widespread inflammation that leads to feelings of hopelessness and unhappiness, the expert believes.

The immune system may fail to 'switch off' after an illness or traumatic event, he adds.

Previous research suggests people who suffer severe emotional trauma have signs of inflammation, which implies their immune systems are constantly 'fired-up'.

Until recently, such a process was denied as a cause of depression as scientists believed the brain and the immune system operated separately. 

Yet, recent studies suggest a link between nerves in the brain and immune function. 

Professor Ed Bullmore, head of the department of psychiatry at the University of Cambridge, said: 'In relation to mood, beyond reasonable doubt, there is a very robust association between inflammation and depressive symptoms.

'In experimental medicine studies if you treat a healthy individual with an inflammatory drug, like interferon, a substantial percentage of those people will become depressed,' The Telegraph reported. 

Previous figures suggest around 60 per cent of people who visit a doctor with chest pain are actually suffering from anxiety.

Professor Sir Robert Lechler, president of the Academy of Medical Sciences, said: 'You’re not just a little bit miserable if you’ve got a long-term condition, there is a real mechanistic connection between the mind, the nervous system and the immune system.' 

Experts add the outdated approach of separating mental and physical health conditions is holding back medical advances. 

'Anxiety is physical'

Ms Cooper, who has previously taken a cocktail of medications to help ease her anxiety, is growing increasingly frustrated at doctors who tell the former journalist her brain scans suggest no signs of a problem.

She adds the fact that anti-anxiety medications have never had any effect proves the physical nature of her illness.

Ms Cooper, who also experiences swollen hands alongside her anxious thoughts, said: 'My brain is fine but something is affecting me.

'Anxiety is physical and should be treated with painkillers.'   

The only medication Ms Cooper is currently on is the anti-insomnia drug zopiclone, which she believes has been inappropriately prescribed given its warning not to be used in mental-health disorder sufferers.

Anxiety triggered extreme loneliness

Ms Cooper's anxiety has prevented her from making any social ties after being convinced to move out of London last year by her nephew, who lives half an hour away.

Although her nephew, who she rarely sees, persuaded her that being close to family would help ease her anxiety, Ms Cooper feels abandoned in a small town where she knows nobody.

She said: 'I honestly feel that if I could get something that sorts the physical symptoms of my anxiety, then I can socialise and it will overcome my loneliness.'

Chance encounter set off the disorder

Looking back Ms Cooper believes she always struggled with anxiety to some extent, which she once found motivated her to succeed.

Yet, her mental-health disorder became severe after being persuaded to go into business with a man she met during a chance encounter.

Ms Cooper said: 'I had a successful little publishing business in Fulham. I was energetic and full of beans. 

'Somebody appeared interested in one of the books [I was publishing]. I talked to him and was persuaded to go into partnership with him.'

Ms Cooper claims he stole her publishing ideas, triggering her deep battle with anxiety. 

She said: 'Through indifference and inflated ego he made working so unpleasant I couldn’t take it anymore.

'It was the worst thing that ever happened to me.'

NHS England has been approached for comment.
