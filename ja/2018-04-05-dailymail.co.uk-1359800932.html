12ft pits discovered in Switzerland helped Romans keep their food cool

Before the invention of the refrigerator, Romans stored their food in underground chambers filled with ice and snow, scientists say.

Experts believe that a series of pits, some as deep as 12 feet (four metres), found under a town in Switzerland were used to stop large amounts of food from spoiling.

Archaeologists are attempting to replicate the success of the ancient culture by filling the subterranean shafts with compacted ice for a year.

A team of researchers, lead by Peter-Andrew Schwarz from the University of Basel, is conducting the experiments at Augusta Raurica.

Although it is believed that the pits were adept at keeping cold food throughout the year, the exact way people did it has been lost to the annals of time.

Dr Shwarz and his team are undertaking a third attempt to successfully maintain refrigeration for 365 days, having recruited some outside professional help.

It comes from the Balaeric island of Majorca, where ice-makers, or nevaters, used a specific technique to keep food cool during the hot summer months.  

'With this method, people in Majorca could keep food cool in summer before the arrival of electric fridges,' Dr Schwarz said in 2017.

Their strategy involves using layers of compacted ice and straw to maintain pockets of cold air, therefore insulating the pit and keeping it cooler for longer. 

The team will assess the success of the process in August.  

First discovered in 2013, the archaeological site near the Swiss city of Basel is home to the pits, which are believed to be the fridges of the first century.

The region was home to a Roman colony that was founded in 15 BC. Due to strong trade links, it soon flourished to become a hotspot for activity in the region.

The bustling hub was home to 20,000 people but was abandoned by 300AD after war, famine and disease decimated the population. It remains one of the most well-preserved Roman sites in the entire world. 

Two previous experiments to try and sustain ice over the period of several months failed.

In the first attempt, the researchers filled the deep shafts with snow and ice in one go. This proved unsuccessful and temperatures rose above freezing, even in the winter months.

The second attempt was more successful where the researchers gradually filled the shafts with ice and snow. 

In this instance, the snow stuck around until June, a marked success but an inevitable disaster in ancient times when all food reserves would have been ruined. 

HOW DID ROMANS STORE THEIR FOOD?

During the Roman era, there were certain ways that people preserved food to make it last longer. 

Food was made to last longer using honey and salt as a preservative, which greatly increased the time before it spoiled. 

Smoking was also used in European cultures of the time, enabling our ancestors to produce sausage, bacon and ham.

Romans also knew how to pickle in vinegar, boil in brine and dry fruit too. 

All of these techniques were used to make fresh food last longer.

As well as these treatments, storage was improved, including vast stores that were built to keep grain and cereals in.

Classic storage containers were barrels, amphorae and clay pots, as well as grain silos and warehouses. 

Wealthy Romans also had large storage cellars in their villas, where wine and oil amphorae were buried in sand.

A stone table with a high, smooth, base was used to store fruit during the winter. The design of the table meant that no pests could reach the food.

Romans in affluent households used snow to keep their wine and food cold on hot days. 

Snow from mountains in Lebanon, Syria and Armenia was imported on camels, buried in pits in the ground and then covered with manure and branches.

In some regions, towns in the Alps for example, used local snow and ice as well as deep pits to build huge refrigerators.
