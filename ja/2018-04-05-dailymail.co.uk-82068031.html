'Bipolar' man's family accuse police of killing him without warning

Grieving family members of a 34-year-old Brooklyn man who was shot dead by police officers on Wednesday are speaking out, accusing the NYPD of firing on the mentally ill victim 10 times without a warning.

Police said Saheed Vassell, a Jamaican-born welder with a 15-year-old son, was killed shortly before 5pm in the Crown Heights neighborhood of Brooklyn after officers mistook a metal pipe he was holding for a gun.

‘They have witnesses around here that say they did not tell him to drop anything,’ his aunt Nora Ford said. ‘They said the police guy just pull around, and started shooting at him, and I'm very, very upset.’

NYPD officers responded to reports of a man wielding what appeared to be a silver gun in Brooklyn on Wednesday. This surveillance image shows Saheed Vassell, 34, pointing what turned out to be a metal pipe at a bystander on the street  

'No warning': Vassell's aunt, Nora Ford (left), and eyewitness Jesus Santiago (right), both said police officers opened fire on Saheed without a warning 

Local resident Jesus Santiago told the station PIX11 he was standing 5 feet away from the officers at the time of the shooting, and claimed that none of them ever told Vassell to drop the pipe.

Another witness, Jaccbot Hinds, recounted for the New York Daily News how the officers jumped out of their patrol car and opened fire on Vassell without a word.

‘It’s almost like they did a hit. They didn’t say please. They didn’t say put your hands up, nothing,’ Hinds said.

The NYPD would not say whether or not the officers ordered Vassell to drop the object before shooting him.

New York Attorney General Eric Schneiderman this morning announced that he is investigating the deadly officer-involved shooting of the unarmed black man.

Schneiderman said his office is committed to ‘an independent, comprehensive and fair investigation.’

Saheed's father, Eric Vassell, said his son was bipolar but had not taken medication for years.

‘Police had a choice. They always have a choice,’ he told ABC 7 New York. ‘They should not train them to kill. They should train them to protect life, to save life.’

Grieving dad: Eric Vassell (left) said his son Saheed (right) was bipolar but had not taken medication in years 

Police said no gun was recovered from the scene and officers later determined that the man had been holding this metal pipe pictured above

NYPD officers responded to several 911 calls at 4.40pm on Wednesday reporting that a man in a leather jacket was pointing what appeared to be a silver gun at bystanders in Crown Heights. 

Five officers arrived on the scene at the intersection of Montgomery Street and Utica Avenue when Vassell 'took a two-hand shooting stance' and pointed a metal object at them, NYPD Chief of Department Terence Monahan said.

The officers fired off 10 rounds between them and struck the man at least once.

He was rushed to Kings County Hospital, where he was pronounced dead.

Police said no gun was recovered from the scene and officers determined that the man had been holding a 'metal pipe with a knob at the end of it'.

Security cameras from the intersection showed Vassell appearing to point a weapon at the officers, Monahan said. 

Another surveillance image showed Vassell pointing the metal pipe at a person on the street.  

None of the officers were wearing body cameras at the time.

The shooting sparked anger from residents in the neighborhood who described the victim as a quirky character who wasn't known to be violent

The shooting sparked anger from residents in the neighborhood who described the victim as a quirky character who was known to be mentally ill but harmless, the New York Daily News reported.

'He's a little touched in the head. But he's not crazy enough to go around hitting people,' a witness who knew Vassell said.

Another resident said: 'All he did was just walk around the neighborhood. He speaks to himself, usually he has an orange Bible or a rosary in his hand. He never had a problem with anyone.

'The officers from the neighborhood, they know him. He has no issue with violence. Everybody just knows he's just mentally challenged. This shouldn't have happened at all.' 

Vassell's teenage son, Tyshawn, described him as a good father who was always there for him.
