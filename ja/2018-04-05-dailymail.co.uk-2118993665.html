Former footballers share their amazing stories of Ray Wilkins

The world of football united to pay tribute to Ray Wilkins on Wednesday after the former England captain passed away in hospital at the age of 61.

He enjoyed tremendous success in a playing career that spanned three decades, but it was his time as a manager and coach where he inspired so many notable names of the next generation.

Here, Sportsmail looks at five touching tributes from former stars lucky enough to have worked with a true legend of the English game.

Ray Wilkins built legacies at Chelsea and QPR during his playing and coaching career

Nigel Quashie

Writing on Instagram, Quashie retells the story of the moment his footballing career began under Wilkins at Queens Park Rangers. The former Scotland international would go onto play over 400 times in a career spanning 18 years. 

'I will always be proud to say how lucky I am to have been coached, managed and even meet you. If it wasn't for you I would never have started my career playing football and will never forget going to Manchester United as a YTS trainee to do all the kit and boots in the changing room for the first team squad. 

'One hour 30 mins before kick off you name the team and I was in it and that's how my debut went. I didn't even get to think about it because all you said was 'you're No 18, here are your boots and shin pads, just go and play, have fun and treat it like your over the park with your mates'. 

'My mum never had a mobile phone back then because we couldn't afford anything like that and everything went through her work or home phone. I asked you after the game if I could call home to speak to her, you said 'use my mobile and call this number'. 

'It was my mum on the other end of the line and she said she was outside by the away team bus. I went outside to see her and asked how did you get here because I knew she couldn't afford it. 

'She told me Ray had got the club to pay for her train ticket to come to the game and then told me Ray got the club to give us our first mobile phone in life during the week and he wanted to surprise me knowing I was making my debut. 

'I was given a chance by Ray Wilkins one of the legends of the game and to this day you are the first person I mention when it comes to football and that will never change. You will be missed so much not just in the football world but as a real human being with such class. Thanks for everything and my thoughts go to all the family. Sleep well Sir Ray Wilkins'

Jody Morris

Morris is now a coach in Chelsea's academy but enjoyed a fruitful career with the Blues where he was mentored by Wilkins. He tells a tale of the moment Wilkins reached out to make him feel better during an injury.

'I'd been out injured for a long time and Razor bursts into the treatment room while mid-training session and says "come on fella let's get you involved...you can be ref as I've had enough of those herberts moaning". 

'Typical of Ray to know I hated missing training, and being out a while you can feel isolated. I came out and took his whistle and he took his watch off and put it on my wrist so my timing was impeccable and I ref'd the game with aplomb. 

'I'd been used to seeing Ray with Rolexes but as I was taking the watch off to give it back I noticed it was was an Omega. Me being an uneducated "young pup" (as Ray would say) and wanting to have a bit of banter with him I cheekily said "Omega? Come on Razor I expected better from you".

'Ray with his face resembling that "I'm about to take you to school" look says "why don't you just have a look at the back of the timepiece before handing it here fella".

'So I flipped it over and it had a big AC Milan badge engraved with 'FRANCO BARESI TESTIMONIAL' and the date. He just winked at me and walked off chuckling to himself...loved him!'

Ian Holloway

At Queens Park Rangers, Ian Holloway was coming through the ranks while Wilkins was an established star. Speaking to talkSPORT, Holloway talks of the time Wilkins took to teach him how to be a better player. 

'We played this game [in QPR training], I chased him everywhere and I couldn't get near him.

'When we came off, he put his arm around me and said: "Son, you're a wonderful fella, keep your chin up, keep going and you can learn how to do that as well." 

'He taught me how to do it.'

'As a teammate, the belief he gave me was something else and he gave it to all of us,' he added.

'The world is a much sadder place without him.'

Joe Cole

Joe Cole was another player to benefit from the wisdom of Wilkins during his days as a coach at Chelsea. Writing on Instagram, the former England international regales the good times they had in training.

'I would come out to training 20 minutes before we would start and play the boxes game and two touch every day, we had such a giggle.

'I loved you telling me about Franco Barresi and all the greats you have played with and against and reminiscing about the “good old days”. 

'Loved our daily chats and arguments about football and will always remember you saying: “You young chappies should never give the ball away, these days you play on bowling greens, we played underneath the grass young man”.

'But most of all, mate, I will miss bumping into you and catching up and I always left your company with a smile on my face and a spring in my step.

'You will always be “dangerously well“. They don’t make them like you anymore Raymond.'

Paul Gascoigne

Gascoigne remembers the daunting task of replacing Wilkins in the England squad, with the latter retiring as the former emerged. Speaking on talkSPORT, he reveals how Wilkins influenced the way he would go on to play the game. 

'I remember years ago I was playing against him. It was all about who should be picked for England - Gazza or Ray Wilkins?

'I got myself right up for the game but I swear I didn't get a touch.

'I couldn't get the ball ofF him. I learned how to shield the ball because when you got close to him he'd stick his backside out and you couldn't get close.

'Whenever I spoke to him he'd always say "never give the ball away, treat the ball like a diamond". I worked on that and that was one of my main points.

'Then at Rangers, when you see someone like Ray Wilkins go to Glasgow Rangers, the fans are incredible and he was worshiped there.

'So to take his place then the pressure was on already.'
