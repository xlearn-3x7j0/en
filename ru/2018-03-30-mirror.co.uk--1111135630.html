The talking is almost over as Anthony Joshua and Joseph Parker prepare to trade leather in front of 80,000 fans in Cardiff.

The pair will put their respective world titles on the line in Cardiff on Saturday night in a clash of two unbeaten heavyweights.

But first the formalities.

Joshua and Parker stepped on to the scales in the Welsh capital on Friday afternoon before staring each other down in front of their adoring fans.

The Brit - who holds the WBA and IBF belts - came in a stone lighter than he did against Carlos Takam last year, while Parker, the WBO title holder, also looked slimmer as he tipped the scales six pounds under Joshua's limit.

12:13

Parker on debt he owes boxing-mad dad

With a dad called Dempsey, Joseph Parker was always going to be a boxer.

Some of Parker’s earliest memories are of his father holding up his hands for him to punch.

Boxing-mad Dempsey, who moved to New Zealand with his wife Sala from Samoa in the 1980s, trained Joseph and his younger brother John, also now a professional boxer, at their South Auckland home.

Dempsey took a chubby Joseph to his first club at age 10, and he and Sala held down three jobs between them to fund his amateur career.

His parents built a gym for him at home because Sala felt sorry for him training in the wet and cold. They also gave him a kick up the backside when needed — Sala rollocking him for skipping training to indulge in his favourite hobby of fishing.

Joshua is softer than Parker, claims Kiwi's promoter

Joseph Parker’s promoter has taunted Anthony Joshua , claiming he is “softer” than the Kiwi he faces on Saturday because he’s had it easy in his career.

David Higgins says Britain’s world heavyweight champ has had the red-carpet treatment since joining the GB Boxing programme as an amateur in 2010.

Joshua was fully funded with lottery money before he turned pro in 2013, whereas Parker received no state help.

“New Zealand doesn’t have a boxing heritage and there’s no government funding of boxing, so we’ve done it on the smell of an oily rag,” said Higgins.

“Joseph didn’t win any medals. He started with nothing and no name and we have scrapped, hustled and fought our way to the top of the world and that gives us real confidence and an in-built strength.

“Joshua, on the other hand... credit to him for turning his life around, but British boxing gives their fighters a lot of support, a lot of sponsorship. Then he won Olympic gold, and it’s all easy.

“Therefore I think he may be softer.”
