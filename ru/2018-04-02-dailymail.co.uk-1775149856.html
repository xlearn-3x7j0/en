Can I claim a lump sum from my wife's pension in our divorce?

My wife and I are to be divorced, with the decree nisi in March 2018. I'm 72 and on state pension, plus a monthly private pension of £53.40 per month and an even smaller pension that pays just £90 per year.

My wife is 20 years younger than me and she is employed by the NHS as a nursing manager.

As part of the divorce settlement, I might be getting part of her pension. The matrimonial home belongs to her mother, and my wife still lives there. I had to move out almost 12 months ago now.

I'm in a housing association rented bungalow and have had to endure a lot of hardship since she said the marriage was over.

I've had to increase my overdraft over and over again to furnish my bungalow and build a new life for myself.

I have no savings. All my money has gone. She's had most of it. If I'm entitled to part of her pension could I take it as a lump sum to help my financial situation or will I have to wait and see what the court decides?

Zahra Pabani, partner and divorce specialist at law firm Shakespeare Martineau, replies: ‘Need’ trumps everything when a divorce is concerned.

When the courts are considering a divorce, need supersedes all other factors when deciding on the division of marital assets, particularly in small and medium wealth cases.

This means that age, financial stability, condition of health and housing provisions determine who gets what when divorce proceedings are issued.

When starting proceedings, the court begins with attempting to split the assets 50:50 in anything other than a short marriage.

This involves confirming that the needs of both parties are met and includes ensuring that each person has somewhere to live and money to live on.

For example, if one party’s basic needs – such as having a roof over their head or enough money to live on – are not met, then such settlements should be challenged. 

Pensions: What can you claim?

With regards to the division of assets, ‘what’s mine is yours and what’s yours is mine’ certainly rings true and this even incorporates pension provisions.

Many people believe that a pension scheme is private to the individual who holds it, but when a divorce is concerned, the courts assume that such assets are also ‘jointly’ owned.

In your case, this means that if your basic needs are not met through the proposed divorce settlement, you could be entitled to some of your wife’s pension too.

Pension structures can be extremely complex. Seeking advice from a pension actuary would be beneficial. An actuary can be found from a simple online search, but it is always best practice to explore a few advisers before committing to one.

On appointing an actuarial specialist, they will prepare a report for you based on what the pension provision looks like for you and your wife. This report costs in the region of £1,000-£1,500.

The courts will consider whether to equalise the income or capital from the pension across both parties.

You seem to be in a financially vulnerable income position and claiming a proportion of your wife’s pension would go some way to satisfying your basic needs.

There are a number of other factors to consider, such as both your ages and also the type of pension scheme in question.

First of all, gaining full and frank disclosure of the pension is crucial in order to identify the financial position of both sides.

This form of disclosure can be done on a voluntary basis and it is the ‘cash equivalent value’ of the pension that is required. If your wife refuses, you can issue court proceedings to compel her to disclose her pension information.

Although it makes no difference that you are retired, and your wife is not, an actuary's calculations make certain assumptions, such as that men have a lower life expectancy than women.

This means that when calculating how much of the pension income each party is entitled to, your wife will have a much larger period for her pension to last and therefore will need enough income to cover that time.

Should you get a proportion of your wife’s pension, you would potentially be entitled to claim 25 per cent of it as a tax-free lump sum.

What is more important is the timeframe. If you reached the decree nisi in March, then a decree absolute can be issued in six weeks and one day exactly.

You should not approve the decree absolute until the finances are resolved whether by agreement or by the order of the court.

This means you should take action quickly. Usually, pension funds are split by a Pension Sharing Order, which takes effect when the couple’s divorce is finalised. 

Property: What can you claim?

Considering your situation, you could also make a claim on your soon-to-be ex-mother-in-law’s property.

If you used to live in the property, and contributed to it - such as through payments or improvements - and if there were promises of both you and your wife inheriting the property eventually, then you could also be entitled to a share.

Although your wife can ringfence her inheritance, if it is required to cater for your basic needs, then a claim can be brought.

Maintenance: What can you claim?

Unfortunately, the divorce system is inherently sexist and it is very unusual for a man to receive spousal maintenance payments. However, in this case, there may be calls for this as well.

As your wife is clearly working and you are not, she is in a more financially-secure position and equalising the income through ongoing monthly payments could be an option. 

What should you do now?

You have a potential claim not only on your wife’s pension, but also the house, and for ongoing spousal maintenance payments.

You must act quickly, get good legal advice if you haven't already, and seek full and frank financial disclosure of all your spouse's income and assets, to ensure you get the most favourable and fair settlement.
