Stunning portable holiday cabin that cost just £20,000 to build

This is the stunning portable holiday cabin that has been designed and constructed for just £20,000 and made from recycled materials.

Incredible images show the wooden house illuminated in the twilight as an orange glow shines through the large windows.

While other pictures show the spacious interior with an open plan room taking up the whole ground floor while a ladder leads to a second living space.

The stunning portable holiday cabin which is currently located in the woods in Bath and cost just £20,000 to design and construct

The self-built prototype, called Trailer, is located in Bath and is the work of architects Invisible Studio.

They explained: 'This building is designed to be able to be legally transported on a public highway and used as permanent or temporary accommodation.

'It has a removable wheeled 'bogey' that slides out from under the steel chassis when not being moved.

'The trailer was driven to site, the bogey removed, and then the bogey used to transport all of the timber frames (which were prefabricated in a workshop) to site.

The cabin is fully portable and has been designed to be transported on public roads so owners can pitch up their cabin wherever they like 

The trailer has been made from scavenged insulation, doors that were found in a skip and roof lights that were trade 'seconds' 

'Externally, the trailer is clad in corrugated fibreglass and steel, and internally lined in used but cleaned shuttering ply. All of the joinery is from plywood off cuts, including the two staircases.

'High levels of natural light are provided by both gable ends which are 'glazed' with high performance interlocking polycarbonate.

'The building is insulated with scavenged insulation, the doors were sourced from a skip, and the roof lights were 'damaged' and thus trade 'seconds'.'

The spacious interior with an open plan room taking up the whole ground floor while a ladder leads to a second living space

A ladder allows owners to reach the second floor of the cabin, which has large windows giving a light and airy feel 

The architects said they wanted to create a super low cost, versatile and usable space that could be transported 

To reduce milling costs, the architects used timber of the 'same section,' meaning it was cut into 125 millimetre-by-50-millimetre pieces and then laminated up into the structural sections for the cross frames.

The architects added: 'The project aims to provide a super low cost, versatile, usable space that could act as a kit of parts for any self builder to improvise around or easily adapt.

'While conceived as a domestic space, it could easily function as a work space or something else.'
