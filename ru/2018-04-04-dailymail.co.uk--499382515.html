Von Miller accused of killing a 9.5ft hammerhead shark in Florida

Denver Broncos linebacker Von Miller is known to get after the quarterback, but animal rights group are now looking to sack him.

The Super Bowl-winning All Pro is the subject of an investigation by Florida’s Fish and Wildlife Conservation Commission after he posted a video on social media catching and then releasing a hammerhead shark in the waters off Miami.

Miller was the subject of a complaint by the People for the Ethical Treatment of Animals after he posted the video last week, according to TMZ Sports.

He and crew members of a boat are seen in the clip reeling in the hammerhead shark.

Interception! Miller and a crew were fishing off the waters of Miami last week when they came upon a big catch

A crew member with Miller is seen reaching down as the hammerhead shark is caught

Miller and his crew then pull the fish into the boat. It is a massive animal that spans over 9 feet in length

After a few moments in which they admire the shark and pose for pictures with it, Miller and his crew release it back into the water.

Now state authorities want to investigate his handling of the shark.

‘The FWC has received the images and video and is looking into whether or not a violation occurred in this incident,’ commission spokesperson Carol Lyn Parrish said.

Florida law prohibits the capture of hammerhead sharks off its coast, according to the FWC.

Animal rights groups were outraged. PETA filed a complaint with state authorities alleging that Miller (seen on January 28 at the Pro Bowl in Orlando) broke the law by not releasing the shark immediately

Hammerheads (like the one seen in the above stock image) as well as tiger shark are endangered species and are thus given special legal protection in Florida waters

If someone fishing does happen to catch a shark, it must be released back into the water immediately.

Pausing to take a photo with a captured shark and then releasing it is not considered 'immediate' and hence is illegal. 

Hammerheads as well as tiger shark are endangered species and are thus given special legal protection in Florida waters.

These animals are captured normally for their fins, which are used in shark soup.

PETA was outraged by the footage. They say Miller killed the 9.5-foot animal. 

If Miller is found to have broken the law protecting hammerhead sharks, he could possibly face a second-degree misdemeanor charge.

The maximum penalty for a conviction would be 60 days in jail and a fine of $500.
