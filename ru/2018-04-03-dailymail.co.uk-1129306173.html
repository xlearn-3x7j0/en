'Keep fighting. We got your back!' What Trump told Pruitt on the phone

President Trump called embattled Environmental Protection Agency Administrator Scott Pruitt on Monday night and indicated he still supported him. 

'Keep you head up. Keep fighting. We got your back,' Pruitt was told by the president, an administration official told CNN. 

But on Monday night, the Washington Post reported that aides to Pruitt had looked into leasing a private jet on a month-to-month basis to fit his travel needs. 

Then on Tuesday the Atlantic wrote that Pruitt had gone around the White House to dole out big raises to two of his favorite aides. 

And the EPA hasn't answered fully how Pruitt got his sweetheart rental deal from an energy lobbyist's wife for a condo unit just steps from the U.S. Capitol. 

At a working lunch with Baltic leaders, Trump seemingly reaffirmed his support for Pruitt telling reporters, 'I hope he's going to be great.' 

Environmental Protection Agency Administrator got a call from President Trump indicating he still had the president's support, but scandals continue to swirl around the official  

Pruitt kind of got off the hook Monday for the real estate deal, with an agency ethics official saying his Capitol Hill condo lease, tied to a prominent fossil-fuels lobbyist, didn't violated federal ethics rules.   

A memo signed by Kevin Minoli contends that Pruitt's $50-a-night rental payments constitute a fair market rate, as the administrator would pay about $1,500 a month. 

But Pruitt actually paid a total of $6,100 over the six month period he leased the property, an average of about $1,000 a month. 

Pruitt's college-aged daughter stayed in the second bedroom while interning at the White House last summer. 

Two, two-bedroom apartments similar to the one Pruitt rented costed $3,750 a month and $4,740 a month.

Minoli's was dated March 30, the day after ABC News first reported Pruitt lived last year in condo co-owned by the wife of Steven Hart, a registered lobbyist whose firm pushed EPA to relax pollution regulations.

An ethics official for the Environmental Protection Agency wrote a memo that said EPA chief Scott Pruitt's $50-a-night Capitol Hill condo rental was fair 

EPA Administrator Scott Pruitt rented out one bedroom in this apartment unit last year. It belongs to the wife of a prominent energy lobbyist  

The apartment was the scene for one of the most bizarre episodes of the administrator's tenure: in which his detail broke down the front door and called 911 claiming he was unresponsive. He was found inside, waking up from a nap 

Pruitt's lease on the Capitol Hill unit required him to only pay for the nights he occupied the apartment 

'The lease authorized use by the administrator and his immediate family, specifically including his spouse and children, and consistent with that provision of the lease his immediate family did stay there when they were in Washington, D.C.,' Minoli wrote. 'The lease did not require payment when the property was not utilized. Neither of these two provisions render the rental cost under the lease as something other than market value.'

EPA did not provide a copy of Pruitt's lease and did not respond to questions about how Minoli determined what he considered to be the fair-market rate for the neighborhood.  

Left unanswered was how Pruitt came to rent a property tied to a lobbyist with business interests involving his agency.

Additionally, ABC News reported that Vicki Hart, the lobbyist's wife who co-owns the Capitol Hill property, was given $2,460 from the EPA for a broken-down door at the apartment, caused by Pruitt's security detail trying to get in. 

The EPA administrator was unconscious and unresponsive, his security team believed, and so they asked a Capitol Police officer to call 911. 

The detail then broke down Vicki Hart's door, only to find a groggy Pruitt getting up from a nap, ABC News found.  

The EPA chief declined medical attention, and a police report was never filed, with the EPA spokesman not responding to ABC's request for information about the incident or the payment made to Vicki Hart.  

Pruitt's condo arrangement has attracted attention in Washington, where Pruitt has been under increasing scrutiny for this frequent taxpayer-funded travel, which has included first-class airline tickets.  

Though federal regulations typically require federal officials to fly in coach, the EPA chief has said he needed to sit in premium seats due to security concerns.

In the Post's new report, Pruitt's aides were found to have contacted NetJets, a well-known company that rents private jets. 

They received cost estimates of roughly $100,000 a month, and so decided against that plan. 

The discussion with NetJets occurred before ex-Health and Human Services Secretary Tom Price resigned over his private jet use, the Post found.  

'This is not news,' Jahan Wilcox, the EPA's spokesman, told the Post on Monday. 'EPS's [chief financial officer] regularly receives solicitations for this type of travel, we did our due diligence, found it was not as cost efficient and continued to fly commercial.' 

Since August, Pruitt's travel has cost taxpayers at least $68,000, according to documents given to House Oversight Committee Chairman Trey Gowdy. 

The Washington Post had previously reported that Pruitt's June trip to Italy cost the agency at least $120,000. 

On the trip he got a tour of the Vatican and tweeted a picture of himself making pasta with Italy's environmental minister.   

The Atlantic found that Pruitt had requested through the White House's Presidential Personnel Office that two of his aides, Sarah Greenwalt and Millan Hupp, receive substantial pay raises.   

The PPO dismissed Pruitt's application for the pay hikes, so he found a clause in the Safe Drinking Water Act that allows the EPA administrator to hire up to 30 people without White House or Congressional approval. 

Pruitt re-hired his two staffers under this provision to grant them the raises, the Atlantic found. 

'This whole thing has completely gutted any morale I had left to put up with this place,' an unidentified staffer told the publication.
