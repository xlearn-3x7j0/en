Carer reveals her life was saved by a visit to Specsavers!

A carer who thought she needed new contact lenses has told how a routine eye test saved her life, after her optician spotted a golf ball-sized brain tumour.

Konnie Rowe, of Burnham on Sea, Somerset, booked a check-up at Specsavers after experiencing headaches and struggling to watch TV.

The 22-year-old expected to be told she needed stronger lenses - but her optician found a lump above her right eye and advised her to speak to her GP.

Her doctor was concerned and booked her in for an emergency MRI scan, which discovered a cystic lesion - tumour - on her brain.

Surgery removed the mass but Miss Rowe has been left partially sighted in her right eye and experiences numbness on the left side of her face.

However, she remains eternally grateful for the eye test, which she had in October 2015, which she feels saved her life.

Miss Rowe said: 'Getting an eye test literally saved my life. The tumour could have caused so much more damage if it had been left there.

'I could have had more vision loss, or I could have been paralysed, or it could have caused speech difficulties. 

'If it had been left there for a long time, it could have killed me. I’m so thankful to Specsavers, where I had the test and to my optician, Rama Venkat.

'I think this shows how important regular eye-checks are.'  

Miss Rowe, speaking about her eye test, said: 'I knew I was due a check-up and expected just to come away with different specs.

'But the optician said there was something causing swelling at the back of my right eye. 

'He did say it just happens to some people and sometimes it's nothing. I was a little concerned, but I thought it would be okay.'

Miss Rowe's headaches intensified after she was advised to speak to her GP because she was waiting for her hospital appointment.

She spent most of Christmas 2015 bedbound. Miss Rowe added: 'I couldn't get out of bed, I was in so much pain. I didn't want to do anything.

Booking a GP appointment straight after the holidays, her doctor arranged an emergency MRI for her at Musgrove Park Hospital in Taunton, Somerset.

After a day of tests, including an MRI and EEG brain test, she was diagnosed with a cystic lesion - a tumour - on her brain.

She said: 'I was in shock. I was there with my grandparents Denise and Robert Machin and they were really upset.

'I've always had a positive attitude and just said, "Let's just take each step as it comes and see what happens with it." I trusted the doctors to treat me and hoped it would be ok.”

Miss Rowe was told she needed surgery to remove the tumour, which was causing pressure in her brain. 

And she was prescribed strong steroids, to arrest its growth, while she went home to rest.

But the drugs had the unpleasant side effect of making her gain 3st (42lbs) in weight.

'It was hard,' Miss Rowe said. 'I tried to remain positive, but I just wanted it to be over.' 

Finally, on February 19, 2016, she had a six-hour operation at Bristol's Southmead Hospital.

Miss Rowe explained: 'It went really well and they believe they removed everything.

'The tumour was inside a fluid sack, which meant it couldn't attach itself to any of the brain tissue, making it really easy to remove. They had to leave the sack but they got the whole tumour.

'Then, a week later, they told me that it was benign and that I wouldn’t need any after treatments, which was fantastic news.' 

Miss Rowe was released to convalesce at home after just four days in hospital, having been warned it would take up to a year to fully recover.

But she took up running and six months after her operation, she ran the Race For Life, in aid of Cancer Research UK, with her mother Kelly Parkhouse and grandma.

Following her own excellent treatment, Miss Rowe, who was working as a waitress, was also inspired to become a carer in a dementia unit.

She said: 'I’ve always wanted to do something to help people, but I didn’t do well enough in my exams to be a midwife. 

'I was talking to one of the nurses while I was in hospital and she suggested I should become a care assistant.

'I felt like I wanted to get better to do that and I absolutely love it. I want to work my way up to a supervisor and it’s made me feel like I have something to work for in the future. I work in a dementia unit and it’s challenging but I love my job.'

Miss Rowe’s vision in her right eye has been affected by the tumour, which has also left her with some numbness down the left side of her face.

Burnham-on-Sea Specsavers optometrist Mr Venkat remembers Miss Rowe coming into Specsavers, thinking she just needed a new prescription.

He continued: 'I carried out a routine eye test but as soon as I saw the results from her visual field test I knew it was something more serious.

'The fundus image of the back of her eye confirmed my suspicions and I advised her to seek further medical advice. 

'There were several possibilities of what the diagnosis could be which I talked her through, including fluid on the brain and potentially a brain tumour.

'I was delighted when Konnie came back to us. She is so happy and has a wonderful future ahead of her.'
