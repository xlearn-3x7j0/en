Genderqueer author Jacob Tobia opens up about gender role conformity

A genderqueer activist and author has penned an open letter in which he implores people to be more empathetic towards teens and adolescents struggling with gender identity.

Writing for Vice, Jacob Tobia, 26, who doesn't identify exclusively as either male or female, and uses the pronoun 'they', shared that as a teenager growing up in Raleigh, North Carolina, they felt pressured to sacrifice parts of their personality to try and 'blend in'. 

'As teenagers, uncertainty begets conformity. When we aren’t sure what’s going on in our lives and we don’t know what’s happening to our bodies, we try to blend in by mimicking the people around us and the characters we see on TV and online,' Jacob wrote.

Jacob went on: 'For boys, more often than not, those images are of action heroes, soldiers, fighters, and men with hulking muscles. 

'They’re of people renowned for physical prowess and athletic ability. They’re of people who represent a narrow mold of archetypal masculinity.'

They explained that often, this desire to conform and not be seen as different can equate to a rejection of 'gentleness, grace, kindness, sensitivity, flamboyance, and enthusiasm'. 

They also suggested that 'male-assigned' teenagers are pressured to not experiment with 'clothing, fabric, texture, and color; our ability to be publicly emotional, with the exception of showing anger and aggression.' 

Speaking from their own experience, Jacob also suggested that every teenage boy is insecure, regardless of his race or sexual orientation, and that insecurity can be easily manipulated by someone with 'only a sliver of confidence'.   

However, Jacob also suggested that people have the power to 'change the model of masculinity simply by insisting on defining it for yourself', something they have been working towards since their teens. 

As a teenager, Jacob belonged to a church choir that encouraged female participants to 'dance enthusiastically when the praise band plays songs' but taught the boys to 'be chill and not dance too much.'

'I hated that rule. I wanted to shake and shimmy and do all of the motions to all of the songs about Jesus and how great he was. I wanted to throw my hands up in the air and bop side to side and get down with my bad self (for the Lord, of course). But I couldn’t, because that wasn’t what guys did,' Jacob explained. 

And it was only when a male high school senior who played guitar joined the girls at the front and 'went for it', that the rules changed 'overnight'. 

'Right then I learned that, as a teenage guy, if you decide that you’re going to wear whatever you want regardless of whether or not it’s "masculine enough," odds are everyone will go along with it if you’re confident.'

Jacob also suggested that having the confidence to stand up to bullies, will impress people more. 

They added: 'And if you confidently acknowledge that being cruel, tough, or physically imposing has nothing to do with adulthood or manhood, your friends just might get with the program.' 

Committed to fostering this mindset, Jacob recently appeared in a new campaign for Fluide, a beauty brand based in New York City that sells make-up for people of all gender identities, with a tagline that reads: 'cruelty-free makeup for him, her, them, everyone'.  

Jacob has also penned a book Sissy, which deals with gender identity.
