Jurgen Klopp rattles Manchester City's cage and they can't cope

So this is the thing with the Pep Guardiola philosophy. It is special and it is beguiling and one day it will be his to patent. But when it goes wrong, it goes really wrong and here at an enraptured Anfield the wheels of Manchester City's European season came spinning off in a way that nobody could really have imagined.

During a pre-match press exchange this week, Guardiola was asked why he never considers a change of approach to his football, especially for away games in Europe where a clean sheet can be almost as valuable as a goal.

In some ways the question was meant as a compliment in that it acknowledged Guardiola's bravery and commitment to attacking football. Equally, it pointed to a stubbornness, a failure to recognise an Achilles heel in his approach to the game.

This night at Anfield was an example of when Pep Guardiola's philosophy can go really wrong

Whatever the case, maybe his answer was telling. Guardiola said his players would find it 'scary' to be asked to change their ways.

So something that at the time sounded like a manager expressing his steadfast belief in winning on his own terms sounded at full-time last night like the words of a coach who has never really understood or appreciated the value and critical importance of flexibility.

That sounds very strange to say about someone as innovative and intelligent as Guardiola. He has brought something special to English football already.

But the truth is that for the Spaniard this was actually the worst kind of Groundhog Day. With Bayern Munich in 2014, he lost a Champions League semi-final 5-0 over two legs to Real Madrid. In Manchester next week, he has the chance at least to save face even if it will take something quite extraordinary to save this tie.

As magnificent as Klopp's buccaneering Liverpool were, this was a defeat fashioned on the back of Guardiola intransigence and some straight forward City mistakes. Between coach and players, the blame must be shared.

Ederson did not cover himself in glory as Mohamed Salah scored to give Liverpool the lead

Guardiola had smiled wanly when asked about the Anfield atmosphere ahead of this match and therefore may have been as surprised as anybody at how abjectly his players failed to cope. For the nuts and bolts of this story are that City began positively and then simply fell apart.

After the second Liverpool goal ripped past him in to the right hand corner of his goal in just the 20th minute, City goalkeeper Ederson could only turn to the blue shirts in front of him and shrug his shoulders. It was already turning in to that kind of night. With the tie still in its infancy, the ground was already slipping from beneath City's feet.

Earlier in the game, there had been little sign of the crisis that was about to crash on to their shores. City had played the better football, enjoyed the possession and, rarely for a City side at Anfield, had looked in control.

But in football one error can soon become two and three and then four and that was what happened during 19 minutes of football that may well have settled this tie.

Earlier City's supporters had attempted to project some kind of superiority over those massed at the Kop end.

Liverpool fans displayed a banner that listed a quintet of European Cup wins before the match

'Just like United, you live in the past,' they sang and a banner held aloft by the Liverpool fans listing a quintet of European Cup wins that concluded with their most recent 13 years ago maybe added a little credence to that.

But Klopp's Liverpool are a burgeoning force and this was another night to add a layer or two more to the feeling that it will be they who challenge City for domestic dominance next year.

Before the game Guardiola had also said he hoped the Liverpool supporters would be 'nice and polite'. That was always a rather forlorn hope and after the regrettable and inexcusable attack on the City team bus came the kind of ambush on the field with which they have become strangely familiar.

As they can be when they get on a roll, Liverpool were irrepressible. City, though, failed to react to the growing crisis, failed to close down the areas of the field in which Liverpool were hurting them. This failing was apparent when City lost 4-3 here in the league in January and it became clear that lessons had not been learned.

The atmosphere that was built inside Anfield remains to be a rare asset and overwhelmed City

Liverpool did have some moments of fortune. Mo Salah was offside in the run-up the first goal, for example. But City contributed to their own downfall by making mistakes in the concession of all three goals and this should alarm those at the Etihad who will not be satisfied until Guardiola – or someone else – takes them to the very top of the European game.

Liverpool also surprised us here but in a different way. Klopp's team showed in the second half that they could retreat in to organisation to keep a team like City at arm's length. To at that end, the most remarkable statistic of the night was the one that showed City failing to register a shot on target.

After three games between the teams this season, City now lead by an aggregate of 8-7. Given that they won the first of the trilogy 5-0, that as much as anything illustrates how Klopp has managed to learn some lessons that maybe the gilded Guardiola has not.
