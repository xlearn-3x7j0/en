The last headhunters: Tribe who tattoo face after decapitating enemies

A  photographer has found and pictured the last headhunters of the Konyak Tribe who are dying out thanks to the spread of Christianity.  

Severing the head of an enemy warrior was a rite of passage in the tribe and earned members a prestigious facial tattoo.

But the spread of western religion in Nagaland, India, played a significant role in ending the brutal practice.

Now, the last remaining Konyaks have reached old age – and despite their fierce reputation and appearance – which includes piercings and many tattoos - they’re warm and welcoming to visitors.

The last headhunters of the Konyak Tribe, who were known for getting their faces tattooed after cutting off the heads of their enemies, are warm and friendly

In the tribe, located in Nagaland, India, severing the head of an enemy warrior was a rite of passage - and earned them a prestigious facial tattoo

Photographer Omar Reda said he got a 'very friendly and hospital' reception when he visited Nagaland, a mountainous state in northeast India bordering Myanmar, in March.

'Despite their fierce reputation, the last generation of the Konyak headhunters is very friendly and hospitable,' Mr Reda said.

'They are known for their dark history which plays a significant part of their identity and culture.'

The Konyaks were infamous for marauding the villages of other tribes – then taking the decapitated heads of the slain as trophies.

They were also known for included gunsmithing, gunpowder-making, brassworks as well as making machetes and wooden sculptures.

They were the last of the Naga tribes to accept Christianity and in the 1940s, the grisly custom of headhunting was banned – although the last incident of it was reportedly in 1969.

'Decapitating heads of enemy warriors was an essential part of the Konyak culture,' Mr Reda said.

'The men used to collect heads, feet, and hands as trophies and hung them on the village biggest tree, or they build a sacred altar for them.

'Nowadays, most of these men are above 70. When Christianity started to spread in Nagaland, it played the central role in stopping these rituals.

'Despite this dramatic cultural change, the elderly are very proud of their culture.'

He added: 'Their tattooed faces are very striking. Buffalos' skulls decorate some houses walls around the villages.

'It signifies the number of feasts each house has offered. During my visit, I saw one real human skull with two horns sealing the ears; they believe that it prevents the killed warrior from hearing what the living are talking.'

This week, the Konyak people celebrate Aoleng, a festival to welcome the spring and also to invoke the Almighty's (Kahwang) blessing upon the land before seed-sowing.
