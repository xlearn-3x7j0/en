Male beauty queen, 19, dazzles in tiaras and high heels

A male beauty queen has shared what it takes to take home the crown - and it doesn't involve fake tan.

Conor Dillow-Parlour, of Ruthin, Denbighshire, entered his first pageant just 18 months ago but proved a natural and won his first title just a year later.

The 19-year-old, who is studying to become a beauty therapist, now holds the national title of 'UK Elite Teen Mr' and is preparing to represent the country at an international competition in Gibraltar next month. 

He enjoys taking to the stage in heels and glittering gowns, but insists judges are interested in far more than how a contestant looks.

Speaking to FEMAIL, Conor said: 'You don't need to have your nails done, your eyelashes done, a fake tan done if you don't want to. It's not a requirement.'

'Pageants aren't just about the glitz and the glamour,' he added. 

While judges focus more on a beauty queen's confidence, commitment and charity work, Conor personally enjoys getting dressed up for the competitions. 

He said he also makes sure to get 'lots of rest' beforehand to make sure he has plenty of energy to teeter around in high heels.

Conor was introduced to pageants by a family friend and her daughter, both of whom have represented the UK in international competitions. 

'They told me about pageants and told me I need to do them,' he said.  

With their guidance, Conor made it to the finals of the Mr Worldwide pageant in October 2016.

While he didn't win, he impressed the judges enough that he was invited to return for the competition the following year. He took home the title on his second try. 

The title earned him a spot in the UK Elite Teen Mr competition in February and now Conor is readying himself for the international finals in Gibraltar. 

The teenager, who describes himself as a beauty queen, said pageants have also boosted his confidence after years of bullying.    

He said: 'I have encountered bullying and it's something that's horrible to speak about it but I like to speak about it because it's necessary.

'I was being bullied because I'm gay and I'm completely different. I wear dresses and heels. I wasn't wearing them then but I do now. Back then they just knew me as "gay Conor". They were just bullying.'

Conor revealed the torment escalated to the point where he was being 'physically bullied' and being 'punched in P.E.' 

Now the teenager, who studies at Wrexham Yale College, is focused on challenging the misconceptions held about himself and his fellow beauty queens.

He continued: 'Way back in the 1970s pageants were a form of female empowerment and they still are now. They are absolutely amazing.

'Last week [at the Miss Galaxy UK finals] I got to sit with the international queens, I got to see people from the US. We're all just one big community.

'There are so many smart women in pageantry, there are doctors, lawyers, they have turned out to be amazing women.

'You don't judge someone for wanting to be a policewoman or a footballer, so why do you judge them for wanting to be a beauty queen? Actually you can be a policewoman or a footballer and a beauty queen at the same time.' 

While Conor's mother, step-father, and siblings are all supportive of his quest to become a beauty queen, he warns would-be pageant contestants to be 'prepared for criticism'. 

He added: 'I say it to both men and women, as long as you are true to yourself then go for it. Even if you're not confident then go for it, it will build up your self esteem. It is the best.'
