Doctor who devoted his life to mountain rescues, dies in an avalanche

A  French doctor who devoted his life to saving those caught up in mountain disasters died today in an Alpine avalanche while skiing off piste.

Dr Emmanuel Cauchy, 58, was hit by a massive wall of snow and ice which cascaded into his skiing party near the resort of Chamonix, in eastern France.

The avalanche took place in the Aiguilles Rouges, part of the Mont Blanc range,  at around 7,875ft, injuring four others - one woman and three men.

A source close to the investigation said: ‘A helicopter and dog team took part in the rescue. Those wounded were evacuated to hospital in Sallanches, where they are in a serious condition.’

Others confirmed that the dead man was Dr Cauchy, an emergency doctor who specialised in illnesses and injuries caused by extreme cold, and especially frostbite.  

Earlier this year he was lauded for helping to save the life of Elisabeth Revol, a 37-year-old mountaineer who was rescued following an unprecedented night climb of Nanga Parbat, in the Pakistani Himalayas.

Dr Cauchy offered advice and guidance to those looking after Ms Revol, who is also French, following an ordeal in which her friend Tomek Mackiewicz, a 43-year-old climber from Poland, died.

Legendary: Dr Cauchy specialised in illnesses and injuries caused by extreme cold, and especially frostbite, and had been nicknamed Dr Vertical because of his constant work high up on mountains

As he tried to save one of Elisabeth’s feet from amputation, Dr Cauchy said at the time: ‘It’s a race against the clock. I prefer to be pessimistic, hoping for a nice surprise depending on the development of the frostbite’.

Such calm advice was typical of Dr Cauchy, who was nicknamed ‘Dr. Vertical' because of his constant work high up on mountains.

At the time of his death, he was running the Institute of Training and Research in High Mountain Medicine (IFREMMONT), which is based in Chamonix.

Saved: Dr Cauchy helped save the life of Elisabeth Revol, a 37-year-old mountaineer who was rescued following a climb in the Pakistani Himalayas

Dr Cauchy had taken part in expeditions all over the world with his wife Sandra Leal, who is also a doctor specialising in mountain health. 

Despite its immense beauty, the Aiguilles Rouges is known for often fatal accidents, especially involving climbers and off-piste skiers.

Forecasters had put out a warning about the risk of avalanches over the Easter weekend, traditionally one of the busiest of the year.   

Eric Fournier, the mayor of Chamonix, said: ‘The route on which the avalanche took place is very well known, and very busy.’ He confirmed it was not only a designated piste.
