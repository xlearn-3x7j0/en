Nasser Hussain's Test marks out of 10 for England’s winter

England failed to win a Test against Australia and New Zealand in what must go down as a hugely disappointing tour. 

The evergreen James Anderson worked magic with the Kookaburra ball and Jonny Bairstow also comes out in credit but Moeen Ali followed a great summer with a disappointing winter. 

Here, Sportsmail's NASSER HUSSAIN rates and slates the performances of England's players over the past few months. 

James Anderson needs to be looked after by England and is still producing the goods

James Anderson 8

(25 wkts at 26, 10 runs at 2)

To achieve those figures with the Kookaburra, against good players on flat pitches, was exceptional. 

There was no lack of pace, and he went for only 2.2 runs an over. The drop-off from him and Broad to the rest is a worry for England – and it shows how long they need to look after them.

Jonny Bairstow 8

(469 runs at 36, 19 ct, 1st)

Scored two of England’s four hundreds, which just goes to show how wasted he is at No 7. His wicketkeeping hardly got a mention in seven Tests all winter, which shows how good it is now. I can’t remember one bad innings with the gloves. I love his energy and the way he plays the game.

Joe Root 7.5

(520 runs at 43, 4 wkts at 32)

Seven fifties in seven games tells you how consistent he is, but he’s too good a player not to have converted at least a couple of them. It’s got nothing to do with being captain: he had the same problem before he got the job. 

He just seems to change tempo occasionally when he gets to 50. There’s no need. He did OK with the captaincy with the hand he was dealt, but he was guilty of waiting until the final Test of the winter to change his bowling attack.

England captain Joe Root had a solid winter but waited too long to change his bowling attack

Dawid Malan 7

(461 runs at 35)

He did very well considering this was his first tour of both Australia and New Zealand. He relished the Australian conditions because he looks more comfortable off the back foot. 

When he was in New Zealand, Trent Boult and Tim Southee were pitching it up and making him play French cricket from the crease. He’ll have to be careful about doing that in England. But he’s a hard worker and offers an option with his leg-spin.

Stuart Broad 6.5

(22 wkts at 33, 154 runs at 12)

He clearly benefited from the work he put in between the tours of Australia and New Zealand, and ended up getting movement away from the right-hander and into the left-hander. 

By the time he got to Auckland and especially Christchurch, his pace was up and his knees were kicking. And he still gets good players out. After a difficult Ashes, that was a real bonus for England.

Jack Leach 6

(2 wkts at 56, 30 runs at 30)

He’s got a bit about him. I liked the way he strolled to the crease and twice batted well with Bairstow, and he was decent with the ball on an unhelpful pitch. He’s the man in possession, so he should start against Pakistan, but his challenge will be carving out a role for himself in English conditions.

Jack Leach played well enough to keep his place in the side ahead of Moeen Ali 

Mason Crane 6

(1 wkt at 193, 6 runs at 3)

One for 193 in his only Test, at Sydney, doesn’t sound like much, but for a young leg-spinner on debut against the Australians it wasn’t a bad effort. He’ll be disappointed, though, that he was outbowled on the Lions tour of the Caribbean by Leach.

Craig Overton 5

(7 wkts at 42, 98 runs at 24)

I enjoyed his emergence as an international cricketer – he was gutsy with the bat and in the field, but away from home he added to the one-dimensional nature of this England attack. And he could with an extra yard of pace.

Ben Stokes 5

(103 runs at 25)

England missed him in Australia, and they missed his bowling in New Zealand, where his injury really affected the balance of the team. Made a good 60 in Auckland, then played a bad shot just before the final session. England need him back with the ball.

Ben Stokes was missed in Australia and an injury prevented him bowling in New Zealand

Mark Stoneman 5

(393 runs at 30)

Made a few starts, including four half-centuries, but couldn’t go beyond 60. As an opener, you’ve got to convert, because there will be plenty of low scores against the new ball. 

The Australians found him out a bit with the short ball, but I’d stick with him going into the summer. He may feel the pressure though, if Nick Gubbins or Haseeb Hameed start the season with big runs. 

James Vince 5

(336 runs at 30)

A bit like Stoneman, he just couldn’t get that big score under his belt. He’ll regret being run out for 83 on the first day of the Ashes in Brisbane. 

A big hundred there could have made a difference, both for him and the team. I can see why they’re sticking with him: he’s certainly elegant to watch. But I’d have him lower down the order, and he faces two big Tests against Pakistan.

Mark Wood 5

(2 wkts at 57, 61 runs at 30)

I was pleased they eventually brought him in at Christchurch, but he’s got to bowl more quickly than 80mph. 

And he should be one of four seamers, not one of three. He’s also better than a bang-it-in-billy. I know England were trying to copy Neil Wagner, but Wood’s a more rounded bowler than that.

Mark Wood didn't get much game time and he needs to be bowling quicker than he did

Alastair Cook 4

(399 runs at 33)

He made one big score on a flat pitch at Melbourne, but only 155 runs at an average of 12 apart from that. He’ll be looking forward to playing for Essex again and finding his rhythm, but he knows that he can’t continue with more famine than feast.

Tom Curran 4

(2 wkts at 100, 66 runs at 33)

Like Overton, he added to the predictability of the attack. Yes, the pitches he bowled on at the MCG and Sydney were flat, but I was surprised when they turned to Curran instead of someone like Wood.

Chris Woakes 4

(171 runs at 19, 10 wkts at 60)

Another who looks worryingly less effective away from home. The Kookaburra just refused to budge from the straight and narrow. 

The discrepancy between his home and away form was such that he ended up being left out at Christchurch. I do expect him though to return to the team back in England.

Jake Ball 3

(1 wkt at 115, 15 runs at 7)

See Overton and Curran above. In his only game, at Brisbane, he didn’t add much to the attack.

Moeen Ali 2

(207 runs at 18, 5 wkts at 126)

A really bad winter after a really good summer. If any player encapsulated the difference between England at home and England away, it’s Moeen Ali. 

His batting became frenetic, and I wasn’t convinced he was watching the ball properly, and his off-spin was innocuous. I still think he’s an option at home, but he probably has to regain his place off Leach. 

Moeen Ali was dropped from the final game of England's tour after some below par displays
