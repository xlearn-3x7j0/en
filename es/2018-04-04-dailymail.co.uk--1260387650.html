Former DEA agent opens up about how he tracked down 'El Chapo' in 2014

Andrew Hogan was one of multiple agents who nabbed Joaquin 'El Chapo' Guzman at a beach resort in Mazatlan, Mexico in 2014

The former Drug Enforcement Administration agent who spent seven years of his life hunting El Chapo spoke out about shared his personal photos from the period he spent trailing one of the world's most elusive cartel kingpins.

Andrew Hogan was one of multiple agents who nabbed Joaquin 'El Chapo' Guzman at a beach resort in Mazatlan, Mexico in 2014. 

For the first face-to-face meeting he wore a black baseball cap belonging to the now 61-year-old, and said he ran up to the drug lord and excitedly yelled: 'What's up, Chapo?'

Hogan said from that moment he knew he was going to tell his story at some point, and chose to write about the exhaustive and enthralling hunt in a new book, called 'Hunting El Chapo.' 

On Wednesday he spoke about the book for the first time publicly in an interview on the Today Show, and will give further details Sunday on Lester Holt's 'Dateline' on NBC.  

He also shared a personal photo from those thrilling years of his life of the moment he caught Guzman, in which the drug lord can be seen frowning with Hogan and another agent behind him and posing for the camera.

The former DEA agent called the that the day he met Guzman a 'souvenir of the hunt,' explaining he plucked it from another place where Chapo had been hiding out. 

For more than a decade El Chapo managed to evade arrest, being shepherded from safe house to safe house by the Sinaloa Cartel, which he was the head of.

The Sinaloa Cartel is one of the main cartels responsible for trafficking billions of dollars worth of cocaine, methamphetamine, marijuana and opioids across the border into the United States.

For years Guzman was at least one step ahead of agents, Hogan said. 

But he said that once they sat down and 'looked at the details,' they were finally able to catch him. 

'We began intercepting members of Chapo's inner circle, and really dismantling layers within a sophisticated structure until we got to the top, where I had his personal secretary's device, who was standing right next to him, and I could ping that,' Hogan told  Today's Savannah Guthrie on Wednesday.

By looking at that information agents could determine the pattern of where Guzman was being hidden. 

'It was in the details, in the numbers,' he said. 'The phone numbers don't lie.'  

The clues eventually lead them to the hotel in Mazatlan, where marines banged on the door of his room. 

The kingpin gave in without a fight - putting his arms up above his head and allowing himself to be arrested. 

Hogan said he knew Chapo had finally been caught when he started to hear excited radio chatter chanting: 'They got him! They got the target!'

He then drove to the underground parking lot where Guzman was being held, and Hogan got the chance to chat to the man he had spent so long searching for. 

For a brief moment they even locked eyes, he said. 

'I ran up to him and jumped into his face and said the first thing that came to my head and I screamed, what's up, Chapo?' Hogan said.

But within 16 months Guzman escaped again in a similarly dramatic fashion after tunneling below the shower in his cell at a maximum-security prison in Mexico. 

Hogan said he wasn't shocked, explaining that he knew the guards were going to have to keep him completely on lockdown if they didn't want him to escape. 

'It was pretty predictable,' Hogan explained. 

'This tunnel that went underneath the prison was the same type of tunnels that went under the safe houses, the same tunnels that were at the US/ Mexico border.'  

Six months after the escape he was captured again. That time agents were helped by informants, satellite images and intercepted cellphone communications. 

The drug lord was extradited to the United States in 2016 so he could stand trial on charges of running the largest drug-trafficking cartel in the world. 

The cartel is also said to be responsile for hundreds of murders in the United States and Mexico. 

Guzman is currently being held in near-isolation at a federal facility in New York.
