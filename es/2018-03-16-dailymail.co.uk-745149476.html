Black Americans are exposed to more air pollution than people of other races, and the toxic air may contribute to their elevated risk of heart disease and death, a new study suggests.

The risk of developing heart disease is between 45 and 49 percent higher for black people than it is for white people in the US.  

The new research from the American Heart Association helps connect the dots between race, pollution and heart disease, a potentially key piece of the puzzle behind the higher incidence of heart disease among black people.

The researchers estimated that pollution exposure could account for a quarter of the link between race and heart health issues.  

Black Americans are exposed to more air pollution on average than their white counterparts, and the toxins may account for 25 percent of their elevated heart disease risks, a study says

Heart disease kills more Americans than any other cause every year, but the threat is even more imminent for black Americans.

Compared to white people, black men are at a 44 percent greater risk of heart failure, and black women’s risks are 49 percent higher.

Though the tide has shifted in recent years, historically, there have been higher concentrations of minority, and, in particular, black Americans living in US cities than there have been white Americans.

But for the population the new study focused on – whose average age was 59 – the damage is already done.

The 30 to 45 percent of people living in urban areas near major roadways are gravely at risk of being exposed to higher levels of pollution than the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) has deemed safe from sources like diesel exhaust.

One of the most dangerous culprits of pollution-related health problems is fine particulate matter, or PM2.5.

PM2.5 lingers in the air longer than other pollutants because it is so small and light. This means that it sticks around to sicken people long after traffic passes by.

The particles are also too small for the natural filtration systems in our nose and throat too catch, so they drift into the lungs, where they can become lodged deep in tissues and even be absorbed into the blood stream.

Once in the body, PM2.5 raises risks of asthma, respiratory problems and heart and lung disease by elevating blood sugar levels and constricting or weakening blood vessels.

While genetics explain some of the predispositions that put black Americans at greater risk for heart disease, the new study suggests that these tiny toxic particles may make up some of the significant racial disparity gap for the deadly disease.

The researchers from the University of Pittsburgh analyzed levels of black carbon in PM2.5 and its effects on 1,717 Pittsburgh-area men and women.

Black carbon damages the cells that line the inner walls of blood vessels, leading to higher glucose levels as fat builds up and worse blood flow – both of which, in turn, can lead to heart disease and failure.

How PM2.5 endangers developing brains 

PM2.5 is extremely fine particulate matter in the air. 

This kind of pollution is mostly created by cars and combustion engines, so it is more concentrated in urban areas. 

Because it is about one third the diameter of a human hair, they easily slip into and become embedded in lung tissue.

According to the US Environmental Protection Agency, PM2.5 levels above 12 micrograms are unacceptable and dangerous to human health. 

Once the fine particulate matter has been inhaled, scientists think it either travels directly to the brain, or is carried there through the bloodstream. 

Previous studies have linked PM2.5 to risks for dementia, respiratory issues and, increasingly, mental health problems.

Black study participants, the researchers found, had a 45 percent greater risk of both cardiovascular disease and death from any cause.

Exposures to both black carbon and PM2.5 in general were about 2.5 percent higher, on average, among black participants than among white people involved.

Even after accounting for other lifestyle differences – such as smoking or obesity – the researchers estimated that the pollution explained 24 percent of the disparity in heart disease risks between the two groups.

Though the study was purely observational and can thus only establish connections, ‘we suspect the relationship is causal because of prior work,’ says study co-author Dr Sebhat Erqou, a cardiovascular fellow at the University of Pittsburg’s Heart and Vascular Institute.

‘So when we say 25 percent, you could reduce the differences between black and white heart risks by about a quarter if you eliminate the differences [in pollution exposure] between the two groups,’ he adds.

Since Dr Erqou and his team collected their baseline exposure data on participants, beginning in 2003, things have begun to change in Pittsburgh and throughout the US.

More black people are moving to suburbs, and more white people are moving to cities where pollution is likely to be more concentrated.

Dr Erqou says that some studies have shown that, as populations have shifted, racial differences in heart disease risks have begun to even out, but it is unclear how consistent that observation might be from place to place.

However, ‘even in Pittsburgh, pollution the pollution level has been coming down as factories are closing and the economy is becoming more associated with healthcare and computer-related technology,’ which will likely be beneficial to everyone’s health.

Even so, the new finding suggests that those living in high-pollution areas ‘can focus even more on their health and lifestyle’ to offset the damage done by the air they breathe.

Further research must be done to determine how much of the heart health-gap is filled by differences in genetics and other key environmental factors, including access to health care, which research on heart disease, other health risks and death has shown may play a major role in creating health disparities along racial lines.
