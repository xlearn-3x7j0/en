The incredible numbers behind WWE WrestleMania explained

It is Vince McMahon’s greatest creation. It was also the WWE chief’s biggest gamble.

It has become an institution within both the sports and entertainment genres and now garners massive mainstream publicity when it rolls around each spring.

We refer, of course, to WrestleMania – the crown jewel in the WWE calendar.

Vince staked all he had and more to launch WrestleMania at Madison Square Garden in New York City on March 31, 1985.

Had the event been a failure, the financial impact could have been severe enough to drive the WWE Chairman and his company out of business.

The exact opposite transpired – the inaugural WrestleMania was a big success that transformed the wrestling business; propelling the then-WWF to become the biggest wrestling promotion on the planet and helped the company’s top stars (such as Hulk Hogan and 'Rowdy' Roddy Piper) establish themselves as household names.

Over subsequent years the event became a firm fixture on the fledgling pay-per-view platform in the US and drew huge numbers of

WrestleMania 5 in 1989 (which featured a tournament for the vacant WWF Championship, which ‘Macho Man’ Randy Savage won after defeating Ted DiBiase) drew an enormous 730,000 buys.

That record stood until WrestleMania 15 in 1999 when ‘Stone Cold’ Steve Austin toppled The Rock to become WWF Champion for a third time, drawing 800,000 buys.

The WrestleMania buy-rate record was broken several more times before topping out at 1.3m buys, resulting in revenue of more than $70m (£50m), for Mania 28 in 2012 – a show headlined by John Cena versus The Rock.

Since the arrival of the WWE Network in 2014, pay-per-view has greatly diminished with most fans viewing WrestleMania through the online streaming platform.

Despite this, the event is still a massive money spinner and a key driver for the company’s biggest revenue streams.

The live gate for last year’s WrestleMania was a huge $14.5m (£10.3m), eclipsed only by Mania 32 in April 2016, which generated $17.3m (£12.2m) in gate receipts.

These figures are driven by the phenomenal attendances that WrestleMania attracts.

WrestleMania 33 drew an announced 75,245 fans last year, whereas the previous year’s show drew a record-setting announced crowd of 101,763.

Huge attendances have been a staple feature of the event since WrestleMania 23 in April 2007, when WWE started running stadiums for their showpiece spectacular on an annual basis (prior to Mania 23, stadiums had only been used on six occasions).

Such large numbers of fans attending the show invariably results in mammoth merchandise sales.

Last year’s extravaganza saw the company achieve $3.7m (£2.6m) in sales of t-shirts, replica belts and other popular merchandise, which equated to nearly $50 (£35) spent per person on WWE souvenirs.

WrestleMania is also the single biggest driver for subscriptions to the WWE Network.

At the end of 2016, the Network had 1.47m subscribers, whereas by WrestleMania, that figure had swelled to 1.95m – an increase of nearly half a million subscribers.

Of those 1.95m subscribers, 1.66m were paid subscribers (the rest accessing the Network for free through trials). Three months earlier, the paid number was 1.4m.

WrestleMania can largely be attributed to that increase of 260,000 subscribers and similar growth is expected leading up to WrestleMania 34 at the Mercedes-Benz Superdome in New Orleans on April 8.

Much of the success stemming from WrestleMania has resulted in significant growth in WWE’s overall revenues.

For 2017, the sports entertainment giant raked in a whopping $801m (£567m).

To put that number into context, only two football clubs in the world topped that number in 2017 – Real Madrid (£580m) and Manchester United (£581m).

WrestleMania season is also the high point for WWE programming’s TV ratings.

Vince McMahon will be hoping this year’s ratings can help secure a bumper new TV deal with WWE's American contract due for renewal in September 2019 and discussions already underway with interested parties.

WWE’s current deal with Universal brings in a reported $200m (£141m) per annum and company chiefs are hoping to secure a substantial increase in that figure.

WrestleMania, however, doesn’t just generate tremendous revenue for the company; it also brings great benefit to the locations.

Last year’s WrestleMania, at Camping World Stadium in Orlando, Florida on April 2, generated $181.5m (£128.3m) for the local economy, with the prior year’s event garnering $170m (£120.3) for Dallas when that city hosted Mania 32.

It’s no wonder, then, that multiple cities across the United States bid for the rights to host Mania each year (it has already been announced that WrestleMania 35 will take place at the Met Life Stadium just outside of New York on April 7, 2019).

WWE is very big business these days and WrestleMania is a key driver behind the company’s enormous financial success.
