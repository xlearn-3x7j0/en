Meet the 11-year-old Cardiff schoolgirl bidding for Commonwealth Games gold

Like most 11-year-old's, Anna Hursey’s bedroom walls are filled with posters of her favourite celebrities.

With images of Ariana Grande adorning her walls, she also likes to binge watch American drama series, Pretty Little Liars, too.

But that's where the similarities end: Cardiff schoolgirl Hursey is quite extraordinary.

The 5ft 3in table tennis phenomenon will be the youngest athlete to represent Wales at the Commonwealth Games.

And the numbers behind her success are staggering - she is the European No.1 for her age, the best in Britain at Under 18’s and Wales' youngest-ever 'senior' international in any sport.

"Sometimes the older girls’ get really annoyed when they lose to me and kick the barriers and stuff," said Hursey, in an interview with the Daily Mail .

But there is more to this sporting marvel - and the sacrifices made by Anna’s parents have made a massive impact on her career so far.

Her extraordinary tale started in her mother's birthplace of China, where Anna was taken away on holiday to, aged five.

It was just a few months before, however, when Anna picked up a bat for the first time.

Then, while visiting the Harbin Heilongjiang province, her mother Phoebe took her to a local coach for a session.

"It wasn’t anything we had planned on that occasion," explained her father, Lawrence, who had dabbled in table tennis as a club player.

They continued to pursue her talent he said because, "it was apparent that she had some technique".

When Anna returned from her family holiday, she joined a local club. It wasn't long before she was approached to join the Wales set-up when a national coach spotted her incredible talent.

Within a year, she was crowned Welsh Under 11’s champion and, aged six, her family relocated from Swansea to Cardiff to be based nearer her current club to train more often.

Their next move was, perhaps, even more dedicated as Anna returned to China - the nation who have won 28 of the 32 gold medals available since table tennis became an Olympics sport in 1988 - for the summer, aged eight.

Naturally, returning to a country that had won 28 of the 32 gold medals available since table tennis became an Olympic sport in 1988 for training seemed like the best direction to go in.

Her special talent was identified in February 2016 for a five-month training trip and when she turned nine she returned to China for a further five months of intensive training.

Anna found she was training up to thirty hours-per-week alongside her primary school education.

But her dad stated that, "despite it being a big step for Anna, she was in a place where you are training against some excellent world class players."

Anna described the experiences in China as, "really good but hard - three sessions a day, with morning training, rest, evening, night time. It is a lot."

However, this all paid off as she found herself representing the Welsh senior side at just 10-years-old.

Looking forward to her Commonwealth journey, Anna 'doesn’t want to go and just be there and make up numbers’.

But she is aiming to hit her ambitious goal of finishing on the podium in Australia.

And if all goes to plan, she will come out on top.
