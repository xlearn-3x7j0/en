Alistair Brownlee 'gunning' for glory after surprise present from brother Jonny

Alistair Brownlee revealed last night that his biggest rival for triathlon gold has gone out and bought a shotgun.

Yorkshire’s two-time Olympic champion is hot favourite to retain his Commonwealth crown when the XXI Games get underway here tomorrow.

Standing between him and yet more glory is brother Jonny who, famously, Alistair picked up and carried to the line during the 2016 World Series final in Mexico.

The younger Brownlee was on the point of collapse through heat exhaustion when Alistair came to his rescue. And not a day has gone by that he hasn’t been required to show his gratitude.

“He’s reminded of it every time we go to buy a cup of tea or lunch,” said the older sibling, who carries England’s flag at today’s opening ceremony.

“Every time I run out of money at the café stop on the bike ride, it’s his turn. He says ‘surely we’re equal now’. I say ‘nah, not even close’.

“To be fair he did buy me a present - a shotgun! We are friends with clay pigeon shooter Peter Wilson, the London 2012 Olympic champion.

“We’ve got a trap in the garden and Jonny and I do a bit of shooting. Who’s better? Me of course.”

There is seemingly no end to their competitive rivalry and Alistair, 29, has warned Jonny he is on his own this time.

“I’ve done my friendly bit for my brother,” he said. “So I won’t be helping him halfway around this course, no.

“People might call these the Friendly Games, but at the end of the day you’re on that start line with 30 other people you want to beat.”

Jonny insists he won’t need brotherly love this time, having prepared for the Gold Coast’s heat and humidity on a stationary bike in a heat chamber at home.

“Actually it was my conservatory,” he said. “I put four heaters in there - and a boiling kettle to make it more humid.”

As the curtain prepares to rise on these Games, Alistair dares to hope that progress is being made in the move towards clean sport.

India boxing team doctor Amol Patil was yesterday given a "strong written reprimand" for dispensing a vitamin injection in contravention of the ‘no-needle’ policy.

But Brownlee snr said: “You can look at that in a pessimistic way or think ‘we’re not talking about blood bags and people blood doping, we’re talking about needles on the floor’.

“There’s evidently always going to be some form of cheating in sport, whether it’s sandpaper or drugs, but in my mind it shows we’re probably moving in the right direction.”
