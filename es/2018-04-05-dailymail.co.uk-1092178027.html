Government's diesel bashing takes its toll as sales fall 37% in March

The new car market has declined for the twelfth consecutive month with diesel sales plummeting by more than a third in the industry's most important month of the year.

There was a 15.7 per cent fall in all vehicle registrations in March, which is one of the most lucrative months of the year for the sector as buyers look to get their hands on the latest number plate.

Figures released by the Society of Motor Manufacturer's and Traders showed that petrol sales were marginally higher than in March 2017 - as were registrations of alternative fuel vehicles - but that sales of diesel models, which have been subject to additional taxes in the last year, plunged by 37 per cent.

Sales slip in motor industry's biggest month of the year: The SMMT confirmed on Tuesday that total new car registrations were down by 15%, with diesel sales dropping by more than a third

Total new car registrations in March were 474,069, which is down from 562,337 a year ago.

But despite the drop in sales the SMMT remained upbeat about the strength of the motor sector. It highlighted that the declining figures were in comparison to record sales in March a year ago, which was the most fruitful month for registrations ever.

Last March was also drivers' last opportunity to purchase new models before Vehicle Excise Duty car tax rates were changed in April 2017, which ultimately sparked the year-long monthly decline in registrations confirmed on Thursday.

The SMMT said sales were 'still running at a historically high level' and confirmed that last month's market was the fourth biggest March since records began.

However, this optimistic outlook did little to hide the fact that the government's recent diesel crackdown has taken a significant toll on the motor industry's performance.  

Diesel sales in March collapsed by a whopping 37.2 per cent on last year.

And a further update to VED introduced this month could see registrations fall further as all new diesel models move one tax band higher in the first year as part of ministers'  ploy to wipe out the under-fire fuel type and persuade drivers to switch to greener cars.

Registrations of these 'cleaner' hybrid, plug-in hybrid and electric vehicles rose by a modest 5.7 per cent last month while the petrol market remained stable with an increase of 0.5 per cent.  

While petrol and alternative fuel vehicle (AFV) sales were higher, they failed to plug the substantial hole left by the decline in diesel registrations

Plunging diesel sales have seen the new car industry post a decline in registrations for the twelfth consecutive month 

While the SMMT attempted to put a positive glow on these stats, it shows that the hole left by diesel isn't being plugged by the other fuel types.

The motor industry representative added that the combination of economic and political uncertainty and confusion over air quality plans had impacted the confidence of all sales types, with business, fleet and private registrations all falling at a rate of 14.3 per cent, 15 per cent and 16.5 per cent respectively.  

Speaking on Thursday, Mike Hawes, SMMT chief executive, said the disappointing sales performance in March was 'not unexpected' given the huge surge in registrations in the same month a year ago, but admitted that the government needed to act to end 12 continual months of declining showroom activity.

'The market itself is relatively high with the underlying factors in terms of consumer choice, finance availability and cost of ownership all highly competitive,' he said.

'Consumer and business confidence, however, has taken a knock in recent months and a thriving new car market is essential to the overall health of our economy. 

'This means creating the right economic conditions for all types of consumers to have the confidence to buy new vehicles. 

'All technologies, regardless of fuel type, have a role to play in helping improve air quality whilst meeting our climate change targets, so government must do more to encourage consumers to buy new vehicles rather than hang onto their older, more polluting vehicles.' 

The SMMT said new passenger car registrations last month were the fourth highest of any March on record, though it has now dropped to the same level it was in 2014

Auto Trader, the nation's biggest new and used car selling site, said the new car industry had suffered a 'triple whammy' in the first quarter of 2018, claiming the combo of high registration stats from a year ago, several days of Arctic weather in recent weeks and the ongoing negative impact of Brexit had all played a part in the bleak figures. 

Jim Holder, editorial director at What Car? magazine, said the failure to plug the sales gap left by diesel declines showed that motorists were not feeling encouraged to purchase new cleaner alternatives.

'Buyers don't believe that petrol or electrified cars can deliver the performance or economy benefits they need, and so they are holding on to older vehicles for longer,' he said.

'That evidence would suggest once again that the Government would do well to clarify its position on diesels and to stimulate people into buying newer, cleaner cars.'

Car selling comparison website Motorway.co.uk said last year's announcement that all new petrol and diesel vehicles would be banned from sale in 2040 had also played a role in quashing consumer confidence.

Alex Buttle, director at the site, said: 'Car owners are confused about what they should be doing and what their purchasing choices are across petrol, Euro 6 diesels and AFVs.

'The government can do more, and it could start by putting its full financial backing behind electric and hybrid motoring, and offering attractive subsidies to those motorists who switch now.

'The accelerator pedal also needs to be pushed on the government's electric car infrastructure rollout to be attractive for would-be electric car owners. There are simply not yet enough visible charging points on UK streets to convince people to switch.'

The SMMT reiterated that the importance of the UK new car market to the UK economy shouldn't be overlooked, especially because it has a wider impact on adjacent sectors, such as logistics, retail and distribution to car finance, fuel, maintenance and insurance.

Some 200,000 people are employed in new car retail alone, while UK-based car finance firms employ over 45,000 more, with an annual £12.5 billion economic contribution. 

On the road, the vehicle fuel industry supports 40,000 jobs, and a further 347,000 are employed in vehicle servicing and repair, the the sector representative said. 

The Ford Fiesta remained the nation's favourite new model, while the VW Golf cemented its spot as the next most-bought car in the country. 

The Ford Fiesta is still the best-selling new model in the UK, the SMMT confirmed
