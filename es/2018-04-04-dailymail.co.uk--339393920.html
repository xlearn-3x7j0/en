Fired NFL cheerleader details 'humiliation' she was subjected to

A former New Orleans Saints cheerleader who was fired for posting a sexy photo on Instagram has alleged that a franchise executive told her she had 'dirty face' in the social media snapshot during the humiliating dismissal.

Bailey Davis, 22, from Hattiesburg, Mississippi, was let go in January after Saints officials accused her of appearing nude, semi-nude, or in lingerie and attending a party with one of the franchise's football players — both of which are prohibited. However, she has denied breaching any protocols.

After filing a complaint with the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission against her former employer on March 23, Bailey insists in an essay published by Cosmopolitan.com that she 'didn’t think of it as a sexy photo' when she shared an image of herself modeling a sheer black lace one-piece.

Innocent: The 22-year-old from from Hattiesburg, Mississippi, insisted that she 'didn’t think of it as a sexy photo,' noting that she planned on using it at future dance auditions 

'I had it taken for my portfolio, to show at future dance auditions,' she explained. 'It was a full-body shot because I wanted to show off my physique, my athleticism. 

'I liked that you could see a little bit of bicep. 

'As far as baring skin, it wasn’t any different from our Saintsation uniforms,' she added. 'When I was fired, the human resources director told me I had a dirty face in the photo.' 

NFL cheerleaders must adhere to a strict set of rules regulating everything from their personal appearance, their shape, and their conduct on the field.

Bailey, who was a Saints cheerleader for three years before she was let go, explained that there was a strict 'no fraternization' policy that forbid her or any other cheerleader to ever be in the same place as a player.

If a cheerleader is dining at a restaurant and a player walks in, they are expected to leave. They are to avoid all and any contact — be it online or in person — with players, however the football players themselves are not bound by the same rules.

Bailey noted that cheerleaders aren't even allowed to follow players on Instagram or 'like' their pictures — even if they were official photos posted from the Saints account. 

'If they tried to follow me on Instagram or liked my photos, I was to block them and report it to HR,' she added. 

'If a player spoke to me in person, I could only use two phrases: "hello" or "good game." Anything past that was fraternizing.' 

Bailey said trouble first started after the franchise started investigating reports of a blonde girl from Mississippi attending a party where a football player was present.

According to Bailey, she was in Orlando with her family at the time of the alleged party, and while no one used her name or provided any proof, the franchise believed it was her. 

Human resources asked to see her in early January, and during the meeting, they went on to ask if a player had ever messaged her on Instagram.

Bailey admitted that sometimes players would send messages or emoji to her via Instagram, but she — like the other cheerleaders — would ignore it.

She said HR then wanted to know how the players were finding her on Instagram. 

As a result of the meeting, they decided that every Saintsation cheerleader had to make her Instagram private, so players couldn't find them.

A few days after the meeting, Bailey took to her private Instagram and posted the portfolio photo of herself posed in black lingerie. 

In the professional portrait taken by Bryce Ell Photography, Bailey is wearing a V-neck leotard featuring a floral applique, lace, and sheer paneling. She is posed with one arm raised behind her head as she gazes into the camera lens.

A teammate texted her 15 minutes later, advising her to delete it. Although she followed her friend's advice, the cheerleader still ended receiving a scolding text message from her coach.

Accusations: Bailey (pictured celebrating the military at a game last November) was also accused of attending a party with a football player, which she has denied 

Ashley Deaton, the senior director of the Saintsations, told Bailey it was 'very poor judgement to post a picture like that' following their recent conversation about the rumors regarding her being at a party with a player.

The coach went on to notify her mother, Lora Davis, who served as the team’s field director and choreographer for the past 18 years, telling her that Bailey had to resign immediately or be terminated. 

When Bailey refused to quit, she was asked to attend another 'humiliating' meeting with HR and the Saintsation leadership. 

'A Saints executive said the photo made me look guilty of hanging out with the players; he said that I had a "dirty face" and that he’d never let his granddaughters post something like that,' she recalled, noting that he kept telling her: 'Perception is reality.' 

After she was fired, Bailey contacted an employment lawyer she had recently met to ask a few questions about her termination. When the lawyer asked for a copies of both the Saintsasions and the players' handbooks, Bailey learned that the players don't have the same restrictions that cheerleaders do.

The dancer admitted that she really didn't want to file the lawsuit, but she was determined to make things equal between the football players and cheerleaders once and for all.

'If the cheerleaders can’t contact the players, then the players shouldn’t be able to contact the cheerleaders,' Bailey's lawyer Sara Blackwell told the New York Times. 'The antiquated stereotype of women needing to hide for their own protection is not permitted in America and certainly not in the workplace.'

However, the Saints are standing by Bailey's termination, as well as the organization's handbook policies.  

In an email statement sent to the New York Times, the Saint's lawyer Leslie A. Lanusse said the organization 'strives to treat all employees fairly, including Ms. Davis.'

Life's a beach: Following her termination, Bailey has been staying with her uncle in Sarasota, Florida, sharing plenty of bikini photos 

Moving on: Bailey said she is looking forward to starting a new life without the New Orleans Saints 

'At the appropriate time and in the appropriate forum, the Saints will defend the organization’s policies and workplace rules,' she added. 'For now, it is sufficient to say that Ms. Davis was not subjected to discrimination because of her gender.'

Following her termination, Bailey has been staying with her uncle in Sarasota, Florida, and she is considering staying there now that she no longer has to commute two hours each way to New Orleans. 

'I have no reason to be near New Orleans anymore,' she said at the end of her Cosmopolitan.com essay. 'I’ve been auditioning for new dancing jobs. I have to start a new life — one without the Saints.'

Bailey appears to be doing just that. Her Instagram page is no longer private, and she has been sharing plenty of snapshots of herself wearing bikinis while enjoying the sunny Florida weather

She has even reposted the snapshot that had gotten her fired.

'I wanna break glass ceilings not fit glass slippers,' she captioned the image, using the hashtag #leveltheplayingfield.'
