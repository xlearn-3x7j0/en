The four graphs that show why households face a very bumpy road ahead 

There's no knowing how the UK economy will fare over the next few months. Whether wages will finally pick up and businesses boom or if a downturn is just around the corner – as tends to happen every few years.

But a series of data released by the Bank of England and the Office for National Statistics suggests that households could struggle if things take a turn for the worse.

These four graphs offer a snapshot of the difficulties British households are already facing.

1. Household saving ratio has hit a record low

The household saving ratio has dipped to just 4.9 per cent - not long ago it neared 12 per cent

The savings ratio is the proportion of our income that we stash away every month. Last year it dropped to just 4.9 per cent - a record low. 

That means that for every £100 we made last year, we put away just £4.90 in savings. Savings are the ultimate buffer against something going wrong – job loss, an unexpected bill. 

As individuals, having a smaller savings stash can have a huge impact should our circumstances change - it leaves us more vulnerable to spiraling debt. 

The ONS, which compiled the figures, gave a very simple explanation for why we are saving less on average: we are continuing to spend more.

Household disposable income grew by a pitiful 0.3 per cent over the same year, and we are spending a greater proportion of our incomes than we used to.

2. Household borrowing is nearing record highs

As of the end of last year, households owed £207billion. That doesn't even include mortgages or student loans. As much as £70.3billion is on credit cards alone, while tens of billions is on debts such as car finance and personal loans.

The amount of outstanding consumer debt has been steadily rising by over nine per cent a year for some time now.

It has only been higher once: in September 2008 – hardly an exemplary moment in our financial history from which to be benchmarking.

So what will happen if interest rates start to rise, if the availability of consumer debt dries up, or if the record low employment rate starts to inch up?

Individuals could really struggle with their other expenses as the cost of debt eats up a greater proportion of their disposable income.

There could also be a wider impact, should default rates rise and impact lenders, or households reign in their spending shrinking the size of consumer spending on which our economy depends.

Consumer debt continues to rise every year, leaving many without a buffer should they need it

Joanna Elson OBE, chief executive of the Money Advice Trust, the charity that runs National Debtline, said: 'Despite consumer credit balances now standing at over £209billion, at present this is unlikely to be an issue for the majority of households able to meet repayments. Our concern is of a growing risk of difficulty for those households already struggling with rising living costs and slow wage growth.

'All too often it takes just one change in circumstances, such as a job loss or a relationship breakdown to push people into financial difficulty. At National Debtline we already hear from thousands of people struggling to meet daily costs, with nearly one in three are already struggling with credit card debt and one in seven are struggling with loan repayments.

'I encourage anyone who is struggling to cope to contact National Debtline or another free debt advice agency as soon as possible.'

It's easy to see these astronomical consumer debt figures as a sign that households are overspending on things that they don't need – and that financial prudence would solve everything.

But the debt charity StepChange estimates that 1.4million households used high cost credit last year for basic living expenses.

Prices have continued to rise at a faster pace than most people's pay packets.

3. Wages are still falling

Wages have been falling in real terms over the past year, which means that we have less spending power.

Real wage growth has stayed between -0.6 per cent and 0.6 per cent excluding bonuses throughout 2017.

Furthermore, many people are not able to work as much as they would like. 'It remains the case that for any 100 people who work part-time, the number of people who want to work more hours is higher now than it was prior to the economic downturn,' the ONS says. 

It adds: 'Similar to the case of part-time workers who could not find a full-time job, the number of temporary workers who could not find a permanent job started decreasing thereafter but remained relatively high and is growing again – perhaps a sign of economic uncertainty'.

Wages have failed to rise recently - private sector wages are doing slightly better than public

4. Prices are continuing to rise

Inflation remains way above target, with costs of essential items continuing to rise. Housing costs, household bills such as gas, electricity, and food and drink costs are all increasing, putting further pressure on household budgets.

Rising costs don't matter in theory if incomes are rising to match them. However, wage growth and increases to benefits remain stagnant while prices race on ahead.

Prices continue to rise with cost pressures from housing costs, food and transport in particular

...But there is some good news

Unemployment is at a record low of 4.3 per cent. This means that while everyone is not working the amount that they wish, or earning to their potential, most people who want to work are able to do so.

Most people are also benefiting from auto-enrolment, which will mean millions of people will have greater nest eggs to live on in their later years. 

However, the proportion of people's wages diverted towards their pension savings will continue to rise, leaving less cash in their pay packets at the end of each month. With finances already stretched, this could have a material impact on people's ability to spend.

The unemployment rate remains at record lows, with most people who want to work able to find employment.
