Trump's Amazon attack and trade war with China sends markets plunging

President Donald Trump's renewed attacks on Amazon and China's pushback against billions in new U.S. tariffs sent the markets on another dive that wiped out the gains Trump has touted.

A day that began with another Trump tweet going after the online retailer that also owns the Washington Post ended with the Dow Jones Industrial Average dropping 1.9 per cent, or 459 points.

That was an improvement from earlier in the day, when the Dow plunged more than 750 points. 

The broader S&P 500 lost 58.99 points, or 2.2 percent of its value on the day Monday.

Weighing down the index was Amazon, down 5.9 percent after Trump's relentless attacks on a company Trump blamed for costing the Postal Service a 'FORTUNE.' 

'Only fools, or worse, are saying that our money losing Post Office makes money with Amazon. THEY LOSE A FORTUNE, and this will be changed,' Trump tweeted. 'Also, our fully tax paying retailers are closing stores all over the country...not a level playing field!' 

The tweet followed a series of earlier attacks last week – a period which has seen Amazon's stock drop by more than 10 per cent.

The Dow closed a 23,644, in another dramatic day of high-pressure trading in a year that featured a long climb through January and a steep February drop.

Among other losers in the market were those bracing for new tariffs applied by China Monday, in response to new Trump administration tariffs following a saber rattling tweet by Trump that the U.S. would 'win' any trade war.

The also followed the Trump administration's decision to slap a 25 per cent tariff on imported steel and a 10 per cent tariff on aluminum. 

China jacked up duties on $3 billion worth of U.S. goods. U.S. companies from Tyson Foods – down 4.6 per cent – to Hormel and Pilgrim's Pride took a hit.

The new tariffs ran as high as 25 percent on a list of U.S. products.

In the escalating war of words, the White House accused China of 'distorting global markets.'

'China's subsidization and continued overcapacity is the root cause of the steel crises," said White House spokeswoman Lindsay Walters. 'Instead of targeting fairly traded U.S. exports, China needs to stop its unfair trading practices which are harming US national security and distorting global markets,' she said. 

On March 29, Trump went after Amazon for its tax treatment, and allied himself with brick and mortar retailers. The Trump organization is a major real estate owner – and it has commercial tenants including Nike and Tiffany in New York.

'I have stated my concerns with Amazon long before the Election. Unlike others, they pay little or no taxes to state & local governments, use our Postal System as their Delivery Boy (causing tremendous loss to the U.S.), and are putting many thousands of retailers out of business!' Trump wrote.

Amazon collects state taxes on direct sales, but not on third-party transactions that go through its site.

He also blasted the 'Fake Washington Post' in a subsequent tweet. 

Other tech stocks also took a beating. Amazon rival Netflix was down 5.1 per cent, Google was down 2.3 per cent, and Facebook dropped 2.75 per cent.
