Scientists who claimed Crispr caused mutations admit they were wrong

Crispr-Cas9 gene editing technology has been hailed as humanity's route to 'designer babies' who are tailored with desirable genetic traits. 

Crispr can effectively 'cut and paste' small sections of DNA out of a genome and replace or delete harmful genes. 

This ability to 'customise' genes has led to speculation it could also be used to eradicate cancer and Huntington's disease.

Last year, research was published which claimed the popular process could introduce hundreds of unintended and potentially harmful mutations into DNA.

Now, these researchers have been forced to admit their research was flawed and that Crispr is in fact harmless and does not cause any genetic mutations.

Crispr has previously been touted as a cheap and quick route to treating genetic diseases such as congenital blindness or asthma.

Dr Antony Adamson from the University of Manchester told MailOnline: 'In the short term CRISPR has already revolutionised life science research. 

'We are now able to generate more accurate and representative models of human disease, and any data we glean from these is of course more useful. 

'In the long term we will see Crispr being used in the clinic, most likely for severe genetic diseases and life threatening illnesses.' 

In a 2017 study, researchers from Columbia University sequenced the entire genome of mice that had undergone Crispr gene editing and looked for mutations.

The team concluded that Crispr had successfully corrected a gene that causes blindness in the mice.

At the time, cell biologist Stephen Tsang from Columbia University said it was important to look at the bigger picture. 

'We feel it's critical that the scientific community consider the potential hazards of all off-target mutations caused by CRISPR'.

Off-target mutations can happen when Crispr targets a section of DNA that is the same or similar to the one it was designed for, but on a different chromosome. 

Study coauthor Kellie Schaefer from Stanford University found that the DNA sequences of two independent gene therapy recipients had been hit by more than 1,500 small mutations, known as single-nucleotide changes.

They also found more than 100 larger deletions and insertions in the genetic code, in which a larger section of the DNA is changed. 

As a result, the research team, led by Columbia University, said that previous research into the risks associated with Crispr was flawed and it could be dangerous. 

The study suggested that children born through Crispr gene editing could suffer from hundreds of off-target genetic mutations, some of which could be dangerous. 

If this mutation hits an important gene, such as a cancer-resisting 'tumour suppressor gene', it could cause unwanted health problems for users of Crispr.

The original research claimed that Crispr can cause hundreds of off-target genetic mutations, some of which could be dangerous. After publishing, the study was widely criticised 

As soon as it was published, the study widely condemned.

Outside of academic circles, the, paper damaged the perception of gene-editing, and Crispr in general, to the general public.

The controversial study was even greeted with a statement from the editors of the publishing journal, Nature Methods. 

The authors of the study claimed an 'alternative proposed interpretation is that the observed changes are due to normal genetic variation'. 

This was the crux of most people's concerns.

A critique of the original paper by another team pointed out that the two gene-edited mice in the experiment were genetically more closely related to each other than to the third, 'control' mice.

With such a small sample size, the results weren't just unreliable, they were misleading.

WHAT IS CRISPR-CAS9?

CRISPR-Cas9 is a tool for making precise edits in DNA, discovered in bacteria.

The acronym stands for 'Clustered Regularly Inter-Spaced Palindromic Repeats'.

The technique involves a DNA cutting enzyme and a small tag which tells the enzyme where to cut.

The CRISPR/Cas9 technique uses tags which identify the location of the mutation, and an enzyme, which acts as tiny scissors, to cut DNA in a precise place, allowing small portions of a gene to be removed

By editing this tag, scientists are able to target the enzyme to specific regions of DNA and make precise cuts, wherever they like.

It has been used to 'silence' genes - effectively switching them off.

When cellular machinery repairs the DNA break, it removes a small snip of DNA.

In this way, researchers can precisely turn off specific genes in the genome.

The approach has been used previously to edit the HBB gene responsible for a condition called β-thalassaemia. 

The implication from critics of the study was that the 'unexpected mutations' seen were not the result of Crispr.

It was believed these were simply caused by standard genetic differences between different animals which is amplified when looking at close relatives.

Following the condemnation of their method, the team of researchers from Columbia University returned to their study.

With the criticism in mind, they assessed their work and their method.

In a 'corrigendum', a correction, published this week in pre-print site bioRxiv, they began by defending their earlier work.

Nevertheless, the scientists conceded that the results could not be replicated when the recommended changes were made.

According to the correction, the latest research 'support(s) the idea that in specific cases, CRISPR-Cas9 editing can precisely edit the genome at the organismal level and may not introduce numerous, unintended, off-target mutations'.

All in all, the controversial scientists admit that the first experiment may have been a mistake.

As a result, this all but clears Crispr of any risk and removes one of only a few black marks on its reputation.

After trying, and failing, to replicate their results again, the scientists conceded that Crispr is an effective tool that does not cause any mutations 

Although concerns remain, predominantly focusing on ethical issues, the practicalities and safety of the gene-editing process are hard to refute. 

Some people argue that the damning first paper should never have been published and should now be retracted.

Although this is yet to happen, it is evidence of the scientific method at work. 

HOW COULD CRISPR CREATE DESIGNER BABIES? 

Crispr gene editing technology can precisely 'cut and paste' sections of DNA to either remove unwanted genes or insert desirable ones.

Some have suggested the technology could be used to remove damaging genes from children before they are born, such as those that cause Huntington's disease or hereditary blindness.

But the technology could also be used to insert the genes for desired traits such as blonde hair or above-average height.

If science's understanding of genetics improves, the technology could one day be used to insert genes that encode certain skills, such as musical ability.

Dr Edward Wild, a clinician scientist at the UCL Institute of Neurology who was not involved in the study, spoke to MailOnline about the importance of this development. 

He said: 'This is how science is supposed to work - a team does important work and finds a striking result, publishes it after peer review and then they and others try to replicate it.

'They have been pretty open about the lack of replication. 

'A bigger problem is when replication isn’t attempted and the field carries on taking previous findings for granted - combined with a tendency for new discoveries to be overhyped in the beginning. 

'So this is an encouraging course-correction that offers reassurance about both Crispr and the scientific method in general.'

The scientific method is a universal 'code' which all scientists adhere to which contributes to the collective knowledge of academic fields. 

As a first, and crucial step, the findings are published in a journal and peer-reviewed by fellow experts.

After being published, the wider scientific community then scrutinises the work and this drives further research. 

Often it is at this point that flaws in the scientific method are discovered, and further research rectifies them.

Either corroborated or dispelled, scientific discoveries are all put through the wringer at several different stages before they become widely accepted.   

In the scientific method, validation is only achieved through consistent replication.

Replication is 'critical, or science fails' says Dr Adamson, who also believes the paper should be retracted. 

Famed physicist Michio Kaku said in 2014: 'Science, however, is never conducted as a popularity contest, but instead advances through testable, reproducible, and falsifiable theories.'

That is, to be considered proper science research must be easy to replicate around the world.
