Dean Wilson: 'England have gone nowhere on this mammoth five-months-plus tour'

It started with a headbutt and finished with their skipper on the floor, but after five and a half months and seven Tests on an epic tour England have gone nowhere.

It might not have been as much of a tour de sauce in New Zealand as it was in Australia, where boozy antics resulted in a curfew, but the outcome was the same.

The sight of captain Joe Root scrambling around on his knees here in Christchurch on the final day of a possible 35, trying to snuffle catches at the end of a fascinating Test, was one to cause a few chuckles but a second series defeat out of two this winter is no laughing matter.

England had the chance to finish things on a high note after taking four of the 10 wickets they needed inside the first hour, including two in two from Stuart Broad. But the bravery and character of the Kiwi middle and lower order was enough to deny Root's bowlers for 124 overs and earn just a fourth ever Test series win over the tourists.

England were left to rue four dropped catches that might have saved their blushes, but perhaps that would have just papered over the cracks.

This vintage are simply not very good at Test cricket away from home. Fact.

They have now set a new unwanted record of going 13 successive matches on tour without a win, which is the sort of trend that could soon extend to performances at home in a challenging summer against Pakistan and India.

“It is very frustrating,” admitted Root. “We threw everything at New Zealand and created chances, but it has been a disappointing winter in terms of results. We’re going to have to adapt better to conditions and avoiding an innings like 58 all out (at Auckland) will play a massive part in that. We’re a far better side than that.

“There is plenty for us to learn from and to work on, we’re nowhere near where we want to be as a side but I think we’ve got some very talented cricketers in this format and hopefully we can build on this week and have a very successful summer.”

This has to be a hope rather than an expectation, because England have still got so many areas of weakness.

Their batting, behind Root, Ben Stokes and Jonny Bairstow, is fragile.

Their pace bowling behind James Anderson and Stuart Broad is one dimensional, and their spin bowling department has a revolving door turning faster than the balls they are bowling.

Here, it was Jack Leach’s turn to take up the gauntlet and he acquitted himself just fine for a man on debut and should be given the chance to play throughout the summer.

But, just like it is for several of his team-mates, how he will do remains a big unknown.
