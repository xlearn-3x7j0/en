Couple ordered to plant grass even though wife is allergic to it

A Missouri couple have been ordered to plant grass in their front yard - even though one of them is allergic to it.

Janice and Carl Duffner decided to plant a flower garden in the yard of their St. Peters home after they moved in 2002.

As pretty as the flower garden was, the couple's main motivation behind it is because Janice is allergic to grass, Kansas City Star reports.

The Missouri couple's pretty flower garden is against the city ordinance which demands that residents have 50 per cent of their yard turned over to grass

But the city found that they were in violation of a 2008 ordinance, brought in six years after the Duffers landscaped their garden, which stipulates that residents must turn at least half their yards over to grass. 

The Duffners asked for an exemption but were denied by the Board of Adjustment in 2014 who instead ordered them to plant at least five percent of their property with grass.

By 2016, the couple said they were facing penalties of $180,000 and 20 years in jail for non-compliance over their civil rights action.

They have been fighting the ordinance in court for years. Their yard includes a flag which reads 'flowers = freedom'

'If the city is permitted to impose draconian fines and imprisonment simply because a citizen chooses to cultivate on their own private property lawful, harmless plants of their own choosing instead of a potentially harmful plant of the government's choosing, there is no longer any principled limit to the government's control over either the property or the owners,' the Duffners' complaint stated.

The case continued and last week U.S. District Judge John A. Ross ruled that the Duffners 'have failed to identify a fundamental right that is restricted by the Turf Grass Ordinance.'

The judge said the Supreme Court held that 'aesthetic considerations constitute a legitimate government purpose.'

He also found the penalties were not excessive and did not constitute cruel and unusual punishment.

The Duffners' attorney, David Roland, said they planned to appeal. 

'My estimation is that this is one of the most important property rights cases in the country right now,' he said.
