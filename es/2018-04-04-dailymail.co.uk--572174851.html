portsmail takes a look back at Tiger Woods' injury troubles

After missing the last two years of the Masters, Tiger Woods returns to the scene of some of his biggest and most memorable triumphs.

The 14-time major champion, who makes his comeback at Augusta as one of the strong favourites on Thursday, has endured a nightmare time of late, most notably due to his on-going battle with his back injury.

In the last four years, Woods has undergone a total of four back surgeries to relieve the pain and get his body moving in full flow. But it isn't just his back that has played up during his stellar golfing career. Since all the way back in 1994, injuries have niggled away at him despite his dominance in the field.

In 1994, when Woods enrolled on a golf scholarship at Stanford University, he underwent surgery on his left knee to remove two benign tumours and scar tissue. 

The Californian-born rising talent, who turned professional in 1996, remained trouble-free for the next eight years. In that time, he managed to win a remarkable eight majors.

But just a few months after his last triumph in 2002 at the PGA Championship, Woods was forced to have another procedure. In December, he had surgery on his left knee to remove fluid inside and outside of the ACL and remove benign cysts.

Then in 2007, Woods picked up his latest injury in obscure fashion, rupturing his ACL in his left knee while running on a course after the British Open. However, he opted against surgery.

In 2008, his body was starting to really catch up on him as he missed plenty of the season with four injuries. Two days after finishing second at the Masters, Woods had arthroscopic knee surgery to clean out cartilage. 

In May, he was advised to rest for six weeks, the first three on crutches as he had two stress fractures of the left tibia.

Woods has tried to return to his best form but has failed to do so due to on-going injuries

In 2017, Woods had to withdraw from the Omega Dubai Desert Classic with back spasms

Just eight days after winning the US Open in June, his last major triumph, he had surgery to repair the ACL in his left knee by using a tendon from his right thigh before injuring his Achilles tendon in the right knee in December.

In 2010, Woods has to withdraw from the Players Championship on the Sunday with a neck injury, and it was later revealed he had a flamed facet joint.

It was from this point on that Woods' injuries started to move further up his body. After a couple more Achilles tendon and knee problems, he suffered a left elbow strain during The Players Championship in 2013.

Following several problems with his back, it wasn't before March 2014 that the American, who announced he would sit on the sidelines for the Masters, had surgery on his back in Utah for a pinched nerve.  

In September the next year, Woods' back injury returned and he required a second micro-discectomy surgery to remove a disc fragment that was pinching his nerve.

Woods' golfing career was starting to unravel around him and shortly after came a third back surgery in October, in which he had a procedure to 'relieve' discomfort. 

But finally, in April 2017, the Ryder Cup star underwent his fourth back surgery called spinal fusion, which appears to have proved a game-changer, to alleviate the pain in his back and leg. 

The 14-time major champion has suffered with injuries in his knees and back for years now

Woods carefully bends down to remove his ball after injuring his back at the Barclays in 2013

And now one year on, after falling outside the top 1,000 in the world and dealing with personal problems, Woods returns to Masters as a strong favourite, looking to claim his fifth green jacket.

Woods' is swinging like he hasn't done for years now and even on the practice range the players are so astonished they’re asking him if he thinks it advisable for them to have fusion back surgery so they can swing the club harder. 

Speaking ahead of his comeback, Woods said: ‘I’ve gone from my leg collapsing on me and lying on the floor for long periods because my back couldn’t support my weight to swinging the club faster than I did in my prime.

‘I wish I could explain it to you. When they explained the fusion surgery to me I thought that was it, that I was going to have a nice, comfortable life but that I would never swing the club like I could speed-wise.

‘But all of a sudden the pop is back and now I’m going to be out here for a very long time because I have my length back to compete on any course. That is a miracle, isn’t it?’ 

It may be a tough ask for Woods to pull off arguably the greatest major victory ever but if he can manage to compete deep into Sunday, then it would be a huge golfing success for the 14-time major champion.
