Anthony Joshua's had an easy ride to the top claims Joseph Parker

As if battling Anthony Joshua for the unified world heavyweight championship in front of an 80,000 crowd and on live television in 180 countries is not exciting enough, Joseph Parker's next trick will be building an adventure playground.

With or without the several belts at stake in the Principality Stadium, Joshua's Kiwi rival will hasten home from Cardiff after their Saturday night spectacular to begin that construction work in his back garden in South Auckland.

'The materials for the project have already been delivered to a new house I've just bought,' says the holder of the WBO heavyweight title. 'I'm itching to get on with it.'

Joseph Parker (right) claims he has had a tougher journey to the top than Anthony Joshua 

With his wife expecting their second infant daughter in June - 'which is why she can't be with me at ringside as usual'- the amiable holder of the WBO heavyweight title could take his time over the playground. Except that he wants first use.

'I've told my trainer Kevin Barry that we'll be out there before the children,' he says. 'One part of it is a fantastic trampoline which is shown in the ads with a car parked on top of it.

'It's a big springs-free job to minimise the risk of anyone getting hurt. I want to get it in place quickly so me and all my team can jump up and down together celebrating my victory over Anthony.

'The first pleasure for me will be putting it up. I love construction. I won a building scholarship as a boy, when my mum kept telling me that every boxer needs a fall-back plan in life.' 

Parker (left) warms up ahead of the world title fight with Joshua as he plans to add to his belt

That advice was cemented into his builder's psyche as a teenager when he was selected to box for New Zealand.

Parker explains: 'Unlike the kind of financial support Anthony received on his way to winning his Olympic gold medal, our government doesn't put much money behind amateur boxers. I'm thankful that after I missed out on the Olympics, DUCO Events (his promoters) invested in me. Otherwise I wouldn't be here this weekend.

'We've put in the hard yards. Boxing is tough but it was tougher for me to make it than it was for Anthony. That is a part of the motivation for me to beat him but other things matter more.' 

The Kiwi will return home to build a playground in his garden for his infants, win or lose

As a New Zealander of Samoan heritage, Parker adds: 'First off I want to make myself, my family, my team and my two countries proud. If I can do that by winning this fight, it will be a boost to boxing in NZ and the Polynesian Islands as well as bring in the support other fighters need.

'There are a lot of great fighters back there but they don't get the recognition they deserve or the chance to prove themselves by boxing abroad or being financially able to focus on the sport.

'My success is having an impact, already. It will be all the greater after I beat Anthony and become the unified champion. The most important thing about the different paths we have taken is that they have brought us together at the right time to find out who is the best heavyweight in the world.' 

Joshua holds three belts and will look to add a fourth when he faces Parker on Saturday night

Parker's attempt at adding Joshua's WBA, IBF and IBO belts to his title will be compulsory viewing on breakfast television in New Zealand this Easter Sunday morning. 

He says: 'The fight won't start before 8.45 am and there will be huge crowds gathered around public screens back home and in Samoa and the islands. They have been sending loads of messages and they will be praying for me.' 

Parker also expects to have the support of a sizeable minority of his fellow countrymen in the huge Cardiff crowd. 

He says: 'A lot of folk have made the long trip over. It's getting to be a bit like All Blacks rugby.'

The fight is set to land both their highest purses to date, with Joshua earning £20million

This is a 26-year-old man of simple pleasures. When not hammering out the 24 victories which have brought him here as an undefeated world champion, his fists are turned not only to construction work but to playing the piano - Queen's Bohemian Rhapsody is a favourite - fishing and other sports.

The cauliflower ear is a trophy from playing rugby at lock forward.

For the moment, he is more serious about a fight than ever before. So much so that he thinks he is more intense than Joshua - 'albeit less tense.' 

He says: 'When Anthony says he is fully focused on me we have to believe him. Although that doesn't gel with him talking about his next fight.' 

Surprisingly, shortly before the biggest fight of his life, Parker has undergone surgery to both elbows 'to free up my punches.' He has lost weight dramatically in order to gain speed of hand and foot.

That quickness for a heavyweight has been perceived as his biggest asset and he denies that the weight loss will detract from his other weapon, a knock-out punch.

Parker has lost weight for the fight in order to increase his speed and agility against AJ

He says: 'Speed is power.' With both champions receiving their highest purses yet - Joshua in the region of £20million - Parker might add that power is money.

While AJ will continue building his business empire between fights, Kiwi Joe will get on with putting up his expensive amusement park.

Most importantly they both appear to be having fun, which is what sport is supposed to be all about.

Parker v Joshua will be televised live this Saturday night on Sky Sports Box Office.
