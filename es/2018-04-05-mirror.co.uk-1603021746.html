Liverpool made City look ordinary - they can do the same to Barcelona and Real

Just a few months ago Liverpool were being looked at as little more than a stepping stone to greater things.

Spanish giants Barcelona, not for the first time, used their financial might to prise the Reds supposed best player away from them.

On Wednesday night, as the rest of Europe looked on, Liverpool made runaway Premier League leaders Manchester City look pretty ordinary, even vulnerable, and sent out a message that they are capable of doing this to anyone – even Spanish giants.

Madrid might have Ronaldo and Barcelona might have Messi, but Liverpool have got Mo Salah, an incredible player who would walk straight into either of those sides – without question.

One question is whether Philippe Coutinho, who cost Barcelona big in January, would walk back into this Liverpool side right now. There are serious doubts about that and he certainly hasn’t been missed, which shows how extraordinary Liverpool’s form has been.

It is important to emphasise it was only the first leg and if any team knows how quickly 3-0 leads at the halfway stage can evaporate it’s Liverpool – see Istanbul 2005 or Sevilla a few months back for evidence of that. But as the Reds showed after half-time, it’s much easier to hang onto a 3-0 win than a 1-0 win.

Jurgen Klopp will be working hard to ensure there isn’t even the slightest hint of a ‘job done’ attitude from his squad, who have an old-fashioned and no doubt bruising derby to contend with before the second leg, against Sam Allardyce’s Everton.

But for Reds fans, well, how can you not feel like this is one of those moments in LFC history that will be talked about for years to come, not only for what happened but also for what it led to?

Steven Gerrard’s “you beauty” of a goal against Olympiakos, ‘supersub’ David Fairclough overcoming the odds against St Etienne – is Alex Oxlade Chamberlain’s rocket against Manchester City going to be mentioned in the same breath in stories passed down from parent to child?

But it isn’t just about Man City. That was the evidence on the bigger stage, under the most scrutiny, but the evidence has been there a while. Liverpool beat Spartak Moscow 7-0, Porto 5-0 and now Manchester City 3-0.

Remember when all the talk was about Liverpool’s leaky defence?

As in the second half, it is starting to look like maybe, just maybe, they’ve finally started to plug those holes.

There will be times when the front men have to make up for errors at the back, but that applies to any club, even those European giants. The key is that Liverpool are nowadays not making as many mistakes – but when they do, their attacking players are more than capable of making up for it.

Against City Liverpool were three goals up within half an hour and not for the first time this season their opponents looked shell-shocked.

Pep Guardiola is just not used to this, but he isn’t the first opposition manager walking to the dressing room at half time with that look on his face.

If the script was that it would be a game with many goals then it was inevitable that at least one of them would be scored by Mo Salah.

His opener puts him on an incredible 38 for the season so far and it seems strange to look back now to the summer when he was being written off by many as a failed Chelsea winger.

Salah’s early departure holding his groin will be a huge worry for Klopp, but, just like there is more to Barcelona than Messi or to Madrid than Ronaldo, there is far more to Liverpool than Mo Salah. The Reds are far from a one-man team.

Sandwiched in between Salah’s goal and the third, from Sadio Mane, was one from Oxlade-Chamberlain that was as worthy of a mention in any ‘all-time greatest Champions League goals’ lists as the one from Ronaldo the night before.

What will really make any of the goals scored this week truly great is what they ultimately lead to at the end of the competition, but Liverpool fans have every right to dream big, whoever stands in their way between now and the final.

Getting into the next round is, of course, far from a done deal and unlike the tie against Porto, where Liverpool won the first leg 5-0, there will be very few Reds fans assuming that a place in the semi-finals is a formality.

City are dangerous and Liverpool absolutely have a fight on their hands. But which of the two sides is now feared the most in Europe?

Klopp has carefully curated a squad that meets his needs pretty much perfectly. The flair at the front is matched by the newfound air of confidence at the back.

For those who haven’t been playing close attention since the turn of the year, the second half display was almost like the unveiling of Klopp’s best-kept secret and, perhaps, the one that above all else gives the Reds its best chance of big silverware in years. Liverpool are actually very good in defence, something that has maybe slipped under the radar.

Not only do Liverpool have players who can terrorise any defence in the world, they now have a backline that doesn’t scare quite so easily as in far too many recent years.

There is no argument about how much of a difference Virgil van Dijk has made to how the Reds protect their leads and his presence has undoubtedly made life easier for Loris Karius as he establishes himself as the Reds’ No.1.

Even though van Dijk, like all humans, is prone to the odd mistake the Reds defence as a whole is able to cancel them out, working with some kind of hive mind to defend that goal against all odds.

Dejan Lovren has become like a new signing, such is his new-found confidence and work rate and the determination of both full-backs was off the scale.

Not for the first time though – Andy Robertson has been a revelation this campaign and showed it yet again in this encounter.

Trent Alexander Arnold came in for some stick after the win over Crystal Palace and nobody would have criticised Klopp had he decided to protect the youngster and leave him out. Instead he started and instead he banished any ghosts that might have haunted him from the weekend.

Trent will grow into a far better player by being given not only the chance to make mistakes but the opportunity to learn from them.

From front to back this Liverpool side have all the hallmarks of being able to stand alongside all the greats in the long and glorious history of the club. More importantly, far more importantly, they have all the hallmarks of a side that can stand alongside the greats of the game here and now and become legends in their own right.

There are no easy sides left in the Champions League, but that includes Liverpool, and Jürgen Klopp’s side are rapidly turning into the side that will be feared most of all.
