Inside story of Stuart Olding and Paddy Jackson rape trial that rocked rugby

The car parking fees alone faced by Paddy Jackson’s parents during his 42-day trial approached £1,000.

But that pales into insignificance when compared to the costs to reputations, physical and emotional health, faith, trust... and of course hundreds of thousands of pounds in legal fees.

The trial of Paddy Jackson, Stuart Olding, Blane McIlroy and Rory Harrison may be over in terms of verdicts but their journey back to a normal life is just starting.

No one knows if Jackson and Olding will play for Ulster or Ireland again.

On the other side a woman who said she was raped walked away defeated.

The trial split the country and saw almost everyone take sides for almost 10 weeks. Here we look at the key players in the trial of the century.

The jurors

Those who lasted the course had been on duty for almost 15 weeks.

The eight men and three women who delivered their verdict started their work on January 29.

One was discharged, two suffered a bereavement, four became ill and one cancelled a holiday. Yesterday they were excused for life from jury service.

The legal teams

While there were a few skirmishes they largely remained friendly and respectful of each other. Some delivered their cases with flamboyance, some with a calm assurance and others with plain-speaking.

The judge

Judge Patricia Smyth displayed patience, concern for and respect of the jury and determination to display for fairness and transparency. She was charming, polite and at times fierce in her protection of the trial.

The complainant

The 21-year-old testified behind curtains over eight days. She could not see the defendants, the court, the judge or jury.

She could not see the press or the public gallery. While granted lifetime anonymity, she was named in court, her face was seen by the defendants on a plasma screen while testifying, her face could be seen from the gallery, she could be seen in CCTV from Ollie’s night club.

The defendants

They maintained an appearance of calm. Harrison and McIlroy rarely changed expression.

While Jackson’s appearance seemed least tense, it was Olding who appeared to wear his heart on his sleeve. Face drawn and pale, at times dark circles under his eyes.

Defendants’ families

They showed grit and unending support. Some engaged with the media with kindness, others ignored reporters and photographers until the four were cleared.

The witnesses

The trial heard from medical experts, police, friends and family. Dara Florence, Clare Matthews and Emily Doherty’s night out in June 2016 catapulted them back to court 19 months later with vital information.

The complainant’s friends revealed a composed and self-sufficient young woman with strong support from her peers but it was obvious they hurt deeply for her. The experts appeared cool and clear under cross examination. Each spoke earnestly with confidence.

Social media

The trial sat on a social media knife-edge with Twitter spewing almost every word. On Facebook, readers carried out their own mini trials boxing themselves into corners, arguing the same points ad nauseum and baracking and berating each other.

The social media input was described as toxic and vile – it was at times worse, it was at times potentially dangerous.

Victims of serious crime

The PPS, police and Nexus urged sex assault victims not to be put off by the trial and come forward if they have been harmed.

Protests by women’s groups to show support for victims will be held today.

The press

The media were coralled into eight press seats and the rest shared the public gallery. They worked around the clock to feed the demands of their readers and viewers.

The only moment of lightness

The day had been tense, the mood strained. But just as Judge Smyth stepped away from the bench and everyone rose, a mobile phone erupted to the strains of Ennio Morricone’s The Good, The Bad and the Ugly.

The judge smiled as she waked out, the legal teams dived for their phones and laughed, the public gallery erupted into noise and confusion.

Finally, as if given permission to breathe, the defendants looked at each other and smiled.
