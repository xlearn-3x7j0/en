Savings rates could be about to get better

Nationwide Building Society. Halifax. Coventry Building Society. Three big names in the world of savings that in recent years have gone a little shy.

The trio, along with some of the biggest names in banking, had disappeared from the independent This is Money savings tables and gone into hiding.

Not just disappeared from the top of the table - but dropped out of them altogether.

They have been more keen on offering low mortgage rates than good savings ones, especially as they have been able to tap into cheap money in the form of the Funding for Lending Scheme.

Wakey wakey: It's been a while since the big banking boys have been in the independent This is Money tables

Nothing has highlighted this more than Isa season – a time, traditionally, between March and May, in which banks and building societies would battle for your money.

But in the last few weeks, the three providers have emerged, yawning and stretching from their slumber and re-entered our cash Isa savings tables for the first time in what feels like an eternity.

Firstly, Nationwide launched a 1.4 per cent Isa for existing customers – or 1.3 per cent for new ones, via its single-access tax-free account. 

Secondly, Halifax now tops our five year tables with a 2.25 per cent Isa rate. Then, Coventry revealed a one-year fix paying 1.4 per cent.

Okay, none of these beat inflation - but nothing in our savings tables does. 

Meanwhile, a five-year Isa is a niche product, while the Coventry deal can be beaten by a few other deals in our tables.

However, these launches are significant and I'll whisper it: they show that some of Britain's savings powerhouses may finally be on the lookout for a trickle of cash from savers.

The current tax year ends on 5 April and starts on Friday. 

Could more banks and building societies be tempted into offering better tax-free deals for the 2018/19 financial year?

We pointed out in February that things were looking at little rosier this cash Isa season - and there have been murmurs about the Bank of England raising base rate imminently. 

In February, we said that providers in the Isa market currently do not have to compete as hard as they would if they attempted the same tactics in the non-Isa market, where competition among providers is more fierce. 

A glut of challengers have been battling in the fixed-rate savings arena for cash, but not Isas, as they are slightly more complex financial product to launch and run.

It appears cash Isas might be the first savings pond some big financial fish are willing to circle in once more.

Tax-free savers are a good target - many have large sums of cash built up over the years, looking for a new home. 

I wouldn't be surprised to see another high street name or two launch some better rates in the coming weeks. I hope this isn't a false dawn for savers.

WHAT ARE THE BEST CASH ISA RATES?

The deadline is ticking to fill your allowance before the 5 April deadline. The Nationwide account pointed out above offers 1.4 or 1.3 per cent, depending on whether you've been a customer for a year or not.

This beats the best buy easy-access deals - but only one withdrawal is allowed each month.

For a straight forward easy-access tax-free deal, the best rate is 1.25 per cent from both Paragon Bank and Shawbrook Bank.

The top one year rate is 1.46 per cent from OakNorth, two year 1.7 per cent from Al-Rayan and three year 1.85 per cent from Virgin Money.

As pointed out above, the best rate fixed until 2023 is Halifax, offering 2.25 per cent.
