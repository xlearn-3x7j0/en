Manhattan lawyer loses luxury penthouse in bizarre lease technicality

A New York lawyer will be kicked out of his luxury apartment overlooking Central Park because he renewed his lease using a corporate name.

Barry M Fox paid $25,000 a month for a palatial 3,300-square-foot penthouse covering a whole floor of 12 East 88th Street on the Upper East Side.

The partner at law firm Cleary Gottlieb lost his case to keep the rent-controlled apartment in the Appeals Court on Tuesday.

Lawyers for his landlord, which sought to evict him and develop the building, claimed he renewed the lease as a company to write his rent off on tax.

'It would seem obvious that Fox's intention in this scheme was to be able to write off his rent as a deduction on his income taxes,' they wrote, which he dismissed as 'nonsense'.

Mr Fox moved into the building in 1975 and expanded his home to the whole floor, including a 1,900-square-foot terrace, when his neighbour died in 1996.

He said due to ill health he agreed in 2008 with owner Nostra to renew the lease under the business name MBE Ltd, of which he was sole trustee.

However, in 2014 developer 12 East 88th St LLC bought the building to convert it into condominiums, now selling for $3 million to $9.5 million.

The company told Mr Fox his lease would not be renewed after it expired on May 31, 2014, and he could buy it for $25 million.

Mr Fox immediately hired lawyers and discovered the apartments were rent-controlled, which he was never aware of when he signed the lease.

Negotiations to buy the penthouse for a reduced price of between $5 million and $9 million failed and the case went all the way to decision.

Mr Fox argued the landlord illegally deregulated the apartment and he should be able to demand a lease renewal as he had previously.

But four of the five First Appellate Division judges ruled he gave up the right to a rent-controlled lease when he started using a corporation to renew it, as his name was never actually on the lease after that.

'From that time onward, Fox ceased being a tenant and no longer had any of the rights to which a rent-stabilized tenant is entitled,' they wrote.

Mr Fox will reportedly appeal the decision to the New York Supreme Court.
