Good morning on this pleasant Thursday.

“Beware the ides of March.”

That Shakespearean warning, which foreshadowed the assassination of Julius Caesar, sounds less ominous centuries later on a sunny day in New York.

In fact, these days it seems downright positive, as it gives us a reason to remember the playwright’s legacy in our city.

“It’s really important to underscore that not only America’s Shakespeare — but New York’s Shakespeare — is different than the Shakespeare you get in England,” said James Shapiro, a professor of English at Columbia University who compiled the anthology “Shakespeare in America.” “And its history here, over the last 200 years, has been extraordinary.”

Among the first people to bring Shakespeare to New York, in the 18th century, were touring companies from London. During the Revolutionary War — even while British forces occupied our city, Professor Shapiro told us — there was Shakespeare being performed here.

Theaters began popping up in Lower Manhattan in the early 1800s, and by midcentury, America had its own Shakespeare celebrities, like Edwin Forrest and Charlotte Cushman, an actress who played male leads (Romeo, Hamlet and Lear, to name a few).

“It’s hard to give a sense of the superstar, international status of these people, who were the Madonnas or Jay-Zs, if you will, of the Shakespeare community,” Professor Shapiro said.

And although Shakespeare’s writing bridges centuries, languages and communities around the world, three seminal moments in its influential history took place in New York City:

The Astor Place Riot of 1849, which left more than 20 people dead after spiraling out of a rivalry between two Shakespearean actors — one American (Mr. Forrest) and one English (William Macready) — in competing productions of “Macbeth.”

A benefit performance in 1864, when John Wilkes Booth and his brothers performed “Julius Caesar” to raise money for the creation of a Shakespeare statue in Central Park. Months later, Professor Shapiro said, Booth quoted the play in his justification for Abraham Lincoln’s assassination. “If they hadn’t done that benefit,” Professor Shapiro said, “I don’t think the play would have been in his head, and who knows whether the course of the country’s history would have been different.”

And a citywide controversy in 1916, over whether New Yorkers should be charged admission to Central Park for performances commemorating the 300th anniversary of Shakespeare’s death. “It involved every ethnic group in New York City,” the professor said, “and it was really about trying to assimilate immigrant New York into the dominant culture embodied by Shakespeare.” (That debate is part of the reason we have free Shakespeare in the Park today.)

“New Yorkers never tire of Shakespeare; he’s really crucial here in ways that he has not been in England and, for that matter, everywhere else in the world,” Professor Shapiro said, adding, “We’ve done a lot for Shakespeare and shaped the way that Shakespeare is understood.”

And Finally …

If the ides have you feeling inspired to set off on a Shakespearean scavenger hunt, here are a few treasure troves around the city:

In Manhattan: Shakespeare in the Park, a free-for-all Shakespeare festival hosted by the Public Theater, has been running at the Delacorte Theater in Central Park since 1962. (On tap this season: “Othello” in May and June and “Twelfth Night” in July and August, starring everyday New Yorkers from all five boroughs.)

The Public Shakespeare Initiative, now a collaboration between the Public Theater and the Shakespeare Society, hosts performances, talks and other events in NoHo. (Here are a few in March, April and May.)

Meanwhile, New York Classical Theater, which performs the works of Shakespeare and other classical playwrights, is gearing up for a run of “Romeo and Juliet.”

The New York Public Library for the Performing Arts houses an archive of records (scripts, programs and more) from the New York Shakespeare Festival, founded by Joseph Papp in 1954.

And the Players Club on Gramercy Park South, founded by the Shakespearean star Edwin Booth, was Mr. Booth’s home in the late 19th century (and where he died in 1893). Today a national landmark rich with Shakespearean artifacts, it remains a club for theater and entertainment professionals.

In the Bronx: For more than a decade, the Hunts Point Children’s Shakespeare Ensemble, now part of the Public Shakespeare Initiative, has delivered Shakespeare education to young enthusiasts in the South Bronx. (This spring, students will perform “As You Like It.”)

In Brooklyn: Theater for a New Audience at Polonsky Shakespeare Center offers plays, workshops, lectures and other activities in Fort Greene. (“The Winter’s Tale” opened this week.)
