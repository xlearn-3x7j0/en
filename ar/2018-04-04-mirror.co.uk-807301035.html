Spieth admits being close to panic about his game two weeks before the Masters

Jordan Spieth claimed he has left “a panic place” to arrive in Augusta with his old Masters form, writes Neil McLeman in Augusta .

The Open champion has an extraordinary record here, with two runners-up places – including nearly winning on his debut - and a 2015 victory from his first three appearances than 11th place last year.

But the world No.4’s pre-season preparations were ruined by a December illness and he missed the cut at the Valspar Championship last month following a first-round 76 as he struggled with his usually-deadly putting.

Back home in Texas for the Houston Open last week, though, the 24-year-old finished tied third after finishing his final round with a 29-foot putt.

“I come in after a good week last week so I can use that as momentum,” Spieth said. “My belief is very high. I feel really good about this week. I made big strides in the last two weeks to get from kind of a panic place to a very calm, collected and confident place.

“And it’s difficult to do in two weeks. Sometimes it takes years. And I feel like I’ve been able to speed that process up a lot over the last couple weeks.

“I don’t shoot 5‑over very often and you’re like, 'What the heck happened?!' And then when you get a few tournaments in a row where I can’t set my putter down and I look up, they’re not looking at the same place.

“Sometimes I get that for a day, sometimes it’s a couple weeks, and I was getting it for a couple months. It was like: ‘What the in the world is going on? I was getting frustrated.

“I spent most of December in bed very sick, so I couldn’t do all the basic stuff, like alignment, and I was trying to play through it. There was a lot of pictures and stroke comparisons, and it’s getting there.

“I feel better coming into this week than I did in 2016 and 2014 where I came off of missed cuts in Houston. So settling into that from round one will be important.

“Over the last four years I have found my way into the last group or second last group on Sunday and nothing beats that 2.45 tee-off time there — waking up in the morning and understanding you have got a chance to win the Masters.”
