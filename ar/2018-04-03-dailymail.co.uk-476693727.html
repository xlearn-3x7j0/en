The Dow Jones looks set to gain 113 points after opening this afternoon after a dip yesterday.

US markets expected to rise

'Currently the Dow Jones is set to rise 40 or so points once the bell rings on Wall Street, something that may help take the edge off the European losses.

'Now that kind of rise doesn’t mean much considering the Dow shed more than 2% on Monday; however, given the size of that drop, the avoidance of any further losses may as well be tantamount to an actual rebound,' Connor Campbell of Spread Ex has said ahead of markets opening this afternoon.

Flybe profits hit by 'Beat from the East'

Flybe, the independent airline, has predicted that its full-year results will be eroded by the cold snap known as the 'Beast from the East'.

The airline revealed it lost £4 million in revenue after 994 flights were cancelled in February and March.

Care and assistance costs on the cancelled and delayed flights will also contribute to the company's disappointing results.

Its shares fell 0.44% on the back of the news to 34.25p.

FTSE losses ease

A slightly better than forecast March manufacturing PMI has helped ease losses on the FTSE this morning, with the index now down 0.3% against its initial 0.7% drop on opening. 

'Obviously lurking just above 7000 isn’t a great position to be in, but does suggest UK investors’ concerns have cooled following a knee-jerk reaction after the bell,' Connor Campbell of SpreadEx said.

'This isn't the case in the Eurozone, where another month-on-month manufacturing slowdown for the region only served to exacerbate the region’s already macro-mangled trading.

'The DAX saw its already notable decline widen to more than 1%, taking the German index back below 12000, while the CAC dropped 0.6% to dip under 5150.'

Still a 'long way' to go in Fox's Sky takeover bid

Ian Forrest, investment research analyst at The Share Centre, comments on what the latest Sky-Fox takeover update news means for investors.

'Fox agreed to pay £10.75 per share for Sky in December but the deal has run into a number of issues since then and been complicated by a £12.50 offer from rival Comcast.

'Today, Rupert Murdoch’s mass media business highlighted how it had ‘worked diligently with the CMA throughout its extensive review and it went on to say that it believes the ‘enhanced firewall remedies proposed to safeguard Sky News comprehensively and constructively addressed the CMA's provisional concerns.

'The latest proposals attempt to address media plurality concerns in the UK and the shares responded positively today, but there is clearly still a long way to go in such a complicated situation.

'With the price currently trading above both offers, lower risk investors may want to lock in recent gains, others should hold.'

Tech stocks to blame for market downturn

Neil Wilson, a market analyst at ETX Capital, said: 'A limping and bruised US tech sector appears to be chiefly to blame, whilst the escalation of trade sanctions by China means tariffs continue to weigh on market sentiment.

'Amazon came in for more flak from president Trump, while Tesla is managing to do itself no favours. Intel had a terrible day as Apple said it would use its own chips. It looks like there is just no good news out there for the tech sector at the moment but it’s also a fact that valuations were stretched and being priced for perfection means any bad news will produce an oversized effect.

'Indeed in terms of the wider market we can read too much into causes – sparks can come from anywhere – the reason this was so combustible in the first place was because valuations were crazily high. Even with rising earnings mean reversion was always going to force stocks back down to earth.

'On tech’s impact on the market, it’s a case of the star performers suffering and this has a big psychological effect on sentiment. It’s not just that they have been behind the bulk of the gains in recent years and therefore exert an outsized effect on indices when they sell off; there is also a kind of network effect on other stocks.'

Can manufacturers maintain softer growth?

Rob Dobson, director at IHS Markit which compiles the manufacturing output data released today, said: 'The latest PMI survey provided further evidence that UK manufacturing has entered a softer growth phase so far this year.

'Although the pace of output expansion ticked higher in March, which is especially encouraging given the heavy snowfall during the month, this was offset by slower increases in new orders and employment.

'The key question is whether growth can now be sustained, albeit at a lower level, into the coming months. On that front the news is generally positive.

'Manufacturers are still reporting solid inflows of new work from domestic and overseas markets. Business optimism is holding steady at an elevated level, with over 54% of companies expecting output to expand over the coming 12 months.

'With cost inflationary pressures also moderating to provide some respite for margins, the sector looks set to make further slow and steady progress as we head through the spring.'

Manufacturing output rose in March

UK manufacutring output rose marginally in March, up to 55.1 from a level of 55 reported in February. 

Output growth had picked up, but the upturn in new orders slowed according to Markit/CIPS. It also found input cost and output price inflation both remained slow.

European markets fall

Europe has been hit by the market falls today, too.

The German Dax is down 1.18% at 11,953 – a fall of 152 points this morning – while the French Cac is down 0.72% at 5,130 – a loss of 36 points.

What happened on Wall Street last night?

Traders dumped stocks on Wall Street last night as the world lurched closer to a full-blown trade war.

The Dow Jones Industrial Average fell by 1.9 per cent, or 459 points, after the Chinese slapped tariffs on £2.1 billion of US exports, including politically sensitive food products.

It is feared this could be just the start of a damaging tit-for-tat battle between the two superpowers.

It takes the Dow into correction territory as it is now more than 10 per cent below its previous peak earlier this year. The index closed at 23,644.19.

Spotify to take first step into public market

Music streaming app Spotify will list on the New York Stock Exchange today, a first for the company. 

It has opted, unusually, for a direct listing without the help of investment banks - handing the market more freedom to set its price. 

Laith Khalaf, a senior analyst at Hargreaves Lansdown, said it was likely to be a choppy float given the company hasn't yet turned a profit.

He says: 'Indeed it may be some time before Spotify is profitable, as the company is resolutely committed to prioritising growth over profit, and will be channelling money into investing in services for its users, and building on its scale.

'This makes sense as a business plan, but with profits in the long grass it does make the stock particularly susceptible to swings in sentiment, and to any perceived failure to deliver on growth.'

Two possible futures for Sky News

Sky News could be sold off to Walt Disney or ring-fenced in plans released by 21st Century Fox as it attempts to buy out the media firm.

Fox, which is attempting to buy the 61% of Sky it does not already own, faces a number of regulatory hurdles after the UK’s competition watchdog found the £11.7 billion deal was 'not in the public interest'.

It has now proposed either a legal separation and comprehensive ringfencing of Sky News or a sale of the loss-making channel to Walt Disney, which is itself attempting to acquire Fox’s entertainment assets.

The sale of Sky News to Disney would take place 'whether or not Disney’s proposed acquisition of 21st Century Fox proceeds', Fox said on Tuesday.

It comes as the Competition and Markets Authority weighs up whether to recommend Fox’s deal for Sky to Culture Secretary Matt Hancock, who must decide to either approve or block it.
