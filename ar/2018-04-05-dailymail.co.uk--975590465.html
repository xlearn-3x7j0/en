140 cows make two-mile journey to give birth on an uninhabited island

This incredible video shows more than a hundred cows making a two-mile journey across a beach to an uninhabited island to give birth.

Angus MacDonald, his wife Michelle, both 53, and their family take the cows from the mainland to the island of Vallay on the Scottish Outer Hebrides.

They make the stunning journey so that the cows can give birth on clean ground outdoors.

The island of Vallay is a small uninhabited island that is only reachable by the beach at low tide.

Mr McDonald said that the cows love the beach because they are free to roam and run around it.

He said: 'We do the two-mile walk twice a year. We move them so they can calve in April then back to graze on the heather hills in October.

More than 100 Highland cows were herded to the uninhabited island of Vallay in the Scottish Outer Hebrides so they can give birth

'They totally love the beach. They are totally free and run around and eat seaweed and play in the sea.'

Mr McDonald said that he has kept Highland cows for almost 50 years, since his mother had a croft in the 1970s.

The MacDonald family now run Ardbhan Highland Cattle, a herd of purebred cattle whose bloodlines date back to 1885.

Their herd now numbers 140 cows including three new calves.

They are all owned by Angus McDonald, his wife Michelle, both 53, and their family who take the cows out

The cows are sold all over Europe with interested bidders coming from Italy, France and Estonia. 

Mr McDonald and his wife live on the mainland but they hope to retire to the island of Vallay. 

They hope to repair an old house that is on the deserted island.

The cows have become a hit on social media and have been gaining a lot of attention after Mr McDonald's daughter-in-law Carianne posted stunning footage of the cows on their Facebook page.

Mr McDonald added: 'We keep the cows organic and the soil is fertilised with seaweed brought in by the Atlantic storms'.

'The herd of Highland cows have been a well-kept secret but not anymore.'
