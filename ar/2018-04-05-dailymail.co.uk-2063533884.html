US streets where civil rights leader Martin Luther King is remembered

A collection of photographs has been compiled that captures the many communities that have grown around streets named after the late civil rights leader, Martin Luther King, Jr.

Susan Berger, a Tucson-based photographer, spent more than a year taking photos in 33 cities in 17 states plus the District of Columbia. Her pictures featured in an exhibition last year.

The idea for the photo series came came during Berger's travels around the U.S. for another project. 

 Its is estimated that about 900 streets across the US have a Martin Luther King memorial -  photographer Susan Berger has photoed many of them including this one in Jersey City, New Jersey 

Beginning in October 2009, Berger picked an airport, then search on Google Earth for the Martin Luther King-named streets in the surrounding towns that she could reach in the allotted time - usually about three weeks per region. This shot was taken in Macon, Georgia

Oftentimes the graffiti commemorating Martin Luther King is difficult to spot. Here his famous 'I have a dream' speech appears as graffiti on a wall in Chicago, Illinois

Berger went on to take photos of MLK streets and boulevards in Chicago, Milwaukee, Newark, Philadelphia, Seattle and New York. She went to Macon, Georgia; Beaumont, Texas (Houston is photoed above)  Shreveport, Louisiana; Little Rock, Arkansas; Jackson, Mississippi; Birmingham, Alabama and more. In the end she visited 17 states and the District of Columbia

She told Chron.com that she encountered Luther King signs in urban and rural areas.  

'I was surprised to see one even in the middle of farmland,' she said. 'I started wondering about all the streets that have been named in honor of Dr. King and decided I wanted to see what they looked like.'

Perusing through the gallery of images, one of the key findings Berger made was the element of diversity. 

Berger said that after her travels throughout the U.S. she had reached the conclusion that while legally MLK's 'dream' had been realized, with new  laws regarding voting and housing, 'there’s no question that racism still exists in this country and changing people’s hearts and minds has become a much more difficult task.' This street sign is in Washington DC

Berger noticed some similarities among the MLK streets she was photographing around the U.S. Many were in predominately black neighborhoods, and often the local art and businesses would feature King's words and images- this street sign was photoed in Jersey City, New Jersey

Berger made a point to visit those cities most closely associated with the Civil Rights movements. In some of those places, the placement of MLK streets testifies to his enduring local significance - this image was taken in Savannah, Georgia

A taco restaurant Berger photographed in 2009 on King Boulevard in South Los Angles has a mural of King standing next to the Pope - her fist stop on her MLK was  Los Angeles where a street that used to be called Santa Barbara Avenue was changed to Martin Luther King Avenue

On her travels, Berger approached each street with no agenda and no preconceptions. She had one rule - she had to have her feet on the road named after Martin Luther King. She made a specific  point not to get waylaid by the surrounding neighborhoods - this graffiti was photoed in Newark 2010.

'All of my memories of the civil rights movement are in black and white.' Berger said. 'I recall the newspaper photographs and the newsreels. 'Even the television news was in black and white. 'My photographs are black and white, recalling the imagery from that period.'  This image of a house adjoining a road named after MLK was was taken in Shreveport, Louisiana

'It is significant, I think, that I can travel all over the country and find these MLK-themed streets,' Berger said. 'The image and message of Dr King is a unifying one, with cities in the south and cities in the north honoring him in similar ways. 'Except for the weather and the foliage, you’d be hard-pressed to know the difference between the cities.' The above image was taken in Seattle

Often streets named after MLK - such as this one in Little Rock, Arkansas - run through a park or is a country lane, 'perhaps signifying that his name has become as mainstream as Abraham Lincoln’s or George Washington’s', Berger said

Many MLK named streets - such as this one in Montgomery, Alabama - tend to be lower income areas with higher rates of racial segregation than the national norms. Some of her photos reflect this truth in broken chain-link fences and abandoned parking lots

Berger said that she did not interact with local people as much as she thought she would at the start of her tour - 'We’ve become such an automobile-dependent society that often there are no people on the streets,' she said

Some were found in urban cores, others were found off the beaten path, some were in communities that had been directly impacted by King's teachings and activism.

'For the most part, those are lower-economic neighborhoods; however, the people I met were proud of the neighborhoods.'  

Berger's travels also brought her to Texas.

Just south of the 610 Loop, Martin Luther King Boulevard runs through the community of South Park. 

Houston City Council officially designated the roadway in 1978. She noted a church was present in the photo, a common hallmark of many MLK streets around the country, as well as growing Hispanic influences.

In Galveston, their version of Martin Luther King Boulevard has the distinction of being one of two streets named after prominent African-American men. 

The other street was named after the boxing legend Jack Johnson.
