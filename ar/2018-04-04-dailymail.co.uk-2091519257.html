Ben Whittaker sets sights on amateur success at Commonwealth Games

It was May of last year when Anthony Joshua and Floyd Mayweather, the two money-making behemoths of the generation, laughed and joked together in London's late spring sun.

Hundreds of fans had queued up to be photographed between the pair but one of those patient punters needed no introduction to the world heavyweight champion. 

As the tall, lithe figure of Ben Whittaker approached, the ever-relaxed Joshua could not contain his excitement. 'See this guy here,' Joshua said to Mayweather. 'He is the future. 'This is Ben Whittaker. Bad boy fighter. I swear to you, this guy here is the crème de la crème. He can fight, look out for this guy.'

Ben Whittaker is making name for himself as a future British boxing star on the amateur circuit

Mayweather, never one to miss an opportunity to make a quick buck, looked up at the 6ft 3in middleweight and said: 'When are you turning pro? After the Olympics? Oh Hell no! Trophies do nothing but collect dust.'

But the 20-year-old has decided to swerve the American idol's advice and has instead dedicated his life to the pursuit of amateur success. Then, much like Joshua, he hopes to reap the rewards as a professional.

'I've had a few little offers, some people might have taken them,' Whittaker told Sportsmail. 'But this is an apprenticeship here with GB, I'm still young. I can win or lose here.

'Come 2020 I'll be the perfect age and the promoters will want me even more. By then, they will be fighting over me so why turn over now?

The 20-year-old is preparing for the upcoming Commonwealth Games in Australia

'I remember hearing that Joshua got offered £50,000 to turn over way before he did. As a 20-year-old, if I got the same offer I'd think: "50 grand? Give me that now". 'But really, where I'm going 50 grand is nothing. I'm putting my life on the line. If I keep winning, that 50 grand can turn into a million.'

Whittaker's next stop is the Commonwealth Games where he is the 75kg selection in the 12-strong Team England out on the Gold Coast. He is also being groomed for success at Tokyo 2020.

The West Bromwich-born starlet, who took home silver from the 2015 Commonwealth Youth Games, is one of the best kept secrets in boxing and arguably the hottest middleweight prospect on the planet. He does the hard yards from Monday to Thursday alongside the likes of Joshua at the English Institute of Sport, Sheffield. In fact the world heavyweight champion often asks him for advice.

Whitaker was described as the 'crème de la crème' by boxing icon Anthony Joshua (left)

Whittaker said: 'We have a little laugh. He seems to be a fan of me, he likes the way I box.

'It's kind of funny because I'll be putting my wraps on and he will say: "So Ben, in your last fight, what did you do with that jab?" He's asking me for tips!

'People see him on TV and ask me 'what's he like? Is he too big for his boots?' He is one the humblest guys ever. He has been down the same path as us so he knows what we're going through. If you have any problems you can ask him.

'He's living the dream but he comes in here and trains hard every day. We see what it takes to get there.'

Although he won gold at the Olympics in 2012 and a silver at the 2011 world amateur championships, the 2010 Commonwealth Games came too early for Joshua but Whittaker believes they will form an integral part of his own development. 

Advice from AJ has seen Whittaker dedicate his life to the pursuit of amateur success

'It's a different bubble because there is attention on it,' said Whittaker, who will have his first bout of the tournament on Sunday after getting a bye in the first round.

'The smaller countries will see an English opponent as a scalp so they raise their game. A lot of these guys don't usually come out of their countries, but in the Commonwealth Games you see so many upsets because some of the lads might go there and be complacent.

'I see it as like a big shop window. I go to international tournaments usually and nobody even knows. A few weeks ago I went to Hungary and won gold, got best boxer of the tournament, but nobody can watch it.

'But there is a focus on the Commonwealth Games so people can get to know you a bit and I want to make an impression.

'Everyone is hungry so you have to treat every fight like an Olympic final. I'll do that with every bout until I've got that gold medal.

'With that mentality I believe I'm the one to beat.'

His flashy, fan-friendly style seems ready-made for the professional game but he says it was only honed as a result of watching hours of one of history's great amateur boxers.

Whittaker is open to the education he will receive in the 'apprenticeship' of boxing

'I started boxing when I was seven, although I didn't really like it,' he said. 'My dad always used to sit me down and make me watch videos of Cuban boxers. I think that comes across in my style. I hit and don't get hit.

'He used to take me out in the garden to do drills. But he wouldn't let me throw punches, it would just be foot drills. I got my feet ready first so the punches could come later.

'I used to watch a lot of Mario Kindelan who beat Amir Khan in the Olympic final. He's my favourite ever boxer, his style was beautiful. He could have a fight and he could box – that was the perfect style.'

And what about Mayweather? 'Oh yeah, of course,' Whittaker said. 'Meeting him was great and for Joshua to say those things about me to him was just unreal.

'He started from nothing and he's made it really, everyone in boxing looks up to what he achieved. 'Seeing him in real life, having a little laugh with him and Joshua, it made me think I can't wait to be a pro and mix it with these people.

'But I know I've got a lot of work ahead of me before I can think about that and it all starts in Australia.'
