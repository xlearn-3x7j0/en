Memphis Rally Embodies Dr. King’s Activist Spirit

MEMPHIS — Thousands of people gathered in the streets here on a sunny yet brisk morning on Wednesday to commemorate the 50th anniversary of the assassination of the Rev. Dr. Martin Luther King Jr.

More than simply recollecting or celebrating his life, though, many of those who converged on the city where he was fatally shot are hoping to channel Dr. King’s spirit as they work to make progress on his signature issues — racial harmony and economic justice.

A rally organized by labor unions drew a crowd carrying signs with messages like “Fight Poverty” and “We got nothing to lose but our chains.” A banner hung high above a stage that read “#I AM 2018,” a nod to the “I Am a Man” slogan that Memphis sanitation workers used when they were striking in 1968, a cause that prompted Dr. King to travel to Memphis a half century ago.

“Dr. King coming down 50 years ago, and connecting economic rights and racial justice, civil rights, human rights — he was able to connect all of those kinds of things,” said Lee Saunders, president of the American Federation of State, County and Municipal Employees, one of the co-founders of the “I Am 2018” movement. “We are commemorating what happened 50 years ago. But more importantly, after we leave this place, we’ve got to take this feeling, we’ve got to take this energy back to our local communities across the country.”

Among the political luminaries who turned out for the rally were Senator Bernie Sanders and Representatives Sheila Jackson Lee and Barbara Lee.

Many activists expressed concern that Dr. King’s legacy might be sanitized as someone who simply advocated peace. They said they hoped that this moment would provide a chance to highlight the forceful lengths to which he was willing to go — like resistance through civil disobedience — to overcome racism.

To many remembering Dr. King, the situation remains dire today, with poverty, systemic racism and economic inequality still plaguing the country. Memphis is the poorest large metropolitan area in the country.

“He probably would feel sad about Memphis, as so many other places where he did a lot of his work,” said Bernice King, one of Dr. King’s daughters. “The concentration of this poverty that we as a country never really confronted, we haven’t dealt with.”

Ms. King and her brother Martin Luther King III essentially endorsed today’s social justice movements — from Black Lives Matter to #MeToo — as extensions of their father’s work. Speaking on Tuesday evening at the Mason Temple, where Dr. King gave his final public address before he was killed, Ms. King said that her father had planned to preach a sermon titled “America may go to hell.”

“It’s time for America to repent,” Ms. King said, before a fervent and rapt crowd.
