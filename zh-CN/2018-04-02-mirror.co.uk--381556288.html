All you need to know about the Commonwealth Games 2018

The XXI Commonwealth Games take place on the Gold Coast from April 4 to April 15.

A sprinkling of top stars including Adam Peaty, Max Whitlock and Caster Semenya will be present when the Commonwealth Games kick off in Australia.

Four years ago the athletes descended on Glasgow for a hugely successful competition and this time the home nations head for the warmer climes of the Gold Coast expecting to bring back a serious medal haul.

Unlike at the Olympics, there is no Team GB, rather England, Scotland, Wales and Northern Ireland take part as separate nations.

Here is everything you need to know about the Games...

Day-by-day guide

Day one: Thursday, April 5

England's Brownlee brothers will be seeking to continue their dominance in the triathlon. Wales' Non Stanford and England's Vicky Holland will be vying for medals in the women's event. There could be an Ashes battle in the team pursuits at the velodrome, where Wales' women could be in the medal mix with the inclusion of Dani Rowe.

Day two: Friday, April 6

English swimmer Ben Proud will aim to add the 50 metres butterfly gold to the world title he claimed last year. English cyclist Charlie Tanfield competes in the four-kilometres individual pursuit, having set the second fastest time by a Briton and fourth quickest in all in late 2017.

Day three: Saturday, April 7

Adam Peaty of England will aim to win another Commonwealth Games gold, having dominated the 100m breaststroke since Glasgow 2014. English gymnast Max Whitlock will be keen to win the men's all-around final. Ryan Owens of England competes in the men's sprint at the velodrome, where Scotland's Jack Carlin, the world silver medallist, will be a rival.

Day four: Sunday, April 8

The highlight of the opening day of athletics competition will likely be the 100m heats and semi-finals, featuring Jamaica's Yohan Blake. Whitlock is in action again, on the floor and pommel horse. England shooter Amber Hill competes in the skeet. Scotland's Hannah Miley will hope to be in the women's 200m individual medley final, although the 400m is her stronger event in the pool.

Day five: Monday, April 9

The 100m finals, with Blake and fellow Jamaican Elaine Thompson the favourites, take place and squash ace Nick Matthew will bid to complete his career on a high in the singles gold medal match. Peaty will likely go in the 50m breaststroke, targeting another gold, while Wales' Jazz Carlin will be a contender in the women's 800m freestyle, although she has recently switched focus to 10km open water events.

Day six: Tuesday, April 10

The men's 400m final takes place without Olympic and world champion Wayde van Niekerk due to injury. But his fellow South African Caster Semenya will be the likely favourite in the women's 1500m final. Botswana's Isaac Makwala will be eager to impress in Van Niekerk's absence. Proud will be seeking success in the 50m freestyle, while in road cycling the individual time-trials take place.

Day seven: Wednesday, April 11

Olympic champion Shaunae Miller-Uibo will hope to add the Commonwealth 400m title. The diving events begin, with England's Alicia Blagg seeking to win the synchronised 3m springboard title she claimed with the now-retired Rebecca Gallantree in 2014.

Day eight: Thursday, April 12

The men's and women's 200m finals take place on the athletics track, with the Jamaicans again expected to dominate, although England's Adam Gemili and Dina Asher-Smith will hope to challenge. Johnson-Thompson begins her heptathlon campaign. England's Jack Laugher will seek to claim the 3m springboard title in the diving pool.

Day nine: Friday, April 13

The women's heptathlon concludes with Johnson-Thompson seeking to add the title to her world indoor pentathlon crown. Laugher and Chris Mears dive for England in the synchronised 3m springboard, with Tom Daley and Daniel Goodfellow in the synchronised 10m platform event. Boxing reaches the semi-final stage, as does the men's hockey competition and the squash mixed doubles event. The rugby sevens tournament begins.

Day 10: Saturday, April 14

Athletics competition concludes on the track with the men's and women's 4x100m and 4x400m relays, when England will be seeking glory. Daley will bid for another Commonwealth Games 10m platform title. The women's hockey tournament reaches a conclusion and boxing finals take place. There is also the cycling road race, where 30-time Tour de France stage winner Mark Cavendish is slated to compete for the Isle of Man.

Day 11: Sunday, April 15

The final day sees finals in badminton, basketball, netball, rugby sevens - where England and New Zealand will be among the favourites - doubles squash and table tennis, plus the closing ceremony.

How to watch the Commonwealth Games

The Games will be shown on the BBC with daily coverage starting at midnight UK time.

Five Brits to watch

Katarina Johnson-Thompson (Team England, Athletics)

The new World Indoor pentathlon champion heads into her second major competition of the year having finally broken the golden barrier, even if it was without world and Olympic champion Nafi Thiam. Previously failures at the World Championships and Olympics have cast doubt over whether the 25-year-old had the mental strength to seriously compete for titles. She admitted the issue was in her head following her first world title and has targeted one gold and another medal between the Commonwealth Games and August's European Championships. She will go into the heptathlon as favourite having proven she can handle the pressure.

Adam Peaty (Team England, Swimming)

The 50m and 100m breaststroke world-record holder remains the man to beat. His international breakthrough came at Glasgow 2014 when he won two gold and one silver medal. Since then he has claimed the 100m breaststroke Olympic title and a silver in the 4x100m medley, while taking five World Championship crowns. The 50m and 100m breaststroke titles arrived at the British Championships this month to underline the 23-year-old's dominance.

Amy Tinkler (Team England, Gymnastics)

The 18-year-old is making her Team England debut on the Gold Coast. She won Olympic bronze on the floor to became the first British gymnast to claim a medal in the event and, along with Beth Tweddle, is the only British female gymnast to win an individual Olympic medal. She has also just claimed the floor title at the British Championships this month, having been British all-around champion in 2015. Tinkler also helped win a team bronze at the 2015 World Championships.

Dai Greene (Team Wales, Athletics)

The 2011 world champion squeezed into the Wales squad by one-hundredth of a second in February. His time of 49.50 seconds in the 400m hurdles in Potchefstroom, South Africa was almost two seconds off his personal best. Injuries meant, before that, he had completed just one race since June 2016. It will be interesting to see how the 2010 Commonwealth champion fares in Australia when there is little expectation and pressure on him to succeed.

Eilidh Doyle (Team Scotland, Athletics)

Fresh from her 400m bronze at the World Indoor Championships, Doyle heads to Australia in fine form. She clocked a season's best time of 51.60 seconds to make the podium in Birmingham in March - her first individual global medal. The 31-year-old has won two silver medals in the 400m hurdles in Delhi and Glasgow and is looking to go one better in the hurdles this time. She will also run in the 4x400m relay in what is likely to be her last Commonwealth Games.
