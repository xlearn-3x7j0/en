Workers battle to save Jakarta - the fastest sinking city on earth

The Lost City of Atlantis may be little more than a fable but for the residents of Jakarta, the watery fate of that metropolis is an all-too-real threat.

That is because the Indonesian capital is sinking faster than any city on Earth, by up to 25cm per year in some places, with experts warning large parts of it will be underwater within a decade.

Ministers are currently building sea walls to shield the residents from disaster, but that does little more than delay the inevitable, since it is not the ocean rising but the ground dropping that is causing the problem.

Jakarta, the capital of Indonesia, is the fastest sinking city in the world, with parts of the metropolis plunging by up to 25cm per year. Pictured is a sea wall in northern Jakarta, the worst affected part of the city, with a house sitting outside that has already sunk into the ocean

The port city has 13 rivers which drain into it and receives 60 inches of rain per year, but despite the abundance of water, the threat largely comes because the ground underneath their feet is sinking rather than the waters rising

The root cause of Jakarta's problems comes from a lack of piped drinking water, meaning residents rely on private wells which are draining aquifers on which the city rests. As these are drained they deflate, causing the sinking

The government is attempting to fix the problem by building sea walls to keep residents dry, but this is merely delaying the inevitable, since the walls also rest upon the sinking ground

Under normal circumstances the aquifers would refill with rain water, but 97 per cent of Jakarta is covered with concrete or asphalt, meaning this runs off into rivers or the ocean instead, further exacerbating the problem

Another problem is the city's lack of proper waste disposal or sewage system, meaning its rivers and canals - built by Dutch settlers - have become clogged and cannot drain properly

Hydrologists estimate that northern Jakarta (pictured) and its four million residents will be fully submerged within a decade if no permanent solution can be found to the problem

The root cause lies in an almost complete lack of piped drinking water in a city of some 10 million people, meaning residents rely on private wells to draw water up from aquifers on which the city rests.

As the aquifers drain they deflate, like removing stuffing from a cushion, causing the land above to sink. 

While the aquifers would refill under normal circumstances, with Jakarta receiving an average of 60 inches of rain per year, around 97 per cent of the city is covered with concrete and asphalt, meaning it cannot drain through.

Instead, that water runs off into the 13 rivers which feed into the city, or the ocean, compounding the problem. Heavy concrete also weighs the land down, speeding up the descent.

While the problem has been known about for some time, it went largely ignored and was widely underestimated until floods in 2007 - which killed 50 and displaced 300,000 - brought it into focus.

JanJaap Brinkman, a hydrologist working on Jakarta's water problem, told The Guardian: 'We studied the data and looked at the mapping, we discovered the city was sinking not perhaps by a centimetre or so per year, as had previously been thought, but by 5-10cm on average, and much more in places.'

Since then various plans have been proposed to deal with the issue, including a show-stopping $40billion sea wall in the shape of a giant bird akin to Dubai's palm islands, but progress has been painfully slow.

In December last year the grandiose plan was scrapped in favour of a more modest sea wall, which is now under construction, according to the Jakarta Post. 

But there is still no blueprint for tackling the root cause of the problem, nor any concept of how long it might take, despite hydrologists warning the New York Times that northern Jakarta, with its 4million residents, could be underwater within a decade.
