Ivanka to Latin America to promote 'women's economic power issues'

Ivanka Trump will be a part of her father's official delegation next week as he makes his first trip to Latin America since taking office. 

A senior official said Thursday that the first daughter who volunteers her time as an unpaid adviser to President Trump will be making the trip to Lima, Peru, with the administration for the Summit of the Americas.

'She'll be championing women's economic power issues in the region,' the source said.

First lady Melania Trump is not going on the trip, her spokeswoman said.

Her husband Jared Kushner wasn't on the list of White House officials attending, most likely because his foreign policy portfolio has revolved around problems in the Middle East.

With no secretary of state to stand by his side now that he's fired Rex Tillerson, the president will also be accompanied on the trip by the acting secretary John Sullivan.

Trump's pick to replace Tillerson, the current CIA Director Mike Pompeo, awaits Senate hearings and likely confirmation. 

Commerce Secretary Wilbur Ross and USAID administrator Mark Green will come down separately from the White House, an official said, and participate in summit meetings respective to their responsibilities. 

Melania Trump sat out her husband's first foreign trip of the year, a quick there-and-back in January to Davos, Switzerland. 

Her absence then was chalked up to scheduling conflicts that amounted to a visit to the Washington, D.C. Holocaust Museum and a Florida spa trip.

In Peru, her husband is scheduled to deliver a speech at the Summit of the Americas and hold bilateral talks with unspecified regional leaders.

An official said he'd hold joint events in Colombia with President Juan Manuel Santos and meet U.S. embassy staff.

Little else was offered about Trump's schedule while he's in either country, including whether he will meet with Mexican President Enrique Pena Nieto.

Trump lauded Mexico on Thursday morning for breaking up a caravan of migrants headed to the United States' southern border.

But the two leaders have had their fair share of differences over Trump's proposed border wall, and a National Guard deployment to the United States' territory in the southwest threatened to create fresh tension in the relationship.

Asked about the prospect of a meeting Thursday, a U.S. official said, 'We are continuing to work to make sure that good communication is achieved between our Mexican partners and ourselves. 

'And of course the issue of immigration and transnational crime are an important component in the US' vision for Latin America in terms of protecting the U.S. homeland, and of course transparency and accountability throughout the hemisphere.'
