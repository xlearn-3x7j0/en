Can you tell which salad has 600 calories and which has 1000?

When making a salad at home, it's easy to think that whatever you put into it, it's a healthy option.

But one dietitian has recently shown that the calories in salads can vary by huge amounts.

Taking to her Instagram page, Paula Norris explained how something that looks the same - or at least very similar - can in fact vary by as many as 460 calories.

'Unfortunately calorie heavy dressings, too much grain and not enough veg can result in a "salad" with calories closer to that of a risotto,' Paula (pictured) explained

'Unfortunately calorie heavy dressings, too much grain and not enough veg can result in a "salad" with calories closer to that of a risotto,' Paula wrote next to a comparison photo.

Whereas one of the haloumi salads comprises 995 calories, the other one includes just 535.

She also shared how the very thing you're adding and thinking are healthy can actually be 'sneaky' and adding even more fat to your meal.

The dietitian regularly takes it upon herself to illustrate how adding more of some foods and less of others can quickly rack up your calories (pictured)

Salad one 

1 cup cooked Pearl Barley

70g Haloumi

1/3 Zucchini

2 florets Broccoli

1/4 Capsicum

50g Eggplant

3 Cherry Tomatoes

200g Poached Chicken

1/2 cup herbs

Dressing of 2tsp Extra Virgin Olive Oil and 2tsp Balsamic Vinegar

Salad two 

1/2 cup cooked Pearl Barley

35g Haloumi

2/3 Zucchini

5 florets Broccoli

1/2 Capsicum

100g Eggplant

5 Cherry Tomatoes

100g Poached Chicken

1/2 cup herbs

Dressing of 2 tsp Balsamic Vinegar and 2tsp seeded mustard

This meal has no change in ingredients, but just varies the amounts included in the preparation (pictured)

'The salad on the left has one cup of cooked pearl barley, 70 grams of haloumi, 1/3 zucchini, two florets of broccoli, 1/4 of a capsicum, 50 grams of eggplant, three cherry tomatoes, 200 grams of poached chicken, 1/2 cup of herbs and a dressing of two teaspoons of extra Virgin olive oil and two teaspoons of balsamic vinegar,' Paula outlined.

On the other hand, the salad on the right contains just half a cup of cooked pearl barley, 35 grams of haloumi, 2/3 of a zucchini, five florets of broccoli, 1/2 a capsicum, 100 grams of eggplant, five cherry tomatoes, 100 grams of poached chicken, 1/2 cup of fresh herbs and two teaspoons of balsamic vinegar with two teaspoons of seeded mustard.

While it might seem like common sense, as Paula explains it's important to keep an eye on the higher-calorie foods like cheese, oil and meats, and then increase the vegetables and fresh herbs if weight loss is your goal.

'These posts are NOT to encourage calorie counting but are to demonstrate the impact that ingredient manipulation can have on the overall calories in a meal,' Paula said

This pasta meal has slightly less cheese, chicken and pasta on the right, but more vegetables like mushrooms, broccoli and capsicum

This isn't the first time Paula has explained how small changes add up. 

She recently compared two portions of fried rice that look seemingly identical and found that there can be a difference of close to 400 calories, depending what you add.

In the past, the dietitian has also looked at tacos, bowls of pasta, sweet potato salads stir fry. 

'These posts are NOT to encourage calorie counting but are to demonstrate the impact that ingredient manipulation can have on the overall calories in a meal,' Paula concluded.
