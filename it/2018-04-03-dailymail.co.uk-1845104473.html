Satellite images reveal Earth's largest cities in mesmerising detail

Amazing new satellite imagery shows skyscrapers as you've never seen them before.

The 'tilted' photographs are snapped from an angle meaning they reveal mesmerising vertical details rarely captured by orbiting cameras.

Using an array of 13 satellites, ex-Nasa scientist and data visual expert Robert Simmon said the photos are 'like getting a view out the window of an aeroplane 450 kilometres [280 miles] high'.

Mr Simmon has taken 'angled' photos of many of Earth's fascinating vertical landscapes, from Shanghai's towering Pudong district to the two-mile-high (3.3km) Mount Fitz Roy in Patagonia.

In a post to Medium, he wrote: 'The satellite imagery we're most familiar with - taken straight down - flattens and obscures the visual cues we get from perspective, making the imagery appear like maps, not photos.

'When you take an image of Mount Fitz Roy from an angle, the view becomes altogether different: The mountains rise to their commanding height, valleys regain their depth, and background features recede into the distance.

To capture the images Mr Simmon used a constellation of 13 'SkySats' operated by satellite imaging company Planet Labs, based in the Netherlands.

The gadgets, which the company hires out for mapping, archiving and analysis, are capable of collecting more than 400,000 square miles (1 million sq km) of imagery per week, according to the company's website.

In one picture, Mr Simmon captured the Pearl-Qatar, a man-made island that extends from the northern fringes of Doha into the shallow waters of the Persian Gulf. High-rise apartment buildings can be seen as a jagged ring around the Pearl's emerald green lagoons, which Mr Simmon said 'provide plenty of prime beachfront real estate'

In another snap, the SkySat constellation photographed the bustling downtown area of Houston, Texas. The group of sky-high buildings is surrounded by miles of low-rise structures and winding freeways, with Minute Maid Park - home to Major League Baseball team the Houston Astros - also visible in the photo (right of centre)

Mr Simmon said: 'Nearly continuous activity has coated the snowy slopes of Klyuchevskaya Volcano with dark ash.
