Reckless tackles are putting players in peril, warns Neil Lennon

Neil Lennon fears players are destined to suffer serious injury at the hands of Scottish football's hatchet men.

On Saturday, Celtic boss Brendan Rodgers was left furious by a 'disgraceful' and 'dangerous' stamp on Scott Brown by Ross County skipper Andrew Davies.

Davies was shown a straight red card and will serve a two-game ban, ruling him out of tonight's visit of fellow strugglers Partick Thistle and Saturday's home match against Hibs.

Celtic manager Brendan Rodgers has accused Andrew Davies of trying to hurt Scott Brown

The Ross County captain was sent off for a late challenge on the Celtic skipper on Saturday

Sportsmail understands further action against Davies from the SFA for excessive misconduct is possible but unlikely.

Last month, Brown was caught by a wild lunge by Aberdeen's Sam Cosgrove, earning the Pittodrie player a straight red card.

After the latest poor tackle on the Celtic captain, described by Rodgers as the worst challenge he has seen in Scotland since taking charge of Celtic in 2016 - Lennon has accused Davies of letting himself and his relegation-haunted County team down.

The frustrated Hibs boss believes too many players in the Ladbrokes Premiership are deliberately setting out to hurt opponents.

Rodgers decribed the tackle was 'disgraceful' following Celtic's 3-0 victory over Ross County

And, singling out his Easter Road midfielder John McGinn as another player who is regularly targeted, the former Celtic boss warned the most talented players in the Premiership could be literally kicked out of the game for good by thuggish challenges.

'There's a problem with tackling in Scotland,' said Lennon. 'I've seen some great challenges but I have also seen some horrendous ones, where players have made no attempt to play the ball - just the man.

'You don't see that very often in football nowadays, but I have seen it a lot here in Scottish football this season.

'Players have been a bit reckless and that could have been a bad one for Scott Brown at the weekend.

'It's not big, it's not clever and it's not hard. It's an easy way out.

'The Ross County player let himself down, his team down and he's going to miss two important games.

'But it's not just Scott Brown. John McGinn has taken some heavy punishment this season.

The 45-year-old boss insisted his on-field leader is being targeted by opposition players

'My worry is that a player ends up with a serious injury.

'We could lose a very good player to the game due to heat of the moment stuff, or someone's ill-discipline, or recklessness.' Lennon believes it would help if players were better coached on how to tackle properly. The former Celtic and Northern Ireland midfielder also believes stricter punishments for those putting in reckless challenges and injuring players would help erase what he feels is a scar on the Scottish game.

'What can be done about it? Better coaching and discipline? Players need to learn to stay on their feet more,' said Lennon, whose side host Hamilton Accies at Easter Road tonight.

'I know it's a man's game and all that nonsense but you need to play within the laws of the game.

'I like the speed of the game in Scotland and I like the physicality of the game here.

'And I know all about tackling. I did it all through my own career. But there is tackling and then there is deliberately going out to hurt someone - and I am seeing that far too often, at the minute.

'Referees, in the main, are very strong with that kind of stuff. On Saturday, the ref couldn't do anything but send (Davies) off for that tackle.

'Sometimes, I think that if a player goes in deliberately to hurt another player, and the injured one is out for a period of time, then the person making the tackle should also sit out for the same period of time.

'Maybe that's going too far but I don't like to see these sort of tackles. It's not part of the game. It's not good for the image of the Scottish game at all.

'Football is presented well and it's made as attractive and entertaining as possible.

'It's important that we stop all of that stuff because it's not good and it's nothing to be proud of.'
