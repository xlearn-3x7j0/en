Hospital admissions for obesity soar by 18% in a year!

Hospital admissions for obesity have risen by almost a fifth in the space of a year, official figures reveal.

NHS statistics show there were 617,000 admissions where obesity was listed as a primary or secondary diagnosis in 2016/17.

This is 18 per cent more than the 524,725 recorded in 2015/16, according to the NHS Digital data released today.

An analysis shows this is the highest number since records began a decade ago and is double the total recorded four years ago.

Primary diagnoses include weight-loss surgery and treatment, while secondary ones revolve around problems caused by obesity.

These can include hip problems, heart attacks and even sleep apnoea.

Public Health England today blasted the figures and said they show 'obesity and its associated health risks remain a significant challenge'. 

Professor Louis Levy, head of nutrition science for PHE, added that the Government-run body is working to encourage people to lead healthier lives.

He said: 'It's taken many years for levels of obesity to reach this point and change will not happen overnight.'

The new NHS Digital figures also provided a breakdown of physical activity levels and diet across England.

They showed a third of men and 42 per cent of women did not meet the recommended weekly activity levels in 2016.

And 21 per cent of men and a quarter of women were classed as 'inactive'.

Only around a quarter of adults - 24 per cent of men and 28 per cent of women - consumed the recommended five portions of fruit and vegetables a day in 2016.

Women accounted for the lion's share of hospital spells, according to the NHS data from every Clinical Commissioning Group in the country.

Two in every three patients were female for overall admissions, while three quarters of the 10,705 primary diagnoses were women.

The total primary admissions rose nearly eight per cent in the space of a year. Just 9,929 of these admissions were recorded in 2015/16. 

There were 6,700 bariatric surgeries conducted in hospitals across England in 2016/17 - 77 per cent of these procedures were carried out on women.

Bariatric surgery is an umbrella term for a group of procedures which help a person lose weight - including gastric bypasses.

More than a quarter (26 per cent) of English adults are obese.

Two per cent of men and four per cent of women are 'morbidly obese' - meaning they have a BMI of over 40.

The new statistics highlight the stark scale of the country's obesity epidemic, which is adding to the pressure facing hospitals and frontline staff. 

The NHS battled it's 'worst winter ever' this year, with soaring pressure leading to some of the lowest performance statistics ever recorded.
