Community honors Martin Luther King Jr. with song, bells

Mark Wilson was 7 when he heard that Martin Luther King Jr. had been assassinated. “It was 1968, third grade,” he says. “I remember the sadness throughout our family and throughout the world,” Wilson says. “There was so much hope and so much anger. They killed our hero.”

Mark Wilson is the director of the gospel chorus on campus. (UC Berkeley photo by Anne Brice)

To honor King’s life on the 50th anniversary of his assassination, Wilson, the director of the gospel chorus at UC Berkeley, led the campus community and friends at noon in singing “Lift Every Voice and Sing” and “We Shall Overcome” at the base of the Campanile as a carillonist accompanied from the bell tower. The bells also rang 39 times for every year of King’s life.

Wilson, also an ordained Baptist minister — the same denomination as King — says he follows King’s tradition of social justice as liberation in his teaching.

“I try to apply these values to our music in the gospel chorus,” Wilson says. “Next week, our concert will be titled ‘Where is the Love?’ He still gives a lot of hope to us and I think that’s the message that comes out in the music.”

Gia White (center), a Berkeley staff member, grew up singing “Lift Every Voice and Sing.” (UC Berkeley photo by Anne Brice)

Gia White, who has been the administrative director of the Institute of European Studies on campus for 29 years, says she grew up in the 1970s Bay Area singing “Lift Every Voice and Sing.”

“It’s the black national anthem,” she says.

For a time, White went to an all-black after school program, where they sang the song at every assembly.

“We all had to learn that song by memory. You had to stand up and cover your heart. It’s a moving and meaningful song,” she says. “It chronicles the struggles of our people, and it’s also a hopeful song and hope is really important these days.”

The carillon was played by Leslie Chan, a PhD. candidate in chemical and biomolecular engineering, who is also a student of the university’s carillonist Jeff Davis. Davis wrote the arrangements that Chan performed.
