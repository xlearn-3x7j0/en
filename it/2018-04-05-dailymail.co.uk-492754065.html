Ivanka to Latin America to promote 'women's economic power issues'

Ivanka Trump will be a part of her father's official delegation next week as he makes his first trip to Latin America since taking office. 

A senior official said Thursday that the first daughter who volunteers her time as an unpaid adviser to President Trump will be making the trip to Lima, Peru, with the administration for the Summit of the Americas.

'She'll be championing women's economic power issues in the region,' the source said.

First lady Melania Trump is not going on the trip, her spokeswoman said, citing the upcoming state dinner at the White House and the president's summit with the Japanese prime minister at Mar-a-Lago.

Melania Trump sat out her husband's first foreign trip of the year, a quick there-and-back in January to Davos, Switzerland, as well.

Her absence then was chalked up to scheduling conflicts that amounted to a visit to the Washington, D.C. Holocaust Museum and a Florida spa trip.

The summit with Japan's Shinzo Abe at Mar-a-Lago that was mentioned this time will immediately follow President Trump's foreign trip. It is scheduled for April 17 and 18 in Palm Beach. 

A week later, the Trumps will welcome French President Emmanuel Macon to Washington for their very first state dinner.

Ivanka's husband, Jared Kushner, wasn't on the list of White House officials traveling with President Trump next week, either, even though he has taken a leading role in the administration's dialogue with Mexico.

Kushner was part of a delegation last month that traveled to Mexico for meetings with the country's government, including President Enrique Pena Nieto. 

With no secretary of state to stand by his side now that he's fired Rex Tillerson, the president will be accompanied on the trip by the acting secretary John Sullivan, who will stand-in as America's chief diplomat.

Trump's pick to replace Tillerson, the current CIA Director Mike Pompeo, awaits Senate hearings and likely confirmation. The process is sure to take weeks, regardless, leaving Trump to rely on other administration officials during his conversations with foreign leaders.

Commerce Secretary Wilbur Ross and USAID administrator Mark Green will come down separately from the White House, an official said, and participate in summit meetings respective to their responsibilities.  

In Peru, President Trump is scheduled to deliver a speech at the Summit of the Americas and hold bilateral talks with unspecified regional leaders.

An official said he'd hold joint events in Colombia with President Juan Manuel Santos and meet U.S. embassy staff.

Little else was offered about Trump's schedule while he's in either country, including whether he will meet with Pena Nieto.

Trump lauded Mexico on Thursday morning for breaking up a caravan of migrants headed to the United States' southern border.

But the two leaders have had their fair share of differences over Trump's proposed border wall, and a National Guard deployment to the United States' territory in the southwest threatened to create fresh tension in the already troubled relationship.

Asked about the prospect of a meeting between the two presidents on Thursday, a U.S. official said, 'We are continuing to work to make sure that good communication is achieved between our Mexican partners and ourselves. 

'And of course the issue of immigration and transnational crime are an important component in the US' vision for Latin America in terms of protecting the U.S. homeland, and of course transparency and accountability throughout the hemisphere.'

It was also unknown as to whether Trump would meet with Mexican President Enrique Pena Nieto, who is currently up for reelection, during the Summit of the Americas

Speaking more broadly, the official said the focus of Trump's trip would be on boosting regional security, disrupting transnational criminal organizations and illicit trafficking of drugs and humans and promoting fair and reciprocal trade. 

Peru is one of 11 nations that just signed onto the Trans-Pacific Partnership that Trump rejected. 

Trump will also promote the U.S. as a 'partner of choice' for Latin America to 'push back against extreme economic aggression in the region, the official said in a swipe at China.

Good governance, transparency and leadership in the region are also at the top of Trump's agenda at the summit to which Venezuela was disinvited from over Nicolás Maduro's power grab.

Maduro has said he will attend anyway, despite repeated warnings that he's not welcome.

However, A U.S. official previewing Trump's trip on Thursday said the Venezuelan leader, who insists he's the victim of a U.S.-led conspiracy against him, is unlikely to attend.

'We expect that President Maduro will not show up,' the official said. 'I think that would be a very good sign that the region is serious about focusing on good governance and democracy.'
