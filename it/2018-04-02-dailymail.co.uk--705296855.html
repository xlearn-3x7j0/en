Terrifying image of Pacific spiny lumpsucker reveals its bony skeleton

There aren't many fish that would be called 'cute'.

But that's exactly how the Pacific spiny lumpsucker is described by people who are lucky enough to have spotted one. 

Now, one photographer has discovered a dark side to these 'adorable' fish.

Under fluorescent lighting, the Pacific spiny lumpsucker transforms into a terrying 'demonic' creature.

This is the terrifying face of a demonic Pacific spiny lumpsucker fish that lives in the cold waters of the North Pacific. With its seemingly empty eye sockets and hateful stare, this unmodified image reveals this creature's bony skeleton in incredible detail

The bizarre image was posted on Twitter by Leo Smith, an associate professor of ecology and evolutionary biology at the University of Kansas.

'I was quite surprised by how evil it looked once I prepared specimen', Dr Smith told MailOnline. 

Spiny lumpsucker fish commonly live on rocky sea beds and float in seaweed. 

They have an almost spherical head and body. Instead of scales they have plate-like structures with shiny lumps called tubercles to defend against predators, writes Live Science.

Although this one might seem satanic they are generally quite cute-looking, with wide mouths, large lips and big protruding eyes.

Dr Smith wanted to capture this creature's unusual skeleton. 

'It was that disconnect between the evil and the normally cute that resonated with me', said Dr Smith. 

'That's why I put it up on Twitter because I thought it was a good picture and my fish colleagues would be equally surprised by the disconnect. 

'I mean this species is about as cute as a fish can be', he said.

WHAT IS A PACIFIC SPINY LUMPSUCKER FISH?

Spiny lumpsucker fish commonly live among rocky sea beds and seaweed in the cold waters of the North Pacific

Spiny lumpsucker fish commonly live among rocky sea beds and seaweed in the cold waters of the North Pacific.

They grow up to 18cm in length and feed on a variety of crustaceans, worms and molluscs.

Instead of scales, these spherical fish have plate-like structures with shiny lumps called tubercles. They are bony in order to protect them from predators. 

Normally a shade of brown or green with yellow and orange highlights, these fish have wide mouths with large lips and big protruding eyes.   

They are often found in eel grass beds, rocky areas with kelp and other algae growth, writes Aquarium of Pacific.

They attach to solid objects using suction cups. 

Females lay eggs in deep depressions in rocks which are then cared for by the male.

He attaches to the rocky surface around the eggs and aerates them with oxygen by waving his fin. He also keeps them safe from predators such as crabs and small fishes.

These fish are solitary by nature and normally swim alone, using rocks and seaweed for hiding.

Researchers put a dead lumpsucker in a large glass jar held in place with gelatin and stained the specimen with fluorescent dye.

Everything else in the picture is black because it is not fluorescent.

'I chose the Pacific Spiny Lumpsucker because it is a more rotund, so that it's face would be cuter', Dr Smith said.

'Most fish don't look straight ahead like we do, so it is hard to get images where the eyes are even that visible, so the chubbier fishes would be better.

'I also thought the spiny tubercles would fluoresce well', he said.  

During winter these creatures are normally found in deeper water, reaching depths of 500 metres (1,600 feet).
