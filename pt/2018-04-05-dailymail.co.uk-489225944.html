Female top aide to Pruitt who helped bring down Rob Porter resigns

Samantha Dravis, a close aide to embattled EPA Administrator Scott Pruitt and senior policy advisor to him has resigned her post.

The aide dined with Pruitt and also worked for him in a previous post at an association of state attorneys general and another group.

The move comes after the White House declined to say President Trump had confidence in Pruitt, who has been buffeted by press accounts of pricey travel, an unusual living arrangement, and efforts to engineer big raises for two close aides.

Dravis also played a role in the ouster of former White House staff secretary Rob Porter, who left his post after DailyMail.com revealed abuse allegations against him by one of his ex-wives.

She previously dated Porter.

In November 2017, she phoned White House counsel Don McGahn to inform him of abuse allegations against Porter by two of his ex-wives, Colby Holderness and Jennifer Willoughby, CBS reported. 

According to a report in The Hill, a source with close knowledge of Dravis' relationship with Pruitt called the news a 'five alarm story.'

A source told the paper they had a close friendship, and described Dravis as an 'enabler' for Pruitt.   

'That's his best friend there with whom he had dinner with several times every week. If he had a bad idea he'd go to her with it, because she'd just do it instead of raising concerns,' the source told the paper. 

Dravis was the associate administrator of the EPA's office of policy. In addition to working for Pruitt at the AGs group, she worked with him at the Rule of Law Defense Fund. Pruitt chaired both organizations. Both groups battled the EPA its regulations to reduce carbon emissions.

A source told CBS her call to the White House was the first time McGahn had been informed of the nature of the allegations against Porter, who was serving as a close aide to Trump and who was charged with steering sensitive materials to the president's desk.'

Porter had been working on a temporary security clearance while the FBI conducted its own investigation of his background and interviewed people who knew him, including his ex wives.

The Washington Post reported that the departure of Dravis was unrelated to Pruitt's ethics problems, citing other agency officials. It reported she was leaving to join the private sector, but gave no new position she had obtained. 

'It has been an honor to serve in this role at EPA, and I am enormously grateful for the opportunity,' Dravis told CNN in a statement. 'I wish Administrator Pruitt and all of the public servants at EPA the very best.'

Pruitt on Wednesday denied a report that he went around the White House to engineer raises for two friends and aides who joined the agency from his home state of Oklahoma.

'I did not know that they got pay raises until yesterday,' Pruitt said. 

His comment follows a report in the Atlantic that he 'ordered' the two staff aides get raises. Dravis was not one of the aides mentioned in the story about raises.

The report said after the White House rejected an effort to get raises for the two political appointees – a sign-off was required – Pruitt's team found a way to push through the raises anyway using an obscure provision of the Clean Water Act.

'Pruitt ordered it done,' the Atlantic reported. 

The two aides got raises of $57,000 and $28,000, under a provision from 1996 amendments to the law usually used to bring on specialists.

On a day Pruitt navigated the contentious interview, White House press secretary Sarah Sanders declined to express confidence in him, pointing only to past achievements.

Asked if Trump had confidence in Pruitt, Sanders responded: 'The president thinks that he's done a good job, particularly on the deregulation front. But again we take this seriously and we're looking into it.'  

 Pruitt also got grilled on his $50-a-night lease at the home of the wife of a lobbyist who does energy work.

'This was like an Airbnb situation,' he told Fox.

When asked if Trump was okay with what has been reported about Pruitt's lease, Sanders said: 'The president's not. We're reviewing the situation. When we have had the chance to have a deeper dive on it, we'll let you know.' 

Kevin Minoli, an EPA ethics official who cranked out a March 30 report saying Pruitt's lease did not constitute a gift, says in a new memo that his letter was only a narrow review of the lease itself – not any outstanding circumstances.

'The review addressed the terms of the lease as they were written in the lease agreement only.' But evaluating whether the space was used consistent with the lease 'would have required factual information that was not before us and the review does not address these questions,' he wrote April 4.
