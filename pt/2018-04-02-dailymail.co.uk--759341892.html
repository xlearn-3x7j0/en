West Brom begin search for new manager following Alan Pardew's sacking

West Bromwich Albion plan a broad search before appointing a new manager after bringing an end to Alan Pardew's tortuous four-month reign on Monday.

The board held discussions with Pardew following Saturday's loss to Burnley, an eighth in succession in the Premier League, and a parting was agreed.

As revealed by Sportsmail, Pardew had an end-of-season break clause in his contract and it is understood a financial settlement has been reached to see a termination now.

Assistant John Carver - who had a verbal altercation with a fan during Saturday's game - has also left, with first-team coach and former player Darren Moore placed in temporary charge for the remaining six games.

West Brom are 10 points from safety after Pardew managed just eight points from 18 matches, with only one victory, and planning has already begun for the Championship.

Chief executive Mark Jenkins used Saturday's programme notes to insist major changes to the structure of the club will be undertaken in the summer. A massive overhaul of underperforming players is also expected.

West Brom looked at replacing Pardew earlier this season, particularly before and after defeat to Huddersfield, but decided no suitable candidates were available. Marco Silva opted against an approach while a number of names put into the club were outlandish, including Sven Goran Eriksson.

The Baggies have identified Ostersunds manager Graham Potter as a possible successor

The British boss enjoyed success with Ostersunds and played Arsenal in the Europa League

The search now begins in earnest for a permanent successor to Pardew, with Ostersunds manager Graham Potter, Leicester assistant Michael Appleton, and Aberdeen boss Derek McInnes under consideration.

Nigel Pearson and Dean Smith have support, while Mick McCarthy, who is leaving Ipswich this summer, also presents a viable option but his association with Wolves poses a problem for a board looking to unite the fanbase.

It is understood West Brom will take time over an appointment, holding talks with a number of prospective candidates before making a decision in good time ahead of pre-season. There is no current threat to technical director Nick Hammond, who was influential in appointing Pardew.

The 56-year-old had been battling major dressing room dissent since Chris Brunt and Claudio Yacob criticised the tactics in heated scenes after the Huddersfield loss on February 24.

Senior players failed to buy into Pardew's methods, particularly upset at his stern approach to younger team-mates, which included a stinging rebuke of 19-year-old Sam Field in front of the team after the defeat at Manchester City. Field had been ill that week.

Pardew also took an aggressive approach with 20-year-old Oliver Burke and tried to get the Under-18s moved away from the training ground due to space.

At the same time the manager was alarmed at a lack of togetherness. Often the group gave excuses and not action. Some left the training ground after sessions with unseemly haste.

The departure of assistant head coach Ben Garner, who was popular among players and led certain pre-match meetings, should also not be underestimated.

Leicester assistant Michael Appleton is under consideration as West Brom begin their search

Pardew was certainly denied the chance to inject the team with new faces of his choosing. Last summer's spending meant Daniel Sturridge was West Brom's only January signing and that was a move led by chairman John Williams and chief executive Martin Goodman. In hindsight, it has proved a disaster, costing the club £50,000 for each of Sturridge's 78 minutes on the field.. Grzegorz Krychowiak has been another £110,000-per-week miss.

Sturridge, out with a hamstring injury, has only started one game - at home to Southampton - and that was a selection that saw players doubt Pardew because he dropped the in-form Jay Rodriguez.

The Barcelona trip will go down as the motif for this season. Nobody could have predicted four senior players allegedly stealing a taxi during an early morning visit to McDonald's and Gareth Barry, Jonny Evans, Jake Livermore, and Boaz Myhill badly let down the club.

But there had been reservations beforehand about going abroad in the slender gap between a Monday night match at Chelsea and a Saturday FA Cup tie against Southampton.

The desired team spirit was unlikely to find fertile ground in Spain when many wished to spend Valentine's Day with their partners. Some suggest Pardew also letting his hair down filtered through to a group who under Tony Pulis, would be coached to and from the evening meal when on a club tour.

Barry's visit to a student nightclub before Saturday's game was another bad call from an experienced professional and a reminder that Pardew's authority had been eroded.

It was in the week of the Barcelona trip that Williams and Goodman paid with their jobs for the decision to sack Pulis, who owner Gouchuan Lai now believes might have kept the club up even though he had failed to win in 10 Premier League games.

Certainly Pardew's record is excruciating. At his best he is a progressive coach and compelling communicator but proved a poor fit at the Hawthorns.
