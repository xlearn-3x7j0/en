How Guardiola showed Klopp and Liverpool too much respect with team selection

They both stood, hands in pockets, pushing the boundary of their technical area...intent it seemed, on pressing only forward.

As an image, it summed up perfectly the approach of both Jurgen Klopp and Pep Guardiola to this pulsating, at times breath-taking contest. No small box, no mere white line, could contain the ambitions of either men.

Klopp was all pensive emotion, his usual flamboyant, wild gesturing locked more firmly in a tense, almost pained inhibition, the occasion seeming to restrict the excesses of his charismatic nature. Even as the goals rained in during an astonishing first half, the trademark celebrations were not evident.

Guardiola conversely, was the opposite. He was of course as immaculate as ever, the pin sharp wool of his crisply cut outfit in muted tones of grey and black...colours which belie the imagination of the man.

Yet those dull hues were not reflected in his actions on the bench, as the excitement of the opening minutes when City tore at Liverpool and seemed about to tear them apart, made way for a more confused, baffling contest.

Guardiola began with encouragement, with exaggerated applause for his side as they put their opponents under so much pressure.

Even when Liverpool opened the scoring against the run of play, the Spaniard was still smiling, still screaming encouragement and still waving them forward.

Yet that instinct, that desire always to go forward eventually allowed gaps to appear. City seemed still in control at one down, seemed still capable of winning at a canter, yet when Liverpool did break, suddenly there was panic.

It appeared first in a wild attempted clearance from Walker which gave Salah the opening goal, and more obviously when Alex Oxlade-Chamberlain strode too easily before retreating defenders. Panic, blind panic followed.

That second goal on 21 minutes changed the nature of the contest, and changed Guardiola’s expression. Suddenly, there was the tension and then frustration, anger even at his team.

Such obvious criticism of his side is not Pep’s way. Even when they are struggling – rarely of course – he is always positive, always urging them to maintain their gameplan, to get forward. Now though, he was throwing his arms in exasperation.

There were even screams of anger at his players. When they started to hack wildly in clearances, when they stopped passing and started simply getting rid of the ball, there was a demonic look in his eye, and some of Klopp’s animated gesturing. Quite possibly some Spanish curses too.

Maybe they were directed at himself, because Guardiola clearly got his starting line up wrong. The half time scoreline said as much...screamed it in fact.

Klopp before the game had said his opposite number was the best coach in the world, was near perfect in his approach, and always knew exactly what he wanted to do. But when Liverpool scored their second, City were suddenly clueless.

Perhaps Guardiola showed too much respect in return to Klopp, the one manager in the world who has a winning record over him. Certainly, the decision to ditch Raheem Sterling and go with a slightly more defensive line up suggested as much.

As it unravelled, there was a real feeling that Guardiola had not just got it wrong, but spectacularly so. One City pundit, showing the glum pessimism which once was the club’s trademark, suggested that somehow Stuart Pearce had returned as manager, such was the lack of an obvious shape or purpose.

City of course, are too good to be so clueless for long, and they rallied even before half time to suggest there was still plenty of fight left in this tie.

Guardiola had his work cut out at half time though, which his team so lacking in shape, and lacking in even a basic belief at the back, as the sight of Vincent Kompany simply launching the ball testified.

He didn’t immediately send on Sterling, as though reluctant to admit his mistake, but instead pushed De Bruyne much higher up the pitch, and at least exerted more control, suggesting normal service could still be resumed.

As for Klopp, even at 3-0 he knew it wasn’t over, his arms wrapped tightly around his chest saying as much.

When Salah left the pitch early in the second half, those arms went back into the pockets and his shoulders slumped… because he knew it immediately changed the balance of the contest.

Where Guardiola was inhibited by the Eygptian’s presence, now he could attack at will, throwing Sterling immediately on, and abandoning any cover on the flanks.

It was that sort of a contest after all.
