Good morning on this snow-flecked first day of spring.

In anticipation of the ritual cleaning that comes with the season, we wanted to meet the two New Yorkers doing more than most to keep our city pest-free.

Nemo and his partner, Mickey, both pint-size beagles, are the only two canine members of the Department of Housing Preservation and Development’s code enforcement team. Their job: track down bedbugs with their highly trained noses.

We met Nemo yesterday after he was picked up from Biscuits and Bath on the Upper East Side, where he lives, and before he and his handler of six years, David Trevino, headed out for their first inspection of the week.

The canine unit is dispatched to city apartments after a resident complains to 311 about a possible infestation.

Once Nemo arrives, and after a few belly rubs from adoring children, he gets to work with the command: “Find the bugs!”

He’s trained to pick up the scent of bedbugs, and only bedbugs, and he scours headboards, couches and corners for the little bloodsucking critters. If he finds them, he sits down.

“Then I say, ‘Show me, show me,’ ” Mr. Trevino said, “and he indicates the location of the bugs with his nose.”

His reward? A Bil-Jac chicken liver treat.

“He’s accurate about 80 percent of the time,” Mr. Trevino said, a hit rate that has led some in the past to complain about using dogs to inspect for insects.

But Mr. Trevino defended his co-worker. Instead of exploring an entire apartment, he said, the dogs help him narrow his search.

And they are fast. An inspection takes about 20 minutes, or a matter of seconds if it’s a full-blown infestation.

“That’s when the dog walks in and is like, ‘Whoa,’” he said, mimicking Nemo by throwing his head back and crinkling his nose. “He’ll look at me like, ‘What do you need me for?’”

The number of bedbug complaints dropped last year to 8,113, from 9,618 in 2016, but the dogs are still used for about 10 inspections a day. The pair recently hit a milestone, completing more than 25,000 inspections in the six years they have worked for the housing department.

“They’ve gotten better over time,” Mr. Trevino said. “They’ve been doing it for so long, it’s in their blood.”

We asked a housing department spokesman if Nemo (age 8) and Mickey (age 7), who have each worked on the force for 44 dog years, had any plans to retire.

No, the spokesman wrote in an email. “They are in the prime of their careers.”

And Finally...

The parks department is celebrating the vernal equinox by opening five parks and playgrounds today — one in each borough.

They are the most recent run-down green spaces that were improved through the mayor’s Community Parks Initiative. In all, 67 will be remodeled.

You may need to wait until this brew of snow and slush melts away to enjoy them, but here’s a rundown of the new digs and equipment.

The Bronx: At Lyons Square Playground, you can now frolic in a spray shower, or use the new basketball courts, picnic area, Ping-Pong table and fitness equipment.

Brooklyn: The Saratoga Ballfields in Brownsville took their name from the upstate town that was the site of a pivotal battle in the Revolutionary War. The playground has been renamed Hilltop Playground and now has basketball courts with polycarbonate backboards and hoops with actual nets.

Manhattan: During its face-lift, Martin Luther King Jr. Playground in Harlem got two full-size basketball courts, a synthetic turf area, a spray shower and an outdoor gym with shiny new fitness equipment.

Staten Island: The Arrochar Playground converted its asphalt field to turf and also installed a track, basketball courts and a new play area with a spray shower and water games.

Queens: Grassmere Playground in Far Rockaway has two new play areas, a junior soccer field, a junior basketball court and a wooded trail and outdoor classroom. Adults weren’t left out: They now have access to a 100-meter track and fitness equipment.
